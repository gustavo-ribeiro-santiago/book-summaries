### **Core idea:**

**We vastly overestimate what small samples can tell us — and this bias causes us to see patterns, find causes, and draw confident conclusions from data that is mostly noise.**

Put simply: **small samples produce extreme results by accident, but the mind treats those results as meaningful patterns and invents explanations for them.**

---

### **The central principle**

The counties with the *lowest* kidney cancer rates in the US are mostly small, rural, Republican-state counties. So are the counties with the *highest* rates. The explanation for both is the same: **small populations produce more variable statistics**. Random fluctuation looks like a pattern. The mind, designed for causal thinking, invents explanations — clean rural living for the low rates, poverty and poor healthcare for the high rates — that are entirely spurious.

---

### **The law of small numbers**

People intuitively believe that small samples should closely resemble the population they came from — that the "law of large numbers" applies to small numbers as well. This is false. Small samples are far more likely to produce extreme outcomes:

- A sample of 4 marbles drawn from an urn of 50% red / 50% white will show an all-red or all-white result ~12.5% of the time
- A sample of 7 marbles will show the same result only ~1.6% of the time

Research psychologists routinely chose samples too small to detect the effects they were studying, exposing themselves to a 50% chance of a false negative — and never noticed, because they trusted their intuition about what constitutes "enough" data.

---

### **Experts are not immune**

Kahneman and Tversky surveyed professional statisticians, including authors of textbooks, and found the same intuitive biases. Expert statisticians greatly overestimated the likelihood that a small-sample study would replicate a true finding. Knowledge of statistical theory does not automatically produce good statistical intuition.

---

### **The hot hand fallacy**

Basketball players, coaches, and fans believe in the "hot hand" — a player who has made several shots in a row is more likely to make the next one. Statistical analysis of thousands of shot sequences found no such effect. Shooting performance is essentially random around each player's baseline probability. The hot hand is a cognitive illusion: we see clusters in random sequences and immediately infer momentum and causality.

**The public reaction** — Red Auerbach of the Celtics dismissing the finding entirely — illustrates how powerful the illusion is. The pull of causal thinking overwhelms statistical evidence.

---

### **The Gates Foundation mistake**

The Foundation invested $1.7 billion in small schools after finding that the best-performing schools were disproportionately small. A statistician would have predicted this: small schools have higher variance, so they are overrepresented at *both* extremes. The worst-performing schools were also disproportionately small. Small schools are not better — they are simply more variable.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> We are wired for causal thinking and poorly equipped for statistical thinking — small samples produce extreme results by chance, but we interpret those results as patterns and invent causes to explain them, leading to confident but spurious conclusions.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Avoid being fooled by small samples**

Then:

- **Ask "How large is the sample?" before drawing any conclusion** — small samples produce extreme outcomes by chance.
- **Remember that both high and low extremes are overrepresented in small groups** — the best schools and the worst schools are both disproportionately small.

If you want to:

- **Be a better evaluator of people and results**

Then:

- **Wait for more data before concluding someone is a genius or a failure** — three successful deals, three great games, or three good quarters are not enough to distinguish skill from luck.

**Causal explanations of chance events are inevitably wrong. The mind that sees a pattern has created the pattern — not discovered it.**
