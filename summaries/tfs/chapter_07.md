### **Core idea:**

**System 1 jumps to conclusions from minimal evidence, suppresses doubt automatically, and creates a coherent story from whatever is available — even when the data is incomplete, biased, or actively misleading.**

Put simply: **the mind doesn't wait for enough information before forming a confident opinion — it builds the best possible story from whatever scraps it has, and calls that story "reality."**

---

### **The central principle**

System 1 is designed to construct coherent stories, not to evaluate evidence carefully. It has no mechanism for tracking what information is missing — it simply builds the best narrative from what is available. Kahneman calls this **WYSIATI: What You See Is All There Is.** The less information you have, the more coherent your story can be, and paradoxically, the more confident you feel.

---

### **Neglect of ambiguity**

System 1 resolves ambiguity instantly and without awareness. The same shape read as "B" in a sequence of letters is read as "13" in a sequence of numbers. Only one interpretation surfaces; the other never exists in your awareness. **Conscious doubt requires holding two incompatible possibilities simultaneously — that is System 2 work, and it doesn't happen automatically.**

---

### **The bias to believe**

Understanding a statement requires first representing it as true. Unbelieving is a second, effortful step that System 2 must perform. When System 2 is busy or depleted, the unbelieving step is skipped — people accept almost anything as true. This is why tired, distracted, or cognitively loaded people are more susceptible to persuasion.

---

### **The halo effect**

We like or dislike people globally based on first impressions, and that overall feeling colors every subsequent judgment about them. When Solomon Asch presented the same list of adjectives in different orders, people's impressions of the person described were dramatically different — the early traits defined the framework through which later traits were interpreted.

The halo effect produces *correlated errors*: grading a student's first essay highly predisposes you to grade the second one highly too, even when performance genuinely varies. To fight this, Kahneman graded all students' answers to question 1 before reading any answers to question 2 — *decorrelating errors* so that independent judgments can surface.

---

### **WYSIATI: What You See Is All There Is**

System 1 cannot represent information it doesn't have. It builds the most coherent story possible from available data, and the quality of that story — not its completeness — determines confidence. "Will Mindik be a good leader? She is intelligent and strong…" An immediate positive answer forms before you learn she is also corrupt and cruel.

WYSIATI explains:
- **Overconfidence**: confidence reflects story coherence, not evidence quality
- **Framing effects**: "90% survival rate" vs. "10% mortality rate" feel different
- **Base-rate neglect**: vivid individual descriptions crowd out statistical facts

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> System 1 jumps to confident conclusions from limited evidence by constructing the most coherent story possible from available data — the quality of the story, not the completeness of the evidence, determines how certain you feel.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Make better judgments with incomplete information**

Then:

- **Actively ask "What am I not seeing?"** — WYSIATI makes missing information invisible by design.
- **Decorrelate your sources of evidence** — in meetings, collect independent judgments before discussion to prevent halo contamination.

If you want to:

- **Resist the halo effect**

Then:

- **Evaluate attributes independently** — don't let a strong first impression color everything that follows.

**The coherence of a story is not evidence of its truth. A confident, compelling narrative built from few facts is often more dangerous than an uncertain one built from many.**
