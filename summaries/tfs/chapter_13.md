### **Core idea:**

**Our perception of risk is governed by emotion and availability, not by statistics — and this creates availability cascades in which minor events are amplified into public panics, while genuinely common killers go unnoticed.**

Put simply: **we fear the wrong things, at the wrong magnitudes, because our sense of danger tracks media coverage and emotional vividness rather than actual probability of harm.**

---

### **The central principle**

People's estimates of causes of death are strikingly distorted by availability. Slovic's landmark survey found:
- Strokes cause nearly *twice* as many deaths as all accidents combined — but 80% of respondents judged accidents more likely
- Tornadoes are seen as more deadly than asthma, which kills 20× more people
- Death by accident was judged 300× more likely than death by diabetes; the true ratio is 1:4

The pattern is consistent: **dramatic, memorable, media-covered deaths are overestimated; quiet, statistical deaths are underestimated.**

---

### **The affect heuristic at scale**

Slovic's insight: emotional reactions to risks are inseparable from assessments of their probability. Frightening images come to mind easily and vividly, which makes the dangers feel large — regardless of the statistics. When you learn that a technology's benefits are greater than you thought, you spontaneously reduce your estimate of its risks, even without new information about risks. **Likes and dislikes determine risk beliefs; they are not derived from them.**

---

### **Availability cascades**

Kuran and Sunstein describe how public risk panics form:
1. A minor event receives disproportionate media coverage
2. Public concern rises, which generates more coverage
3. "Availability entrepreneurs" amplify the fear for political or commercial purposes
4. Scientists who try to correct the record are dismissed as part of a cover-up
5. Policy responds to public fear rather than actual risk

**Case studies**: Love Canal (toxic waste panic), the Alar scare (apple chemical). In both cases, the scale of public response vastly exceeded the actual health risk. The Alar scare caused widespread reduction in apple consumption — likely increasing net harm to public health.

---

### **Probability neglect**

Sunstein's term for the pattern: people either ignore small risks entirely or give them enormous weight. There is no in-between. A parent waiting up for a teenager who is 30 minutes late *knows* intellectually that the risk is very small — but cannot stop imagining catastrophe. **Availability bypasses probability.** Thinking about the numerator (the horrible image) and ignoring the denominator (the actual frequency) is the default.

Terrorism is the extreme case: in most countries with terrorism, traffic deaths in a week exceed terrorist casualties in a year. But terrorism speaks directly to System 1 — vivid, threatening, available — while traffic fatalities are statistical background noise.

---

### **Experts vs. the public**

Slovic and Sunstein disagree on the implications:
- **Sunstein**: public fears are irrational and should be overridden by expert analysis of actual risk/benefit ratios
- **Slovic**: the public has a legitimate conception of risk that includes qualitative dimensions experts ignore (voluntary vs. involuntary risk, "good deaths" vs. "bad deaths"); experts should not rule unilaterally

Kahneman sides with both: availability cascades distort policy, but widespread fear — even irrational fear — is real suffering that deserves a policy response.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Risk perception is driven by the availability heuristic and the affect heuristic — making dramatic, media-amplified risks feel enormous and everyday statistical killers feel trivial — and availability cascades can convert minor events into public panics with large policy consequences.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Think clearly about risk**

Then:

- **Compare to base rates** — when a dramatic event scares you, look up how common it actually is relative to other risks you accept daily.
- **Recognize availability cascades** — when a story dominates the media, check whether the underlying facts justify the coverage.

If you want to:

- **Be a better risk communicator**

Then:

- **Never dismiss public fear as "irrational" without addressing it** — availability-driven fears are psychologically real even when statistically trivial.

**We protect against the risks we remember most vividly, not the ones that are most likely to harm us. Statistics save lives. Availability kills.**
