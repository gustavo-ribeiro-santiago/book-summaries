### **Core idea:**

**Mental effort is a real, measurable, physical phenomenon — and the mind operates by a law of least effort, automatically doing as little cognitive work as possible.**

Put simply: **attention is a limited budget, System 2 is lazy by design, and the mind constantly seeks the path of least mental resistance.**

---

### **The central principle**

Kahneman's research with pupil dilation showed that mental effort produces measurable physical responses: pupils dilate proportionally to cognitive load, heart rate increases, and muscles tense. The harder the task, the more the pupil dilates — and it contracts the instant effort stops. This makes effort visible and measurable, not just felt.

---

### **The economy of attention**

Attention is a limited resource. You cannot compute 17 × 24 while making a left turn in heavy traffic. System 2 has a capacity cap, and tasks that exceed it either degrade each other or one is dropped entirely.

The "invisible gorilla" study demonstrates the extreme: people who were intensely focused on counting basketball passes became literally blind to a person in a gorilla suit. **Intense focusing on one task can render obvious events invisible.**

---

### **The law of least effort**

If there are multiple ways to achieve a goal, people gravitate to the least mentally demanding option. This is not laziness in a moral sense — it is the brain's rational energy management. As skills become automatic through practice, they demand less System 2 effort and shift to System 1.

Talent works the same way: more intelligent individuals show smaller pupil dilations for the same problems — they literally use less brain energy.

---

### **Task switching is costly**

Switching from one cognitive task to another requires effort, especially under time pressure. The Add-3 task (mentally increment each digit in a 4-digit string by 3, at a timed pace) is near the absolute ceiling of what most people can sustain — because it requires simultaneously holding, transforming, and refreshing multiple items in working memory while maintaining a rhythm.

---

### **What only System 2 can do**

- Follow rules
- Compare objects on multiple attributes simultaneously
- Make deliberate choices between options
- Hold several distinct ideas in mind at once

System 1 can integrate information about a single entity but cannot handle multiple distinct topics simultaneously. Statistics — which requires thinking about many things at once — is naturally hostile to System 1.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Mental effort is physically real and strictly limited — the mind obeys a law of least effort, and System 2 is only recruited when System 1 cannot handle a task on its own.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Understand when your thinking is unreliable**

Then:

- **Recognize when System 2 is under load** — cognitive busyness makes you more impulsive, more superficial, and more likely to default to whatever comes to mind first.
- **Respect the capacity limit** — important decisions made when tired, distracted, or multitasking are decisions made mostly by System 1.

**The mind does not think harder just because the stakes are high. It thinks as little as it can get away with.**
