### **Core idea:**

**When we judge probability, we actually judge resemblance to a stereotype — and this representativeness heuristic causes us to ignore base rates, treat weak evidence as decisive, and systematically miscalculate the likelihood of outcomes.**

Put simply: **"He looks like a computer scientist" is not the same as "He is probably a computer scientist" — but the mind treats them as identical.**

---

### **The central principle**

The representativeness heuristic: when asked how probable something is, people instead answer how much it *resembles* the prototype of the relevant category. Tom W's personality description (orderly, self-centered, nerdy) fits the computer science stereotype closely — so people rank computer science as the most probable major, even though computer science is a small field and social sciences enroll far more students. **Representativeness swamps base rates.**

---

### **The Tom W experiment**

Graduate students in psychology — people trained in statistics — ranked Tom W's likely field of study by similarity to stereotype rather than by enrollment size. The correlation between "similarity" rankings and "probability" rankings was essentially perfect: they were substituting one judgment for the other without noticing.

The correct approach (Bayes): start with the base rate (enrollment size), then adjust for how diagnostic the description is. When evidence is of questionable quality (as it was for Tom W), stick close to the base rate.

---

### **The two sins of representativeness**

1. **Neglect of base rates**: a meek and tidy person is described, and everyone guesses "librarian" — even though there are 20 male farmers for every male librarian. The base rate is invisible when a vivid description is present.

2. **Insensitivity to evidence quality**: people are explicitly told the description may be unreliable, but they still use it as if it were accurate. System 1 processes available information as true; doubting the quality of evidence requires a deliberate System 2 effort that rarely occurs.

---

### **Moneyball and representativeness**

Baseball scouts traditionally evaluated prospects partly by body type and how they "looked the part." Billy Beane (Oakland A's) replaced this with statistical analysis of past performance, picking players whom other teams had rejected for not fitting the stereotype. The team dramatically outperformed its payroll. **Resemblance to a prototype is not a reliable predictor of performance.**

---

### **Bayesian correction**

The right protocol:
1. Anchor on the base rate probability
2. Ask how much the evidence should shift you from the base rate
3. Account for the reliability (diagnosticity) of the evidence

When evidence is weak or of uncertain quality, the adjustment from base rate should be small. When evidence is strong and reliable, larger adjustment is justified.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> The representativeness heuristic substitutes "how much does this resemble the category?" for "how probable is this?" — causing people to ignore base rates, overweight vivid descriptions, and produce predictions that are statistically wrong but intuitively compelling.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Make more accurate probability judgments**

Then:

- **Always start with the base rate** — how common is this outcome in the reference class?
- **Ask "How reliable is this evidence?"** before letting it override the base rate.

If you want to:

- **Avoid stereotype-driven decisions**

Then:

- **Separate "looks like X" from "is probably X"** — resemblance to a category is not the same as probability of belonging to it.

**The story a description tells is not evidence of probability. The most compelling description of a rare outcome does not make that outcome likely.**
