### **Core idea:**

**The overweighting of unlikely events is driven by attention and vividness, not just probability — and this produces a sharp split between two failure modes: rare events are either massively overweighted (when they are vivid and attended to) or completely neglected (when they are abstract and unattended).**

Put simply: **terrorism and lottery tickets are psychologically identical: both involve tiny probabilities amplified by vivid imagery into massive decision weights; but the same rare events can be completely ignored when they are not brought to mind.**

---

### **The central principle**

Prospect theory's original formulation said "highly unlikely events are either ignored or overweighted" but didn't explain which or when. The updated understanding: the overweighting of rare events is driven by the same mechanisms that produce availability bias — focused attention, vivid imagery, and cognitive ease — not just by probability.

---

### **Terrorism and the availability cascade**

Kahneman avoided stopping next to buses in Israel during a period of suicide bombings, even though he *knew* the risk was negligible. The risk from driving was actually higher. But proximity to a bus made him think of bombs, and those thoughts were unpleasant. **He was avoiding buses to avoid thinking about bombs — not to avoid harm.**

This is how terrorism works: it doesn't need to kill many people to be effective. Vivid imagery of death and destruction, reinforced by media and social conversation, creates massive decision weights far in excess of actual probability.

---

### **The two failure modes**

**Overweighting** (when the rare event is focused on):
- Lottery tickets: the possibility is kept mentally alive by advertising and social sharing of dreams
- Terrorism: kept alive by media, conversation, and visual memories
- Explicit descriptions ("1% chance of losing $1,000"): the event is made focal

**Neglect** (when the rare event is not focused on):
- Californians don't bother tying down water heaters for earthquakes, most of which they've never experienced
- Bankers in 2007 had no personal experience of a major financial crisis; the tail risk was statistically negligible to their System 1
- Long-term environmental threats: low media salience → low public concern

---

### **Denominator neglect**

Urn A: 10 marbles, 1 red (10% win chance)  
Urn B: 100 marbles, 8 red (8% win chance)  
30-40% of students prefer Urn B — the *worse* option — because they can vividly imagine 8 red marbles but cannot visualize 1 marble on a background of 9 white ones.

The same mechanism explains why:
- "1 death per 1,000" is scarier than "0.1% risk" (the image of a single person dying is more vivid than an abstract fraction)
- "1 of 100 psychiatric patients will commit violence" causes twice as many professionals to deny discharge vs. "10% probability of violence"
- Prosecutors prefer percentages; defense attorneys prefer frequencies — both exploit denominator neglect

---

### **Vividness overrides probability**

Rich, concrete descriptions of outcomes reduce sensitivity to probability. A gamble for "a dozen red roses in a glass vase" shows much less sensitivity to whether the probability is 21% or 84% than a gamble for "$59." The specific image exists vividly in mind regardless of its probability, leaving little room for the probability to vary the decision weight.

---

### **Choice from experience vs. description**

In experiments where subjects learn about probabilities by *experiencing* outcomes (button presses over many trials) rather than reading descriptions, rare events are *underweighted* rather than overweighted. The reason: many subjects simply never experience the rare event, so their System 1 doesn't register it. This matches the real-world pattern — people ignore the risks of long-tail events they haven't personally experienced.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Rare events are overweighted when they are vivid, concrete, and focal — through the mechanisms of attention, cognitive ease, and denominator neglect — and are neglected when they are abstract and personally unexperienced; there is no intermediate ground where they are accurately weighted.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Avoid being manipulated by vivid descriptions of rare events**

Then:

- **Translate frequency formats back to probability formats** — "1 in 1,000" sounds scary; "0.1%" is less scary; the same thing sounds very different.
- **Check: is this event focal because it's common, or because someone made it vivid?**

If you want to:

- **Protect against tail risks you've never experienced**

Then:

- **Actively make them concrete and vivid to yourself** — the same mechanism that causes overweighting of terrorism can be harnessed to overcome neglect of low-probability catastrophic risks.

**The mind either ignores rare events or is consumed by them. Calibrated probability judgments about rare events are a deliberate, effortful achievement — not the natural state.**
