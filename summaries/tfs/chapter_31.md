### **Core idea:**

**Evaluating each risky decision in isolation (narrow framing) is a systematic, costly error — the same loss aversion that makes individuals reject individually favorable gambles would make them accept aggregated packages that are obviously attractive.**

Put simply: **"you win a few, you lose a few" is not just a coping mechanism — it is a correct risk policy, and the failure to think this way costs investors and decision makers real money every year.**

---

### **The central principle**

Amos Kahneman constructed a pair of concurrent decisions that appeared straightforward in isolation but, when combined, revealed that the dominant choice was the one that only 3% of people selected. The structure:

- Decision (i): Sure gain of $240 vs. 25% chance to gain $1,000 → Most choose sure gain (risk averse)
- Decision (ii): Sure loss of $750 vs. 75% chance to lose $1,000 → Most choose the gamble (risk seeking)
- Combined: these choices produce "25% win $240, 75% lose $760"
- The *dominated* option "25% win $250, 75% lose $750" (strictly better in both outcomes) was the combination that 73% of people preferred when evaluated separately

**Narrow framing made people choose the worse option by a large margin.**

---

### **Narrow vs. broad framing**

- **Narrow framing**: evaluate each decision independently as it arises, driven by immediate emotional reactions
- **Broad framing**: aggregate multiple related decisions into a single comprehensive choice

Broad framing is always at least as good as narrow framing, and usually better. A rational agent will broad-frame. Humans naturally narrow-frame — because WYSIATI and limited mental effort both push toward evaluating problems as they arise.

---

### **Samuelson's problem**

Paul Samuelson's friend rejected a single 50/50 gamble (lose $100 or win $200) but was willing to take 100 such bets. Samuelson proved that under certain assumptions, a utility maximizer who rejects the single gamble should also reject the series. But Rabin and Thaler showed this conclusion is absurd: 100 such bets have an expected return of $5,000, a 1/2,300 chance of losing any money, and a 1/62,000 chance of losing more than $1,000. If utility theory is consistent with rejecting this package, something is wrong with utility theory.

**The resolution from prospect theory**: when 100 favorable gambles are bundled, the probability of a net loss shrinks dramatically. Loss aversion is less influential when there is little chance of losing. The reluctance to accept a single gamble is a by-product of loss aversion applied narrowly; the rational response is to aggregate and accept.

---

### **The mantra: "You win a few, you lose a few"**

Kahneman's advice to investors who reject small positive expected value gambles: treat every small decision as part of a bundle of similar decisions. The pain of the occasional loss is compensated by the pattern of gains over time. Conditions:
- The gambles are genuinely independent (not all correlated with the same factor)
- The possible loss does not materially threaten total wealth
- Not a long shot (where base rates of winning are very low)

---

### **How to reduce loss aversion in practice**

Experimental subjects who were instructed to "think like a trader" — to see each gamble as one of many in a portfolio — showed reduced physiological arousal (skin conductance) to losses and increased willingness to accept favorable risks.

**Individual investors**: checking portfolio value daily produces frequent painful small losses that outweigh equivalent gains in emotional terms, leading to excessive trading and risk aversion. Quarterly review dramatically improves the quality of decisions and the experience of investing.

---

### **Risk policies for organizations**

Thaler's story: 25 division heads of a large company each individually rejected a risky but positive expected value project. The CEO, looking at all 25 simultaneously, immediately said he wanted them all to accept — because statistical aggregation makes the organizational risk very small. **Each executive was narrow-framing; the CEO was naturally broad-framing across divisions.**

A *risk policy* — "we always take the highest deductible on insurance" or "we never buy extended warranties" — is a form of institutionalized broad framing. Following it reduces loss aversion one decision at a time while the aggregate policy remains rational.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Narrow framing — evaluating risky decisions in isolation — is driven by loss aversion and produces systematically inferior choices; broad framing, which aggregates similar decisions, reduces the emotional impact of individual losses and leads to better long-run outcomes.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Make better investment decisions**

Then:

- **Check your portfolio less frequently** — daily fluctuations are mostly noise, and the pain of frequent small losses exceeds the pleasure of equivalent gains.
- **Adopt explicit risk policies** rather than deciding each case separately.

If you want to:

- **Think about your decisions more rationally**

Then:

- **Ask "If I faced this type of decision 100 times, would I accept?"** — if yes, the emotional resistance to accepting once is loss aversion at work, not good judgment.

**"You win a few, you lose a few" is the mantra of the broad framer. It is not stoicism — it is mathematics.**
