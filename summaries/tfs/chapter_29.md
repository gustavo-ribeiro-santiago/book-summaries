### **Core idea:**

**People do not weight outcomes by their objective probabilities — they weight them by distorted decision weights that overweight small probabilities and underweight large ones, producing a specific and important four-fold pattern of risk attitudes across gains and losses.**

Put simply: **lotteries and insurance both make rational people irrational — but they do so through the same mechanism: the overweighting of small probabilities that creates hope in gains and fear in losses.**

---

### **The central principle**

Bernoulli's expected utility theory — and even the von Neumann–Morgenstern axiomatic version — assumed that decision weights equal probabilities: a 50% chance of winning $X is weighted exactly half as much as a certainty of winning $X. This is psychologically false. The actual decision weights people use are derived from probabilities but depart from them systematically:

- **Possibility effect**: low probabilities are overweighted (a 1% chance is treated as if it were 4%)
- **Certainty effect**: high probabilities are underweighted (a 99% chance is treated as if it were only 87.1%)
- **The two effects are asymmetric**: the certainty effect is larger than the possibility effect

---

### **Allais's paradox**

In 1952, Maurice Allais showed that even the world's leading decision theorists — Samuelson, Arrow, Friedman — preferred the safe option in problem B even when they had preferred the risky option in problem A. These two choices were mathematically inconsistent, yet both felt right. The reason: the certainty of winning $500,000 in problem B carries a disproportionate weight that overwhelms the slightly superior expected value of the risky option.

Allais intended this as a devastating blow to expected utility theory. Economists largely ignored it. Kahneman and Tversky took it seriously and built prospect theory to explain it.

### **The fourfold pattern**

| Domain | Probability | Emotion | Risk attitude | Legal example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Gains | High | Fear of disappointment | **Risk averse** | Plaintiff with strong case settles |
| Gains | Low | Hope | **Risk seeking** | Buys lottery ticket |
| Losses | High | Fear of large loss | **Risk seeking** | Defendant with weak case fights on |
| Losses | Low | Fear of loss | **Risk averse** | Buys insurance |

The bottom two cells reverse the standard pattern. Loss aversion alone would predict risk seeking everywhere in losses; but the overweighting of small probabilities (possibility effect) causes *caution* about low-probability disasters — which is why people buy insurance even at prices above expected value.

---

### **The top-right cell: the most dangerous**

Businesses facing near-certain large losses (market disruption, patent expiry, declining market share) gamble with remaining resources rather than accepting certain decline. Wars are prolonged by the losing side because defeat is too painful to accept and any sliver of hope looms large. **This cell is where manageable failures become disasters.**

The legal applications (Guthrie's analysis):
- Plaintiff with strong case (top-left cell): risk averse → accepts settlement below expected value
- Defendant with weak case (top-right cell): risk seeking → fights on despite unfavorable odds
- Frivolous plaintiff (bottom-left cell): risk seeking → sets high asking price, bold negotiating
- Defendant facing frivolous claim (bottom-right cell): risk averse → settles to avoid tiny probability of catastrophe

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> People weight outcomes by distorted decision weights that overweight small probabilities (possibility effect) and underweight near-certainties (certainty effect), producing a four-fold pattern of risk attitudes that explains lotteries, insurance, risky behavior under threat, and systematic inconsistencies in negotiation.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Make better decisions under risk**

Then:

- **Recognize which cell of the fourfold pattern you're in** — are you in a gain or loss domain, and is the probability high or low?
- **In the top-right cell (facing near-certain large loss)**: be especially vigilant — this is where the impulse to gamble rather than cut losses is strongest and most dangerous.

If you want to:

- **Build better risk policies**

Then:

- **Adopt consistent policies across similar decisions** rather than evaluating each case individually — consistent overweighting of small probabilities is costly in the long run.

**The fourfold pattern is one of the central achievements of behavioral economics: a simple 2×2 table that predicts most of the deviation from rational choice that experimental economists have spent 50 years documenting.**
