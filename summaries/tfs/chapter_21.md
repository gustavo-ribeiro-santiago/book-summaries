### **Core idea:**

**Simple formulas consistently outperform expert clinical judgment in prediction tasks — and the right response is not to defend intuition, but to use algorithms for the structured part of decisions and reserve intuition only for the things humans are genuinely better at.**

Put simply: **a formula made of six traits rated on a 1-5 scale will predict a person's job performance better than a 45-minute unstructured interview — and the research on this is not close.**

---

### **The central principle**

Paul Meehl's 1954 "disturbing little book" reviewed 20 studies comparing clinical (expert intuitive) predictions to statistical (formula-based) predictions. In every domain, the statistical formula either tied or beat the expert. Two hundred subsequent studies have replicated this finding across medicine, law, finance, psychology, and business. No exception has been convincingly documented.

---

### **Why formulas win**

1. **Humans try to be clever** — experts look for complex patterns, special circumstances, and unusual combinations. Simple formulas are boring but accurate. Complexity usually reduces validity.

2. **Humans are inconsistent** — when asked to evaluate the same case twice, experienced radiologists contradict themselves 20% of the time. Auditors and psychologists show similar inconsistency. A formula given the same inputs always returns the same answer.

3. **Context noise** — System 1's context-sensitivity means that unnoticed stimuli (a good mood from a pleasant lunch, a cool breeze) influence every judgment. Formulas are immune to this.

---

### **Equal-weighting schemes**

Robyn Dawes's key finding: you don't need to optimize the weights to beat human judgment. Giving equal weights to all predictors often performs as well as (or better than) the optimally weighted regression formula — because equal-weight formulas are less susceptible to overfitting the original sample.

Practical implication: a formula constructed on the back of an envelope — combining a few relevant factors with equal weights — is often good enough to outperform expert judgment.

---

### **The Apgar score**

Virginia Apgar's 1953 delivery-room formula — five variables (heart rate, respiration, reflexes, muscle tone, color), each scored 0-2, summed to a total — replaced inconsistent clinical judgment about newborn distress. It is still used in every delivery room. The formula is credited with significantly reducing infant mortality. **Simple standardized procedures save lives that inconsistent expert judgment loses.**

---

### **When to trust intuition (the compromise)**

Kahneman's Israeli Army interview system: six factual, behavioral traits scored separately on 1-5 scales, summed by formula. He also added a "close your eyes and imagine this person as a soldier" holistic intuition rating — which performed as well as the formula. The lesson: intuition adds value *after* a structured collection of objective information, not instead of it.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Simple statistical formulas consistently outperform expert clinical judgment in low-validity environments, because formulas are consistent and humans are not — but structured collection of specific, objective information followed by a formulaic combination can be supplemented by intuitive judgment.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Improve hiring decisions**

Then:

- **Design a structured interview**: select 6 independent traits, write factual behavioral questions for each, rate each trait on 1-5 before moving to the next (to prevent halo effects), sum the scores, hire the highest scorer.
- **Add intuition only at the end**, after the structured assessment, not before.

If you want to:

- **Make better predictions in your domain**

Then:

- **Ask "Is there a formula available?"** — if yes, the formula is almost certainly better than your judgment.

**"Whenever we can replace human judgment by a formula, we should at least consider it." — Meehl's principle, confirmed by 200 studies.**
