### **Core idea:**

**We maintain mental accounts that keep track of gains and losses in different categories, and we make decisions to close accounts with a gain — even when this is economically irrational, as in refusing to sell losing stocks and selling winning stocks too early.**

Put simply: **the pain of admitting a loss keeps people invested in bad stocks, bad jobs, and bad decisions long after rational analysis would dictate moving on.**

---

### **The central principle**

Richard Thaler's mental accounting theory: people maintain separate psychological accounts for different purposes (education savings, vacation fund, retirement), different sources of money (salary vs. windfall), and different ongoing transactions (each stock, each project). These accounts are not fungible in the way that standard economics assumes all money should be — "money is money."

---

### **The basketball ticket blizzard**

A fan who paid for a ticket is more likely to brave a blizzard than a fan who got a free ticket, even though both lose the same thing (seeing the game) if they stay home. The paid-ticket holder's mental account for "game attended" would close at a loss if he stayed home; the free-ticket holder's account closes at zero. The sunk cost drives behavior even though it is objectively irrelevant — the ticket price cannot be recovered either way.

---

### **The disposition effect**

Finance research finding: individual investors have a massive preference for selling winners (stocks that have gone up) over losers (stocks that have gone down), even though:
- In the US tax system, selling losers reduces tax liability; selling winners creates tax liability
- Stocks that have recently appreciated tend to continue rising; stocks that have recently declined tend to continue falling

The investor who sells winners and holds losers is paying twice — in taxes and in foregone returns — for the psychological pleasure of closing a mental account at a gain.

One year exception: in December, tax considerations override the disposition effect and investors sell more losers — "the tax advantage is available all year, but mental accounting prevails over financial common sense for 11 months."

---

### **Sunk cost fallacy**

A company that has spent $50M on a project will often spend another $60M to protect the first $50M — rather than redirect the $60M to a better investment — because admitting failure closes a large loss account. This is the sunk-cost fallacy: past investments that cannot be recovered should not influence current decisions.

The fallacy is especially persistent in organizations because it involves the reputation and career of specific individuals. A new CEO who inherits a failing project will cut it more readily than the CEO who launched it — not because she is smarter, but because she carries no mental account from the original decision.

---

### **Regret**

Regret is the painful emotion evoked by imagining that a different choice would have produced a better outcome. It is especially intense when:
- The outcome resulted from an *action* rather than inaction (departures from the default cause more regret than the default itself)
- The outcome was close to an alternative (a near-miss triggers more regret than a distant miss)
- The person acted out of character (Mr. Brown, who almost never picks up hitchhikers, feels more regret than Mr. Smith, who always does, when both are robbed)

**Anticipation of regret** shapes decisions: people avoid unconventional choices, stick with brand names, hold on to failing investments, and "clean up their portfolios" near year-end — all to minimize future regret.

---

### **Omission vs. commission**

People expect stronger emotional reactions (including regret) to bad outcomes from actions than from equivalent bad outcomes from inaction. This asymmetry biases toward conservative, conventional choices and makes people reluctant to deviate from the default. The bias appears in: stock selling, medical treatment choices, and consumer preferences for established brands.

---

### **Taboo tradeoffs**

Some mental accounts are psychologically protected from trading — especially when health and safety of loved ones are involved. Parents demand astronomically high compensation to accept even a tiny increase in risk of harm to a child, even when the same money would buy more safety elsewhere (e.g., a safer car seat). The precautionary principle in European law institutionalizes this: any action that might cause harm is prohibited, regardless of the costs of prohibition or the alternative uses of resources.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Mental accounts create reference points for individual transactions, and the desire to close accounts at a gain produces the disposition effect, sunk cost fallacy, and regret aversion — behavioral patterns that are emotionally understandable but economically costly.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Manage your investments more rationally**

Then:

- **Ask "Would I buy this stock at its current price?" not "Am I up or down?"** — the purchase price is a sunk cost; only the future matters.
- **In the US, sell losers and hold winners** — the tax arithmetic is unambiguous and the evidence on momentum favors it.

If you want to:

- **Make better organizational decisions**

Then:

- **Separate the person who decides to continue from the person who started the project** — sunk costs produce rationalization, not analysis.

**"Closing a mental account with a gain is a pleasure, but it is a pleasure you pay for." The pain of admitting loss is the most expensive feeling in economics.**
