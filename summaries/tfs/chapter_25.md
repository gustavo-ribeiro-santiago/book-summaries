### **Core idea:**

**Bernoulli's utility theory, which dominated economics for 300 years, is wrong in a specific and important way: it evaluates outcomes as final states of wealth rather than as gains and losses relative to a reference point — and this omission makes it unable to explain most of how people actually make decisions.**

Put simply: **Jack won $4 million yesterday and now has $5 million. Jill lost $4 million yesterday and now has $5 million. They have identical wealth, but Bernoulli's theory says they're equally happy. They're not — and this is a fundamental flaw in classical economics.**

---

### **The central principle**

Bernoulli (1738) proposed that people maximize expected utility (not expected value), and that utility is a logarithmic function of wealth — meaning each additional dollar of wealth provides less marginal utility than the one before. This insight explained risk aversion: a wealthy person cares less about an extra $100 than a poor person, so rational people prefer a certain moderate gain to a gamble with higher expected value.

The flaw: Bernoulli's model evaluates outcomes based on final states of wealth, not on *changes* from a reference point. History doesn't exist in his model.

---

### **Bernoulli's error demonstrated**

Jack had $1M yesterday and now has $5M: elated.  
Jill had $9M yesterday and now has $5M: despondent.

Same wealth → same utility in Bernoulli's theory → they should be equally happy. They are not. Their *reference points* — what they started from — determine their emotional state, not their absolute wealth.

Anthony has $1M. Betty has $4M. Both are offered: gamble (50% chance of $1M or $4M) vs. sure thing ($2M). Bernoulli predicts identical choices (same options in terms of final wealth). But Anthony sees a chance to double vs. gain nothing; Betty sees a certain loss vs. maybe losing nothing. They have opposite incentives and will make opposite choices. Bernoulli's theory cannot predict this.

---

### **The missing variable: reference point**

The reference point is the baseline against which gains and losses are measured. It is usually the status quo, but can also be an expectation, an entitlement, or what others receive. *Below* the reference point, outcomes are losses. *Above* it, they are gains.

Bernoulli's theory has no reference point — it is a model without memory. This is "theory-induced blindness": scholars accepted the theory for 270 years and looked away from obvious counterexamples, because the theory was elegant and useful in other ways.

---

### **Three principles of prospect theory**

1. **Evaluation relative to a reference point**: outcomes are gains or losses from a reference point, not final states of wealth
2. **Diminishing sensitivity**: a psychological S-curve — changes from the reference point matter more when they are small; large changes in either direction produce diminishing marginal response
3. **Loss aversion**: losses loom larger than equivalent gains — the slope of the value function is steeper below the reference point than above it

The value function is S-shaped: steeper for losses than for gains, and both sides showing diminishing marginal sensitivity as you move further from the reference point.

---

### **Loss aversion quantified**

The typical loss aversion ratio is 1.5 to 2.5: the subjective pain of losing $100 is approximately equivalent to the subjective pleasure of gaining $150-250. Most people reject a 50/50 gamble with potential losses and equivalent potential gains unless the potential gain is approximately twice the potential loss.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Bernoulli's utility theory fails because it ignores reference points — the same objective outcome is experienced differently depending on where one started from; prospect theory corrects this by evaluating outcomes as gains and losses relative to a reference point, with losses weighing roughly twice as much as equivalent gains.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Understand why people make seemingly irrational financial decisions**

Then:

- **Ask "What is their reference point?"** — the same $500 salary increase feels like a windfall to someone who expected nothing and an insult to someone who expected $1,000.
- **Loss aversion explains risk-seeking in losses**: when all options are bad, people prefer the gamble over the certain loss — the reverse of their behavior with gains.

If you want to:

- **Frame proposals more effectively**

Then:

- **Frame benefits as avoiding losses rather than achieving gains** — loss aversion makes "you'll save $X" more compelling than "you'll gain $X".

**Humans evaluate changes from reference points, not final states. Econs evaluate final states. This difference explains most of behavioral economics.**
