### **Core idea:**

**Self-control and deliberate thinking draw on the same limited mental resource — and when that resource is depleted, System 1 takes over, making people more impulsive, more selfish, and worse at reasoning.**

Put simply: **willpower and thinking are fueled by the same tank — when one runs low, so does the other.**

---

### **The central principle**

Physical and cognitive effort compete for the same mental budget. Walking faster makes it harder to think clearly. Holding digits in memory makes you more likely to choose chocolate cake over fruit salad. The mind obeys a single law of least effort across both domains, and self-control is just another form of mental work.

---

### **Ego depletion**

Baumeister's research showed that exerting willpower in one task impairs willpower in subsequent tasks. This is called *ego depletion*. After suppressing an emotional reaction to a film, people give up sooner in a physical stamina test. After resisting tempting food, they perform worse on logic problems.

Tasks that cause depletion share one feature: **they require suppressing a natural tendency**. The list includes:
- Avoiding a thought
- Responding kindly to provocation
- Making a series of conflicting choices
- Interacting with someone from a different group (for prejudiced individuals)

---

### **Glucose is the fuel for System 2**

The most striking finding: ego depletion has a metabolic basis. Difficult cognitive and self-control tasks lower blood glucose. Restoring glucose — even with a sugary drink — reverses depletion effects. The "Israeli parole judge" study is a stark illustration: judges approved ~65% of parole requests right after a meal, dropping steadily to near 0% just before the next meal. Tired and hungry judges defaulted to the safer "deny" option.

---

### **The bat-and-ball problem**

A classic demonstration of lazy System 2:

> A bat and ball cost $1.10. The bat costs one dollar more than the ball. How much does the ball cost?

The intuitive answer — 10¢ — is wrong (the correct answer is 5¢). More than 50% of Harvard, MIT, and Princeton students answered incorrectly. They accepted the first plausible answer without checking it. This reveals a general truth: **System 2 can check System 1's work, but often doesn't bother.**

---

### **Intelligence ≠ rationality**

Keith Stanovich distinguishes between two components of System 2:
- *Algorithmic mind*: raw processing power (measured by IQ)
- *Reflective mind*: willingness to question intuitions and engage in deliberate reasoning (rationality)

High IQ does not guarantee rationality. People with high cognitive ability still fall for the bat-and-ball problem if their reflective mind is lazy. Rationality and intelligence are related but separable traits.

---

### **The Marshmallow Test**

Four-year-olds who could delay gratification (wait 15 minutes for two cookies instead of taking one immediately) showed better executive control, higher intelligence, and lower drug use as young adults. Self-control at age 4 predicts outcomes better than IQ. Attention control can also be trained — children who practiced attention regulation in computer games showed gains in both executive control and general intelligence scores.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Self-control and deliberate thinking draw on a shared, limited pool of mental energy — depleting one weakens the other, and a tired or cognitively busy System 2 cedes control to the impulsive, default-seeking System 1.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Make better decisions under pressure**

Then:

- **Protect your cognitive resources** — don't make high-stakes decisions when tired, hungry, or after a long period of self-control.
- **Recognize depletion signals** — impulsiveness, irritability, and a pull toward default options are signs that System 2 is running low.

If you want to:

- **Avoid the "lazy System 2" trap**

Then:

- **Slow down** when the stakes are high and an answer comes suspiciously quickly.
- **Ask "did I actually check this?"** before acting on an intuition that feels obviously right.

**Thinking is expensive. The mind rations it ruthlessly — and the bill comes due in the quality of our choices.**
