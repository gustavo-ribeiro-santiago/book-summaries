### **Core idea:**

**We systematically predict based on the inside view — our specific plan and our current situation — while ignoring the outside view: what typically happens to similar projects in the reference class. The result is the planning fallacy: massively overoptimistic plans built on best-case scenarios.**

Put simply: **our curriculum project would take 2 years according to our plan; similar projects took 7–10 years and 40% failed entirely — and we ignored this when it was presented to us and pressed on anyway.**

---

### **The central principle**

Kahneman's curriculum team confidently predicted they'd finish their textbook in 2 years. Their own curriculum expert, when asked about the track record of *similar* projects, revealed that none had finished in under 7 years and 40% failed entirely. The team acknowledged this information, felt uncomfortable, and... carried on anyway. The book was finished 8 years later and never used.

---

### **Inside view vs. outside view**

- **Inside view**: focus on the specific circumstances of this project — what we've done so far, what the remaining tasks are, how things have been going
- **Outside view**: identify a reference class of similar past projects and examine their outcomes

The inside view is the default. It feels natural, personal, and relevant. The outside view feels abstract, impersonal, and somehow irrelevant ("every case is unique"). But the outside view is usually more accurate.

---

### **The planning fallacy**

Plans that are:
- Unrealistically close to best-case scenarios
- Based almost entirely on inside-view projections
- Improvable by consulting the statistics of similar cases

Real-world examples:
- Scottish Parliament building: estimated £40M, delivered at £431M
- Rail projects worldwide (1969–1998): passenger use overestimated by 106% on average, cost overrun 45%
- US kitchen renovations: expected $18,658, actual $38,769

The pattern is universal and remarkably consistent. Experts don't improve with experience — after thirty years of documented overruns in rail projects, the overestimates didn't decrease.

---

### **Why inside-view forecasts fail**

1. **Unknown unknowns**: divorces, illnesses, bureaucratic delays, coordination failures — the cascade of things that cannot be foreseen but that always happen in complex projects
2. **Optimistic extrapolation**: early phases go well (people are most committed, easiest tasks done first); this produces optimistic estimates for the whole project
3. **WYSIATI**: focusing on the plan and ignoring both what we don't know and what the reference class shows

---

### **Reference class forecasting (the cure)**

Bent Flyvbjerg's framework:
1. Identify the reference class (kitchen renovations, railway projects, textbook writing)
2. Get the statistics of that reference class (distribution of outcomes, typical overruns)
3. Use the statistics to generate a baseline prediction
4. Adjust for specific features of this case (upward or downward from baseline)

This is the outside view operationalized as a systematic procedure.

---

### **Irrational perseverance**

The team knew the outside view was bad — 40% chance of failure, 7–10 years minimum. They didn't quit because of the sunk cost fallacy (already invested a year), social embarrassment (Kahneman had proposed the project), and the fact that there was no immediate crisis. **Changing course in the absence of a crisis requires deliberate rationality that most teams cannot muster.**

---

### **The premortem**

Gary Klein's technique: before finalizing a decision, conduct a brief session where participants imagine that one year has passed, the project failed, and they must explain what happened. This:
- Suppresses groupthink
- Legitimizes the voicing of concerns
- Forces specific scenarios of failure to be imagined

One of the most practical tools available for reducing optimism bias in groups.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> The planning fallacy arises from defaulting to the inside view — extrapolating from current plans while ignoring what the reference class of similar projects shows — and the remedy is systematic application of the outside view through reference class forecasting.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Make more realistic project plans**

Then:

- **Ask "What is the reference class?"** — kitchen renovations, software projects, curriculum development, marketing campaigns — and look up their typical outcomes.
- **Use the outside-view statistics as your baseline**, then adjust for specific features of your case.

If you want to:

- **Reduce overconfidence in your team**

Then:

- **Run a premortem** before committing: "It's a year from now and we failed. What happened?"

**"There are many ways for any plan to fail, and although most are too improbable to be individually anticipated, the likelihood that something will go wrong in a big project is high." — The planning fallacy summarized.**
