### **Core idea:**

**Intuitive predictions match the extremeness of evidence to the extremeness of outcome — producing predictions that are too extreme and systematically biased — because System 1 ignores regression and treats weak evidence as fully diagnostic.**

Put simply: **we predict grades from early reading fluency, predict company success from a CEO's first impression, and predict career outcomes from a single interview — all forms of the same error: treating imperfect evidence as if it perfectly predicted outcomes.**

---

### **The central principle**

When asked to predict Julie's college GPA from knowing she read fluently at age 4, people naturally match intensity: early reading puts her at the 95th percentile of precocity → assign her the 95th percentile GPA. But early reading and college GPA share perhaps 30% of their determinants — which means 70% of what determines her GPA is completely unrelated to her reading age. The correct prediction should regress heavily toward the average.

**Intuitive predictions are as extreme as the evidence — they do not account for the imperfect correlation between evidence and outcome.**

---

### **Substitution in prediction**

When asked to predict a future performance, people evaluate the present evidence and then *translate* that evaluation directly onto the outcome scale — without distinguishing between "What does this evidence tell me?" and "What will actually happen?" These two questions should get different answers, but they get the same one.

Israeli Army officers asked to predict training grades gave distributions of predictions that perfectly mirrored their own evaluation distributions — they had simply relabeled their current assessments as future predictions, applying intensity matching with no adjustment for uncertainty.

---

### **The correct procedure**

Kahneman's four-step correction:

1. Start with the baseline (average outcome for the reference class)
2. Find the outcome that matches your intuitive assessment of the evidence
3. Estimate the correlation between your evidence and the outcome
4. Move only that fraction of the distance from baseline toward your intuitive prediction

If the correlation is .30, move 30% of the way. If it is .60, move 60%. Most intuitive predictions implicitly assume a correlation of 1.0 — meaning perfect prediction — which is almost never justified.

---

### **When extreme predictions are defensible**

A venture capitalist choosing between many potential investments *needs* to find the rare extraordinary one. For her, the cost of missing a "next Google" is much greater than the cost of modest over-investment in a promising failure. In asymmetric error-cost situations, deliberately allowing some upward bias in extreme predictions may be rational — but she should remain aware that she is choosing to be biased.

---

### **The Kim vs. Jane problem**

A department can hire Kim (brilliant interview, minimal track record) or Jane (strong track record, less sparkling interview). Kim made a stronger *impression*, but the evidence for Jane is more extensive and therefore more reliable. Because Kim's evidence comes from a smaller "sample," it should be regressed more toward the mean — partially neutralizing the strong impression. A rational analysis may favor Jane even when intuition favors Kim.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Intuitive predictions match evidence extremeness to outcome extremeness, ignoring that imperfect correlations require regression toward the mean — producing systematically overconfident, overly extreme predictions that underperform unbiased baselines.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Make better predictions**

Then:

- **Anchor on the base rate** — what is the average outcome for cases like this, with no additional information?
- **Estimate the correlation between your evidence and the outcome** — if it is modest (below .5), your intuitive prediction is probably too extreme.
- **Regress toward the mean** — deliberately moderate your prediction toward the baseline.

If you want to:

- **Evaluate candidates or investments more accurately**

Then:

- **Prefer more evidence over stronger evidence** — a longer track record should outweigh a single dazzling impression.

**Your intuition will predict too extreme, too confident, and too often. Moderation is not timidity — it is accuracy.**
