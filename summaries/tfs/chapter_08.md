### **Core idea:**

**System 1 constantly performs a battery of automatic assessments — about threat, trustworthiness, causality, and emotional intensity — and these basic judgments feed into complex decisions, often replacing the harder question with an easier one.**

Put simply: **you are always evaluating much more than you intend to — and these automatic assessments quietly shape every judgment and decision you make.**

---

### **The central principle**

System 1 continuously monitors the environment and generates automatic assessments of situations, people, and objects — even when no explicit judgment is requested. These assessments include evaluations of threat level, emotional charge, similarity, causality, and moral intensity. They are the raw material from which all intuitive judgments are constructed.

---

### **Basic assessments**

System 1 has been shaped by evolution to answer survival-relevant questions continuously:
- Is there a threat?
- Is this person friend or foe?
- Is this situation normal?
- Should I approach or avoid?

Alex Todorov's research showed that people form impressions of a politician's competence from a fraction-of-a-second exposure to their face — and these impressions predict electoral outcomes with ~70% accuracy. Voters are not deliberately assessing governing ability; they are reporting System 1's automatic dominance-and-trustworthiness scan.

---

### **Sets vs. sums**

System 1 represents categories by *prototypes*, not totals. People asked how much they'd pay to save 2,000, 20,000, or 200,000 birds from oil spills gave nearly identical amounts (~$80–88). They were responding to the emotional image of a suffering bird — a prototype — not calculating the size of the category. **System 1 handles averages but ignores sums and quantities.**

---

### **Intensity matching**

System 1 can translate intensity across completely different dimensions. "If Sam were as tall as he is intelligent, how tall would he be?" is answered quickly and with surprising agreement across people. Crimes can be matched to sound volumes; punishments can be matched to severity of offense. This cross-scale matching is automatic and produces a "sense of justice" — injustice feels like a mismatch in loudness between crime and punishment.

---

### **The mental shotgun**

When System 2 assigns a task, System 1 automatically computes much more than requested. Asked if two words rhyme, people are also — without intending to — checking spelling. Asked if a sentence is literally true, they also evaluate whether it is metaphorically true. This overfiring of computations (the "mental shotgun") feeds additional, unrequested answers into the decision process and is the engine behind heuristic substitution.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> System 1 performs a constant stream of basic assessments — of threat, emotion, similarity, causality, and intensity — that are automatically translated across dimensions and fed into every judgment, often replacing the question that was actually asked.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Understand why first impressions are so sticky**

Then:

- **Recognize that face-reading, threat-detection, and likeability assessments happen in milliseconds** and shape all subsequent reasoning.
- **The question "Do I trust this person?" is answered by System 1 before you consciously decide to evaluate them.**

If you want to:

- **Avoid quantity neglect and prototype substitution**

Then:

- **Force quantitative reasoning explicitly** — when evaluating the importance of a risk or benefit, ask for numbers, not just impressions.

**You evaluate far more than you intend — and the evaluations you didn't ask for are often the ones that drive the decision.**
