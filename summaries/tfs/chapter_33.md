### **Core idea:**

**How you evaluate an option depends critically on what you compare it to — and because single evaluation (considering one thing at a time) and joint evaluation (comparing two things simultaneously) surface different features as salient, they produce systematically different — and often inconsistent — preferences.**

Put simply: **your preference between dolphins and farmworkers, between bet A and bet B, or between two crime victims reverses depending on whether you see one or both at the same time — and there is no "true" preference underneath, only context-dependent reactions.**

---

### **The central principle**

Most preferences are not stable, coherent, pre-existing states that evaluation reveals. They are *constructed* in the moment, based on whichever features are salient in the current comparison context. Single evaluation and joint evaluation consistently produce different preferences because they make different features salient.

---

### **The Lichtenstein-Slovic preference reversal**

Two bets:
- Bet A: 11/36 chance to win $160, 25/36 chance to lose $15
- Bet B: 35/36 chance to win $40, 1/36 chance to lose $10

In *choice* (joint evaluation): most people choose B (safer bet)  
In *pricing* (single evaluation): most people set a higher selling price for A (the big potential prize anchors the value)

This is a preference reversal: people prefer B over A but price A above B. The inconsistency is driven by what is most salient in each frame. In choice, the risk difference is obvious; in pricing, the maximum prize is the anchor.

Economists Grether and Plott set out to discredit this finding. After testing all 13 alternative explanations, they concluded that the psychologists were right. The finding — that preferences are context-dependent — violated a core assumption of economic theory, yet the discipline mostly moved on without changing that assumption.

### **Dolphins vs. farmworkers**

In single evaluation: a request to donate to dolphin conservation evokes high emotion (dolphins are charming, endangered species are salient); people donate generously. A request to donate to skin cancer screening for farmworkers evokes moderate concern.

In joint evaluation: the comparison makes immediately salient that farmworkers are human and dolphins are not. The preference reverses dramatically — people donate substantially more to farmworkers.

The "human vs. animal" distinction was always factually available; it simply wasn't *relevant* in single evaluation because dolphins were only being compared to other dolphin-like causes, not to human causes.

---

### **The burned child and the defrauded bank**

In single evaluation: jurors awarded higher punitive damages to a defrauded bank ($10M loss) than to a burned child (moderate burns), because the financial loss provided an anchor.

In joint evaluation: the human victim's plight became more emotionally salient, and awards to the burned child more than doubled. The comparison made the previous award to the child look inadequate relative to a financial institution.

### **The evaluability principle (Hsee)**

Dictionary A: 10,000 entries, like new  
Dictionary B: 20,000 entries, cover torn, otherwise like new  

In single evaluation: A is valued more (condition is easily evaluated; number of entries is not — who knows if 10,000 is a lot?)  
In joint evaluation: B is valued more (20,000 > 10,000 is immediately obvious and clearly important)

The number of entries is a *non-evaluable* attribute in single evaluation — there is no scale on which to assess how good 10,000 is — but becomes highly evaluable in joint evaluation.

### **Incoherence in the law**

Penalties for violations of occupational safety regulations are capped at $7,000. Violations of the Wild Bird Conservation Act can result in fines up to $25,000. These are sensible within each agency's context but absurd when compared globally. The legal system systematically produces this incoherence by evaluating cases one at a time within categories, never across categories.

Jurors assessing punitive damages are explicitly prohibited from considering other cases — the law mandates single evaluation, which is psychologically less reliable than joint evaluation.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Preferences between options depend on whether they are evaluated jointly or separately, because comparison makes different features salient — single evaluation is dominated by the emotional response to each option's most accessible attribute, while joint evaluation enables comparison on more deliberate dimensions.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Make better decisions between options**

Then:

- **Seek joint evaluation** — whenever you can, compare options directly rather than evaluating each in isolation.
- **Be suspicious of single-evaluation pricing decisions** — the anchor you're using may be irrelevant to the actual value.

If you want to:

- **Design fairer institutional processes**

Then:

- **Force global comparisons** — Sunstein's finding that administrative penalties vary by 3-4x across agencies is a result of never comparing them; joint evaluation would quickly produce more coherent standards.

**"When you see cases in isolation, you are likely to be guided by an emotional reaction of System 1." Joint evaluation always produces more stable, more deliberate judgments.**
