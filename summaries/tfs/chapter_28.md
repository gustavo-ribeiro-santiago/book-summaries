### **Core idea:**

**Losses loom larger than equivalent gains across every domain of life — from negotiation and goal-setting to labor economics, institutional reform, and professional golf — because the brain is wired to give priority to bad news over good news.**

Put simply: **negativity dominance is not a quirk of economic preferences — it is a biological fact that shapes everything from threatened species detection to why reforms fail to why Tiger Woods putts better for par than for birdie.**

---

### **The central principle**

Loss aversion is one expression of a broader *negativity dominance* — the brain's tendency to treat threats as more urgent than opportunities. Threatening stimuli activate the amygdala faster than pleasant ones; an angry face pops out of a crowd of happy faces while a happy face does not pop out of an angry crowd. Bad words (war, crime) attract attention faster than good words (peace, love). Paul Rozin's formulation: a single cockroach ruins a bowl of cherries, but a cherry does nothing for a bowl of cockroaches.

---

### **Goals as reference points**

Loss aversion applies to goals, not just wealth. Not achieving a goal is a *loss*; exceeding a goal is merely a forgone gain. The aversion to failure is much stronger than the desire to excel.

**Golf study (Pope and Schweitzer)**: Analysis of 2.5 million putts showed that professional golfers were 3.6% more successful when putting for par (to avoid a bogey = loss) than when putting for birdie (a gain). If Tiger Woods had putted as well for birdies as for par in his best years, he would have earned ~$1M more per season. The asymmetry is real and costly — and it arises unconsciously.

**New York cabdrivers**: Drivers with a daily earnings target work longer hours on slow days (fewer fares available) and go home early on busy days (target reached quickly). This is the *opposite* of economically rational behavior (work more when rewards per hour are higher), but it is perfectly predicted by loss aversion around a daily reference point.

---

### **Why reforms fail**

Every attempt at institutional reform creates winners and losers. The losers fight harder than the winners support the reform. The political outcome is inevitably biased toward protecting the losers — through grandfather clauses, transition protections, and dilution of the proposed change. This is loss aversion at the institutional level. **Loss aversion is a powerful conservative force that favors the status quo.**

---

### **Fairness is reference-dependent**

Kahneman, Thaler, and Knetsch's surveys of economic fairness showed that:
- Raising snow shovel prices after a blizzard: 82% rated it unfair (the pre-storm price is the reference point; higher price feels like a loss imposed on customers)
- Reducing an employee's wage when unemployment rises: 83% rated it unfair
- Hiring a replacement at a lower wage: 73% rated it acceptable

The moral rule: it is unfair to impose losses on others relative to their reference point, unless the firm is protecting its own entitlement (its own profits). The distinction is between imposing new losses and failing to share windfalls.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Loss aversion is one expression of a universal negativity dominance — threats are processed faster and more powerfully than equivalent opportunities — and this asymmetry shapes goals, negotiations, fairness norms, institutional behavior, and biological responses across species.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Understand why negotiations stall**

Then:

- **Recognize that both sides feel like they are giving up more than the other side appreciates** — it is an inherent asymmetry, not dishonesty or stubbornness.

If you want to:

- **Drive behavior with goals**

Then:

- **Frame goals as avoiding losses** rather than achieving gains — loss aversion around a reference point creates more sustained effort than prospect of a reward.

**"Losses loom larger than gains" is not a theoretical claim. It is a biological fact backed by evolutionary history, brain anatomy, and 2.5 million golf putts.**
