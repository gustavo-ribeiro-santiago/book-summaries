### **Core idea:**

**Prospect theory formalizes the three psychological features that govern how people evaluate gains and losses: reference dependence, diminishing sensitivity, and loss aversion — and the resulting S-shaped value function predicts a specific and important pattern: risk aversion for gains and risk seeking for losses.**

Put simply: **people prefer a sure $900 to a 90% chance at $1,000 (risk averse), and prefer a 90% chance of losing $1,000 to a sure $900 loss (risk seeking) — the same asymmetry repeated everywhere in economic life.**

---

### **The central principle**

Prospect theory's value function has three defining features:
1. It is defined relative to a *reference point* (not absolute wealth)
2. It is *concave for gains* (diminishing marginal sensitivity — each additional gain matters less)
3. It is *steeper for losses than gains* (loss aversion — the same dollar amount hurts more as a loss than it feels good as a gain)

The graph (figure 10) of the value function: an S-shape asymmetric about the reference point. The loss side is steeper than the gain side.

---

### **Risk aversion for gains, risk seeking for losses**

Problem 1 (gains): Get $900 for sure vs. 90% chance of $1,000  
→ Most people take the sure thing (risk averse)

Problem 2 (losses): Lose $900 for sure vs. 90% chance of losing $1,000  
→ Most people take the gamble (risk seeking)

These are mirror images. In gains, the concave curve makes a certain smaller amount feel nearly as good as a gamble with higher expected value. In losses, the same concavity makes a certain large loss feel much worse than a slightly larger probable loss.

**This is the four-fold pattern of risk attitudes**:
- Gains at high probability → risk averse
- Gains at low probability → risk seeking (lottery tickets)
- Losses at high probability → risk seeking
- Losses at low probability → risk averse (insurance)

---

### **Problems 3 and 4: the knockout blow to Bernoulli**

Problem 3: Given $1,000, choose between: 50% chance of $1,000 MORE, or $500 sure.  
Problem 4: Given $2,000, choose between: 50% chance of losing $1,000, or losing $500 sure.

In terms of *final wealth*, both problems offer identical choices: sure $1,500 or 50/50 between $1,000 and $2,000. Bernoulli predicts identical choices. But in Problem 3 people are risk averse (sure gain); in Problem 4 people are risk seeking (gamble to avoid the sure loss). The framing — gains vs. losses — changes the choice entirely. Bernoulli's theory cannot explain this.

---

### **Loss aversion in everyday life**

The loss aversion coefficient (~2:1) appears across many domains:
- Labor economics: the minimum acceptable wage for unemployed workers averages 90% of their previous salary
- Housing markets: sellers in down markets set higher prices when their reference point (purchase price) is higher
- Supermarket pricing: price increases reduce demand about twice as much as equal price decreases increase it
- Negotiations: concessions hurt; deadlock is common because both sides feel they are giving up more than they are receiving

---

### **Prospect theory's own blind spots**

Kahneman acknowledges two failures:
1. **Disappointment**: winning nothing after a 90% chance of $1M is experienced as a large loss — but prospect theory assigns it the same value (zero) as winning nothing after a 1% chance
2. **Regret**: the pain of a bad outcome is affected by what the alternative would have been; prospect theory treats options as evaluated independently

These are real failures. Prospect theory is more accurate than utility theory but still incomplete.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Prospect theory's S-shaped value function — reference-dependent, concave for gains, and steeper for losses — predicts the four-fold pattern of risk attitudes and explains the central finding that losses loom approximately twice as large as equivalent gains in human decision making.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Predict how people will respond to risky choices**

Then:

- **Identify the reference point**: is this a gain domain or loss domain problem?
- **In gains**: expect risk aversion — people will prefer the certain option
- **In losses**: expect risk seeking — people facing sure losses will gamble to avoid them

If you want to:

- **Understand negotiations and conflict**

Then:

- **Recognize that both sides feel they are in the loss domain** — every concession is a loss, every gain from the other side feels smaller; this asymmetry creates systematic conflict and deadlock.

**Loss aversion ratio of ~2: the most consequential number in behavioral economics.**
