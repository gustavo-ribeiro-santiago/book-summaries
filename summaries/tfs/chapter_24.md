### **Core idea:**

**Optimism is the single most consequential cognitive bias in business and entrepreneurship — it fuels innovation and persistence, but it also causes entrepreneurs, executives, and planners to systematically underestimate risk, overestimate their ability, and ignore competition.**

Put simply: **the optimistic bias is the engine of capitalism — and also its greatest source of self-inflicted financial damage.**

---

### **The central principle**

Optimism is not random variance — it is a systematic, heritable trait that manifests in predictable patterns: entrepreneurs overestimate their odds of success, CFOs overestimate their ability to forecast the market, CEOs overpay for acquisitions, and planners consistently produce best-case projections. The bias is pervasive and largely unkillable by experience or training.

---

### **Entrepreneurial delusions**

- The 5-year survival rate for US small businesses is approximately 35%
- Entrepreneurs' average estimate of success odds "for any business like yours": 60% (nearly double the true value)
- 81% of entrepreneurs put their personal odds of success at 7 out of 10 or higher
- 33% said their chance of failure was zero

Yet this optimism has value: optimists persist, recruit talent, maintain morale, and attract funding. The bias that leads many to fail also drives the few extraordinary successes that create economic progress. "Optimistic martyrs" — firms that fail but signal viable markets to better-equipped competitors — may be economically net positive.

---

### **Competition neglect**

WYSIATI in action: entrepreneurs focus on what they know (their own capabilities, plan, and opportunities) and fail to imagine that competitors are making the same plans. When asked "How much will your outcome depend on what you do?", founders consistently answer 80%+ — ignoring that market outcomes are determined as much by competitors and external forces as by internal efforts.

The Disney executive who asked why studios release multiple expensive films on the same holiday weekend answered his own question: "Hubris. Hubris. If you only think about your own business..."

---

### **Above-average effects and overconfidence**

- 90% of drivers believe they are better than average
- CFOs' 80% confidence intervals about S&P returns were wrong 67% of the time (they should have been wrong only 20%)
- The correctly calibrated confidence interval for S&P returns would be –10% to +30%: a range no professional would publicly state

Social pressure reinforces overconfidence: admitting uncertainty is read as incompetence. An honest CFO who says "I have no idea where the market is headed" gets mocked. Overconfident CFOs get promotions and media coverage.

---

### **CEO overconfidence and acquisitions**

Malmendier and Tate: CEOs who hold unusually large personal stakes in their company (a proxy for optimism about the company's future) are more likely to overpay for acquisitions and pursue value-destroying mergers. Remarkably, the stock market *can identify* these overconfident CEOs — the acquiring firm's stock drops more on announcement when the CEO is in the overconfident group. Prestigious press awards to CEOs correlate with subsequent underperformance and more time spent on celebrity activities.

---

### **The premortem as partial remedy**

Training people to reduce overconfidence has largely failed. The one promising technique is the **premortem**: imagining that the project has already failed and writing a history of the disaster. This legitimizes dissent, unlocks specific concerns that individuals had suppressed, and temporarily overrides the optimism that consensus and momentum produce.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Optimism is the most consequential cognitive bias in business — it fuels entrepreneurship and economic dynamism while producing systematic underestimation of risks, costs, and competition; it is largely unkillable but partially manageable through deliberate outside-view thinking and adversarial exercises.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Counteract optimism bias in planning**

Then:

- **Apply the outside view**: identify what the reference class of similar ventures looks like and use that as your baseline.
- **Run a premortem**: explicitly imagine failure before committing to the plan.

If you want to:

- **Harness optimism's benefits while limiting its costs**

Then:

- **Let optimism drive action, but let statistical analysis drive estimates** — optimists should execute; pessimists (or reference class forecasting) should set the odds.

**"Bold forecasts and timid decisions" — the optimistic bias in a phrase: we plan as if success is likely but often fail to commit resources that match the stated ambition.**
