### **Core idea:**

**We judge the frequency and likelihood of events by how easily examples come to mind — but ease of retrieval is driven by salience, recency, and emotional charge, not by actual frequency, creating systematic distortions in how we assess risk and importance.**

Put simply: **what is easy to remember feels common; what is hard to recall feels rare — regardless of the actual statistics.**

---

### **The central principle**

The availability heuristic: when estimating the frequency or probability of something, people judge by the ease with which examples come to mind. This shortcut is usually reasonable — common things do tend to come to mind more easily — but it fails systematically when ease of recall is driven by factors other than frequency: media coverage, emotional vividness, personal experience, and recency.

---

### **What drives availability (and isn't frequency)**

- **Salience**: celebrity divorces and political sex scandals get more media coverage than their actual frequency warrants → people overestimate their prevalence
- **Vividness**: a plane crash seen on TV is more available than the statistically more common car accident → flying feels more dangerous than driving
- **Personal experience**: a judicial error that affects *you* shakes your faith in the legal system more than reading about an equivalent error in a newspaper
- **Recency**: insurance purchases spike after earthquakes and disasters, then decline as memories fade

---

### **The paradox of fluency and quantity**

Schwarz's key experiment: people who listed **12** examples of their own assertive behavior rated themselves as *less* assertive than people who listed **6** examples. Listing 12 was harder — fluency was lower — and low fluency felt like evidence of having few examples to find.

The number retrieved matters less than the ease of retrieval. This produces counterintuitive results:
- Listing more ways a car is good → liking the car *less*
- Listing more ways to improve a course → rating the course *higher*
- Listing more arguments for a choice → feeling *less* confident in it

**The experience of fluency is the actual input to the judgment — not the content that was retrieved.**

---

### **When availability is overridden**

People with more at stake (personal involvement) engage System 2 more and base their judgments on content rather than fluency. Students with a family history of heart disease evaluated cardiac risks based on what they actually recalled, not how easily it came. High personal relevance shifts the process from System 1 (fluency-based) to System 2 (content-based).

People in good moods, powerful people, and those who trust their intuition are *more* susceptible to availability biases.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> The availability heuristic substitutes ease of recall for actual frequency — making salient, vivid, recent, and emotionally charged events feel more common than they are, and quiet, statistical, or unfamiliar risks feel negligible.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Correct for availability bias in risk assessment**

Then:

- **Look up the statistics** — media coverage is a terrible guide to actual frequency of risk.
- **Ask "Is this memorable because it's common, or because it's dramatic?"** — the two are very different things.

If you want to:

- **Apply this to everyday self-assessment**

Then:

- **Know that both you and your partner overestimate your own contribution** to shared tasks — your own actions are more available to you than your partner's.
- **When you struggle to come up with examples of something, don't conclude the thing is rare** — you may just be hitting a retrieval limit.

**The world in your head is not a precise replica of reality. It is a world shaped by what is easiest to remember.**
