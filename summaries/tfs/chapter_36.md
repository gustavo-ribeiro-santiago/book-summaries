### **Core idea:**

**We value experiences as stories — with peaks, turning points, and endings — not as time-weighted sequences of moments; and this narrative evaluation governs how we judge entire lives, vacations, and the lives of others, making duration almost irrelevant to the worth we assign an episode.**

Put simply: **adding 5 "slightly happy" years to a very happy life makes the whole life seem worse — because we are averaging, not summing, and the disappointing additional years dilute the prototype of a good life.**

---

### **The central principle**

Kahneman watched La Traviata and noticed he cared intensely whether the lover would arrive before Violetta died — 10 minutes that would change the character of the whole story — but felt nothing at all upon learning she had died a year younger than he thought. **A story is about significant events and memorable moments, not about time passing.** Duration neglect is normal in narrative mode.

---

### **The "Jen" experiment**

Ed Diener asked participants to evaluate the desirability of a fictitious woman's life (Jen), who lived either a very happy life of 30 or 60 years, or the same life with an additional 5 "pleasant but less happy" years added.

Results:
- Doubling the duration of a happy life (30 to 60 years): **no effect** on judgments of life desirability or total happiness
- Adding 5 slightly less happy years to a very happy life: **significantly reduced** judgments of total happiness

This is the *less-is-more* effect driven by prototype thinking. The remembering self evaluates a life by a representative slice, not by the integral. Adding mildly positive years dilutes the average of what the life "was like," making it seem worse despite adding genuinely pleasant experience.

---

### **Duration neglect in life evaluation**

The same logic that makes colonoscopy evaluations insensitive to duration makes life evaluations insensitive to duration. A person is represented by a prototypical period, not a sequence of time-slices. This leads to paradoxes:
- Long happy lives are not valued more than short happy lives
- Adding good-but-not-great years can reduce the overall evaluation of a life
- What matters is: how the person was "typically," and how things ended

---

### **Vacations and memory-driven choice**

Students kept daily diaries of their spring break and provided a global evaluation at the end. The global evaluation — not the average of daily experience — determined whether they intended to repeat the vacation. **People choose by memory, not by experience.** The remembering self governs future decisions even when it doesn't accurately represent the quality of the experience.

The "amnesic vacation" thought experiment: if all photos and memories were erased at the end of the trip, most people would drastically reduce how much they'd pay for it — or not bother going at all. This reveals that many people are not really pursuing experience; they are constructing a memory.

---

### **We are our remembering selves**

"I find it helpful to think of myself as a stranger in the experiencing self's house." The experiencing self lives through moments; the remembering self keeps score and plans. They are not the same person. We identify with the remembering self — the narrator who tells the story of our lives — even though the experiencing self is the one who suffers or enjoys each moment.

Implications:
- **Touristic photography**: the photographer is designing a future memory, not experiencing the present
- **"Memorable" experiences**: the word reveals the goal — the experience is valued as a future memory
- **Decisions about repeated experiences**: governed entirely by the quality of the memory, not the experience

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> The remembering self evaluates experiences as stories — governed by peaks, turning points, and endings, with duration largely ignored — and this narrative mode of evaluation determines how we judge entire lives, plan vacations, and make choices about repeating experiences.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Get more out of experiences**

Then:

- **Be present**: the experiencing self only enjoys what it attends to. Frenetic picture-taking designs a memory while sacrificing the experience.
- **Notice that "memorable" is not the same as "enjoyable"** — what makes a memory good is different from what makes an experience good.

If you want to:

- **Design experiences for others** (events, services, treatments)

Then:

- **End well**: the end of an experience has disproportionate weight in memory.
- **Create a peak**: one memorable high point influences the evaluation of the whole.

**"A story is about significant events and memorable moments, not about time passing." The remembering self lives in stories; the experiencing self lives in time.**
