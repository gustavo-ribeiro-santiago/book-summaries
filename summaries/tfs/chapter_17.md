### **Core idea:**

**Regression to the mean is one of the most important and most systematically misunderstood facts of statistics — whenever performance fluctuates around a baseline, extreme results tend to be followed by more moderate ones, and we invent causal explanations for what is simply mathematical inevitability.**

Put simply: **praise is followed by worse performance and punishment by better performance — not because feedback works or doesn't, but because exceptional performance is partly luck, and luck doesn't persist.**

---

### **The central principle**

Flight instructors noticed that praising a good maneuver was followed by worse performance, while yelling at a cadet for a bad maneuver was followed by improvement. They concluded that punishment works and reward doesn't. They were completely wrong. The instructor praised only exceptionally good performance (which included a large luck component) and yelled only after exceptionally bad performance (also luck-driven). Both were destined to regress toward the average regardless of any feedback. The causal story the instructor told — "reward hurts, punishment helps" — was entirely spurious.

---

### **What regression to the mean actually is**

Whenever two variables are imperfectly correlated (which is always), extreme values on one are accompanied by less extreme values on the other. The success formula:

> success = talent + luck  
> great success = a little more talent + a lot of luck

A golfer who scores 66 (6 under par) on day 1 probably had an above-average talent *and* above-average luck. On day 2, the talent persists but the luck averages out — so their expected score is higher (worse) than day 1. This is not a psychological tendency; it is a mathematical certainty.

---

### **Ubiquitous regression we fail to see**

- **Sports Illustrated jinx**: athletes on the cover performed exceptionally to get there (partly due to luck); they predictably regress afterward. The "jinx" is spurious.
- **"Sophomore slump"**: outstanding performance in year 1 → average regression in year 2.
- **Medical treatments for depression**: depressed patients are extreme (very low mood); they would improve over time with any treatment, or with no treatment. Control groups are essential to separate regression from treatment effect.
- **Management of star employees**: a standout year is followed by a more moderate year — not because success breeds complacency, but because the extreme year contained a luck component.

---

### **Why the mind doesn't see it**

Regression to the mean was not discovered until the 19th century — *after* calculus and the theory of gravitation. The difficulty is conceptual: regression has no cause. It is a mathematical consequence of imperfect correlation. The mind demands a causal story, and when it detects a regression pattern, it immediately invents one. The sports commentator who says "He was nervous protecting his lead and deteriorated" has noticed regression and provided a causal story for which there is no evidence.

---

### **Practical correction**

For any prediction where the correlation between predictor and outcome is imperfect (which is always), the correct prediction regresses toward the mean:

1. Start at the baseline (average outcome for the reference class)
2. Find the score that matches your intuition
3. Estimate the correlation between evidence and outcome
4. Move that fraction of the distance from baseline to your intuitive prediction

A correlation of .30 means moving only 30% of the way from the average toward your intuitive prediction.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Regression to the mean is a mathematical consequence of imperfect correlation between any two measures — extreme results contain a luck component that does not persist, making exceptional performance followed by deterioration the rule, not the exception.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Avoid perverse feedback loops**

Then:

- **Recognize that behavior after extreme outcomes is not caused by your feedback** — it was going to regress regardless. Don't conclude that punishment works and reward hurts.

If you want to:

- **Make better predictions**

Then:

- **Always regress your intuitive prediction toward the mean** — the more extreme your evidence, the more luck it contains, and the more you should expect regression.

**"She improved after we criticized her" and "He deteriorated after we praised him" are not lessons about feedback. They are lessons about luck and regression. Causal explanations of regression are inevitably wrong.**
