### **Core idea:**

**Anchoring is one of the most powerful and reliable biases in all of psychology — any number you are exposed to before making an estimate will pull your estimate toward it, even if the number is obviously random and irrelevant.**

Put simply: **you cannot ignore a number once you've seen it — it plants itself in your thinking and biases every estimate you make from that point forward.**

---

### **The central principle**

When Kahneman and Tversky rigged a wheel of fortune to land on either 10 or 65, and then asked subjects what percentage of UN member nations are African, the average answer was 25% for the group that saw 10 and 45% for the group that saw 65. The wheel of fortune is obviously irrelevant to African nation membership — yet it shifted estimates by 20 percentage points. This is the anchoring effect: any number considered before an estimate pulls the estimate toward it.

---

### **Two mechanisms of anchoring**

**Anchoring as adjustment (System 2):** You start from an anchor you know is wrong and deliberately adjust toward the right answer — but you stop too soon, at the near edge of your uncertainty zone. This explains why people who are told to shake their head (a "no" gesture) while hearing an anchor adjust further from it than people who nod.

**Anchoring as priming (System 1):** The anchor activates a set of associated ideas that make information consistent with the anchor more accessible. A high anchor for annual temperature in Germany primes "summer" words; a low anchor primes "winter" words. The estimate then draws on a biased sample of retrieved information. System 1 tries to construct a world in which the anchor is the true number.

---

### **The anchoring index**

Anchoring effects are not just real — they are large and measurable. In one study, a high anchor of 1,200 feet for the height of the tallest redwood produced an average estimate of 844 feet; a low anchor of 180 feet produced an average of 282 feet. The anchoring index (the ratio of the shift to the range of anchors) was 55% — meaning anchors explain more than half the variance in estimates.

Real-estate agents assessing a house were anchored by the listing price to almost the same degree as business school students with no experience. The agents insisted the listing price had no effect. They were wrong.

---

### **Random anchors are just as powerful**

The last two digits of a participant's Social Security number serve as an effective anchor for estimates of the number of physicians in their city. German judges who rolled dice (loaded to show 3 or 9) sentenced a shoplifter to an average of 5 months (dice = 3) vs. 8 months (dice = 9) — a 50% anchoring effect. **There is no such thing as an irrelevant number once it has entered your mind.**

---

### **Anchoring in real life**

- Negotiators who set the first number (the asking price) gain a structural advantage
- Charitable donation amounts are highly anchored to the suggested amount shown
- "Limit of 12 per person" signs at supermarkets cause customers to buy twice as many cans as "no limit" signs
- Award caps in litigation can paradoxically *increase* small awards by acting as upward anchors

**Defense against anchoring:** Deliberately generate arguments *against* the anchor. Focus on the opponent's minimum acceptable offer, not their stated demand. System 2 effort can partially override System 1 anchoring, but never eliminates it.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Any number encountered before an estimate — even an obviously random and irrelevant one — anchors the estimate by priming compatible information and biasing adjustment, producing large and reliable distortions in judgment.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Protect yourself from anchoring**

Then:

- **Assume any number you've seen has anchored you** — especially in negotiations, real estate, salaries, and legal damages.
- **Actively think of reasons the anchor is wrong** — "thinking the opposite" partially counteracts the bias.
- **If possible, make the first move** in negotiations rather than letting the other side set the anchor.

If you want to:

- **Use anchoring deliberately**

Then:

- **Set a high initial number** in any negotiation or sales context — it pulls the final outcome upward.

**Every number on the table has already shaped your thinking. The question is whether you know it.**
