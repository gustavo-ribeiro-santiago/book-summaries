### **Core idea:**

**When faced with a hard question, the mind automatically substitutes an easier one and answers that instead — without noticing the swap — producing intuitive judgments that feel like answers to the original question but often aren't.**

Put simply: **you are rarely stumped because you are often not actually answering the question you were asked.**

---

### **The central principle**

Kahneman's mechanism of *substitution*: the mind faces a difficult target question, finds a related heuristic question that comes to mind more easily, answers the heuristic question, and then maps that answer (via intensity matching) onto the target question's scale. The whole process is automatic and invisible — System 2 accepts the result without realizing the question was changed.

---

### **The substitution table**

| Target question (hard) | Heuristic question (easy) |
|---|---|
| How much would I give to save endangered dolphins? | How much emotion do I feel about dying dolphins? |
| How happy am I with my life? | What is my mood right now? |
| How popular is the president? | How popular will he be in six months? |
| How should financial predators be punished? | How angry am I about financial predators? |
| How far will this candidate go in politics? | Does this candidate look like a winner? |

---

### **Intensity matching: the translation mechanism**

Substitution works because different scales of intensity can be matched across dimensions. The emotional intensity of concern about dolphins can be translated into a dollar amount via intensity matching. The perceived political potential of a candidate is expressed as a position on a scale from "loses the primary" to "president someday." The result *feels* like it answers the original question — because it is the right *type* of answer — even though it was derived from a different source.

---

### **The 3-D heuristic**

A figure on the right side of a perspective corridor drawing appears larger than the identical figure on the left. You know both figures are the same size on the page, yet the illusion is irresistible: the question "How big is the figure on the page?" is automatically replaced by "How tall is the person in 3D space?" — and the 3D answer dominates.

---

### **The mood heuristic for happiness**

When German students were asked how many dates they'd had last month *before* being asked how happy they were, the correlation between dating and reported happiness was very high. When asked in the reverse order, the correlation was near zero. Thinking about dating put a specific emotional state in mind, and that emotion was mistaken for general life satisfaction. **What is currently in your mind looms very large in self-assessments of well-being.**

---

### **The affect heuristic**

Paul Slovic showed that people's overall emotional attitude toward a technology, product, or policy determines their beliefs about both its risks and benefits simultaneously. If you increase someone's sense of a technology's benefits, they spontaneously rate its risks as lower — even without new information about risks. Likes and dislikes determine beliefs, not the other way around. **The emotional tail wags the rational dog.**

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> When faced with hard questions, the mind automatically substitutes easier ones and maps the answers back — the affect heuristic, mood heuristic, and intensity matching all operate this way, producing confident intuitive judgments that often answer a different question than the one that was asked.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Make more accurate judgments**

Then:

- **Ask "Which question am I actually answering?"** — especially in evaluations, interviews, and assessments of risk.
- **Slow down when an answer comes immediately and confidently** to a question that should be hard.

If you want to:

- **Understand the affect heuristic in practice**

Then:

- **Recognize that liking/disliking something changes your factual beliefs about it** — if you love a politician, you believe their policies are more effective; if you dislike an industry, you believe its risks are higher.

**The mind is never stumped for long — because it quietly changes the question.**
