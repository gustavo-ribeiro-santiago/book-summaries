### **Core idea:**

**The way a choice is framed — the words used, the default option, the reference point established — can change the decision dramatically without changing the objective reality, because System 1 responds to words and associations, not to facts.**

Put simply: **"90% survival rate" and "10% mortality rate" describe the same reality but produce different choices in physicians and patients alike — because losses (deaths) evoke stronger reactions than equivalent gains (survivors), and no amount of medical training eliminates this.**

---

### **The central principle**

Framing effects are the influence of the *form* of a description — rather than its *content* — on beliefs and preferences. Two descriptions that are logically equivalent (same truth conditions, same states of the world) produce different emotional reactions and different choices. This is possible because the meaning of a description is not just its truth conditions but what it activates in the associative machinery: "Italy won" and "France lost" are logically identical but psychologically different.

---

### **Costs vs. losses**

A $5 loss is psychologically much worse than a $5 cost. The credit-card industry exploited this: they preferred that any price differential be labeled a "cash discount" rather than a "credit surcharge" — because discounts are forgone gains, while surcharges are losses. Loss aversion makes the second frame emotionally stronger even though both frames represent the same $5 difference.

---

### **The survival/mortality framing of surgery**

When physicians were told "the one-month survival rate is 90%," 84% chose surgery. When told "there is 10% mortality in the first month," 50% chose surgery. The objective probability is identical; the frame changed the choice. Crucially, physicians were just as susceptible to the framing effect as hospital patients and business school students — medical training was no protection.

---

### **KEEP vs. LOSE**

In a brain-imaging study, the same sure outcome was labeled "KEEP £20" or "LOSE £30" from an initial endowment of £50 (identical real outcomes). Subjects were more likely to choose the sure option when labeled KEEP and the gamble when labeled LOSE. The amygdala activated preferentially on trials where the choice conformed to the frame. The anterior cingulate — associated with conflict — activated when subjects chose *against* the emotional pull of the frame.

---

### **The Asian disease problem**

Two programs for a disease expected to kill 600 people:
- Gain frame: Program A saves 200 people sure; Program B one-third chance to save all, two-thirds chance to save none → Most choose A (risk averse in gains)
- Loss frame: Program A' 400 die sure; Program B' one-third chance nobody dies, two-thirds chance 600 die → Most choose B' (risk seeking in losses)

The same programs, framed as gains or losses, produce opposite choices. Public health professionals were equally susceptible. When confronted with their inconsistency, subjects typically fell silent — System 2 had no independent moral intuition to resolve the contradiction. **The moral feelings are attached to the frames, not to the underlying reality.**

---

### **The Schelling tax problem**

Standard framing: should the child exemption be larger for the rich than the poor? → Rejected as unfair.  
Reframed: should childless poor pay the same surcharge as childless rich? → Also rejected.  
But these are logically equivalent — you cannot reject both without contradiction.

The lesson: moral intuitions are reference-dependent. There is no underlying principled moral view — only reactions to frames. "Our moral intuitions are about descriptions, not about substance."

---

### **Default options and organ donation**

Austria (opt-out): ~100% donation rate  
Germany (opt-in): ~12% donation rate  

The only difference is which option requires checking a box. The decision about organ donation is not thoughtfully made by most people — they simply follow the default. The default is a framing effect, and it is the single best predictor of donation rates across countries. This is not trivial: the choice of default option is a policy decision that determines whether organs are available to save lives.

---

### **The MPG illusion**

Miles-per-gallon is the wrong frame for fuel economy decisions. Improving from 12 to 14 mpg saves 119 gallons per 10,000 miles. Improving from 30 to 40 mpg saves only 83 gallons. But the mpg frame makes the second improvement look larger. Gallons-per-mile (or liters-per-100km) is the correct frame for calculating actual savings. The standard US framing has misdirected policy and consumer choice for decades.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Framing effects arise because System 1 responds to the words and associations of a description rather than to the objective reality it represents — logically equivalent frames produce different emotions and different choices, and neither medical training, intelligence, nor experience reliably eliminates this.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Resist framing effects in your own decisions**

Then:

- **Reframe**: translate the description into equivalent alternative frames and check whether your preference changes.
- **Be especially suspicious when you react strongly to a gain/loss label** — ask what the same situation looks like stated the other way.

If you want to:

- **Use framing to design better choices (or policies)**

Then:

- **Set defaults deliberately**: the default option will be chosen by the majority who don't actively override it.
- **Use the correct frame for the decision**: gallons-per-mile, not miles-per-gallon.

**Our preferences are frame-bound, not reality-bound. Understanding this is not pessimism — it is the necessary first step toward better individual and policy decisions.**
