### **Core idea:**

**Experienced well-being (how you actually feel, moment to moment) and life satisfaction (how you evaluate your life when asked) are systematically different — and only one of them is reliably improved by income above a threshold of ~$75,000 per year.**

Put simply: **money buys life satisfaction but not happiness: beyond a comfortable income, people evaluate their lives as better but don't actually experience more positive moments in their daily lives.**

---

### **The central principle**

Kahneman's team developed the Day Reconstruction Method (DRM) to measure the well-being of the experiencing self: participants relive the previous day in detail, breaking it into episodes, and rate the feelings associated with each. This approximates a "hedonimeter" reading of experienced well-being. It is distinct from global life satisfaction, which is a remembering-self judgment.

---

### **The U-index**

The percentage of time a person spends in an unpleasant emotional state (where a negative feeling exceeds all positive feelings). Key findings for American women:

- Morning commute: 29% of time unpleasant
- Work: 27%
- Child care: 24%
- Housework: 18%
- Socializing: 12%
- TV watching: 12%
- Sex: 5%

**The big surprise**: time with children is *slightly less enjoyable* than housework for American women — but significantly more enjoyable for French women, who spend less time on child care and less of their time driving children to activities.

The U-index is highest on weekdays vs. weekends by ~6%, because of work stress and reduced time in enjoyable activities.

---

### **What determines moment-to-moment well-being**

Mood at work is largely *not* driven by benefits, status, or job satisfaction. It is determined by situational factors:
- Opportunities to socialize with colleagues
- Noise exposure
- Time pressure (significant source of negative affect)
- Whether a boss is present (in the first study: worse than being alone)

**Attention is key**: emotional state is largely determined by what you're attending to. People who are in love feel joy even in traffic; people who are grieving feel sad during a funny movie. In normal circumstances, mood tracks the immediate situation.

Frenchwomen rate eating as twice as focal as American women (who often eat while doing other things), which is why the French enjoy eating more despite spending similar time on it.

---

### **Can money buy happiness?**

Gallup data from 450,000+ Americans:
- **Being poor makes people miserable**: poverty amplifies the emotional impact of illness, divorce, and loneliness
- **A headache** increases sadness and worry from 19% to 38% for the top two-thirds of earners — and from 38% to 70% for the poorest tenth
- **Beyond ~$75,000** (in high-cost areas): additional income produces zero increase in experienced well-being
- **But life satisfaction** continues to increase with income well beyond the satiation point

Higher income buys *vacations in interesting places and opera tickets* — but high income is also associated with reduced ability to enjoy small pleasures. Students primed with wealth imagery showed less facial pleasure while eating chocolate.

---

### **Two aspects of well-being**

| Dimension | Driven by |
|---|---|
| Experienced well-being (daily positive feelings) | Social contact, health, activities, immediate situation |
| Life satisfaction (global evaluation) | Income, achievement, goals, comparisons |

These are related but genuinely different. Education raises life satisfaction but not experienced well-being; it is associated with *higher stress*. Religious participation improves positive affect and stress reduction but not life evaluation. Ill health impairs experienced well-being much more than life evaluation.

**"It is only a slight exaggeration to say that happiness is the experience of spending time with people you love and who love you."**

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Experienced well-being (how you feel in daily life) and life satisfaction (how you evaluate your life) are distinct — the former is determined mainly by social contact, health, and attention; the latter by income, goals, and comparisons; only the former is what it feels like to be alive.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Increase your actual happiness**

Then:

- **Control your use of time** — commuting less, socializing more, reducing time in activities you actively dislike
- **Prioritize social contact** with people you enjoy — it is the most reliable predictor of positive daily affect
- **Be present during pleasant activities** — you only enjoy what you attend to

If you want to:

- **Think clearly about money and happiness**

Then:

- **Up to ~$75,000**: more income reliably reduces misery and improves daily experience
- **Beyond that**: additional income improves how you evaluate your life but not how you experience it day to day

**The most useful investment in happiness is not purchasing goods — it is purchasing time in the right activities with the right people.**
