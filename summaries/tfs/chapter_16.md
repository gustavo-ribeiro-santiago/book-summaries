### **Core idea:**

**Statistical base rates are ignored when a vivid individual description is present — but base rates that tell a causal story are readily used. Teaching people statistics changes what they can recite; it rarely changes how they think about individual cases.**

Put simply: **the mind ignores "merely statistical" facts but embraces base rates the moment they feel causal — and this is almost impossible to fix by teaching statistics alone.**

---

### **The central principle**

Two versions of the cab problem are mathematically identical but psychologically opposite:

- **Version 1**: "85% of cabs in the city are Green, 15% are Blue." → Most people ignore this statistical base rate when a witness says "Blue."
- **Version 2**: "Green cabs are involved in 85% of accidents." → People immediately use this base rate, because it implies a *causal story* (Green drivers are reckless).

The base rate is the same number, but one is a statistical frequency and one is a causal fact about individuals. System 1 processes causal stories; it is blind to mere statistics.

---

### **Two types of base rates**

- **Statistical base rates**: facts about a population. Used as background context but easily overridden by case-specific information.
- **Causal base rates**: facts that imply something about how individual cases came to be. Easily integrated with case-specific information and treated as relevant to the individual.

Stereotypes are the prime example of causal base rates. They create expectations about individual members that feel like facts about the person, not just about the group.

---

### **Teaching psychology doesn't change thinking**

Nisbett and Borgida's experiment: students learned that only 4 of 15 participants in a famous "helping experiment" rushed to help a stranger having a seizure — a shocking base rate. They could recite this fact. But when asked to predict whether a specific, nice-seeming person they saw on video would help, they predicted yes — ignoring the statistical reality they had just learned.

When students were instead shown *two specific individuals* who had failed to help, they immediately generalized: "Helping must be much harder than I thought." **Subjects were unwilling to deduce the particular from the general, but immediately inferred the general from the particular.**

The lesson: statistics provide base-rate information that people generally ignore. Surprising individual cases are far more effective for changing beliefs — because they create a new causal story that must be integrated.

---

### **Implications for teaching and persuasion**

Statistical facts change what people know but not how they think. To change how people think about a class of situations, you need to show them *individual surprising cases* — specific people behaving unexpectedly — that create cognitive dissonance and force a new causal explanation. **Stories change minds. Statistics fill footnotes.**

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Statistical base rates are treated as background context and routinely ignored when individual case information is available; causal base rates are integrated as facts about individuals. Surprising individual cases change beliefs; surprising statistics do not.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Get people to use base rates**

Then:

- **Make the base rate causal**, not just statistical: "This type of investment fails 90% of the time because founders consistently underestimate market resistance" works better than "90% of investments like this fail."

If you want to:

- **Actually learn from statistics**

Then:

- **Ask "What individual case would change my mind?"** — if no individual case would, you're probably not actually letting the statistics influence your thinking.

**We are masters at reasoning from individual cases and beginners at reasoning from statistics. Design for how people actually think, not how you wish they would.**
