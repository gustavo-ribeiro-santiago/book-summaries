### **Core idea:**

**A more specific, detailed description feels more probable than a less specific one — even though adding details can only reduce probability — because coherence and plausibility are mistaken for likelihood.**

Put simply: **"Linda is a bank teller who is also a feminist" feels more probable than "Linda is a bank teller," even though it is necessarily less probable — this is the conjunction fallacy, and it is extremely robust.**

---

### **The central principle**

Linda is described as a politically engaged, justice-oriented philosophy graduate. Participants are asked: is it more likely that she is (A) a bank teller, or (B) a bank teller who is active in the feminist movement? 85–89% of respondents, including statistically trained doctoral students, choose (B) — violating the basic logic of probability. Every feminist bank teller *is* a bank teller, so (A) must be at least as probable as (B).

The mind confuses *plausibility* with *probability*. The more detailed, coherent, story-like option feels truer — even when logic demands it be less likely.

---

### **Why the fallacy is so robust**

- It survives transparent comparison (both options shown side by side)
- It survives statistical training (85% of doctoral students in decision science made the error)
- It is driven by System 1's automatic coherence-seeking — the feminist bank teller *fits* the description of Linda; the plain bank teller does not

Stephen Jay Gould, who knew the correct answer, wrote that a "little homunculus in my head keeps jumping up and down shouting — 'but she can't just be a bank teller!'" This is System 1 speaking over System 2's formal knowledge.

---

### **The plausibility trap in forecasting**

Adding detail to a scenario makes it more narrative, more compelling, and more plausible — but necessarily less probable. *"A massive flood in North America next year"* is more probable than *"An earthquake in California causing a massive flood in North America next year"* — even though the second is richer and more imaginative.

This is a trap for forecasters, consultants, and strategic planners. **The more detailed a scenario, the more likely it is to be admired and the less likely it is to occur.**

---

### **Less is more**

Hsee's dinnerware experiment: a set of 24 dishes in good condition was valued *more* in single evaluation than a set of 40 dishes — where 16 of the extra items were broken. Adding broken dishes lowered the average quality of the set, and the average (a prototype) dominated the evaluation rather than the total.

This is structurally identical to the Linda problem: **System 1 averages rather than sums, so adding low-value items to a set can reduce its perceived value.**

---

### **When logic prevails**

The conjunction fallacy is reduced when the question is framed as "How many of 100 people fitting this description are bank tellers?" rather than "What is the probability?" Frequency formats prompt a spatial/set representation that makes the inclusion relationship visible and intuitive.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> The conjunction fallacy arises because System 1 evaluates plausibility and coherence rather than probability — a more detailed, story-like description feels more probable even when it is necessarily less so.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Avoid the plausibility trap**

Then:

- **Recognize that adding details makes scenarios *feel* more convincing but makes them *logically* less likely** — every added constraint is another way the scenario can fail.
- **Be especially skeptical of highly specific predictions that feel compelling** — their vividness is the danger sign.

If you want to:

- **Think about probability more clearly**

Then:

- **Use frequency formats**: "How many people out of 100..." rather than "What is the probability..." — it makes inclusion relationships visible.

**A story that is coherent and compelling is not more likely to be true. It is often more likely to be wrong in exactly the specific ways it is elaborate.**
