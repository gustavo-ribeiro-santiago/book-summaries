# Chapter 3: Do We Own Ourselves? — Libertarianism

## Overview
Sandel examines **libertarianism** — the political philosophy that places individual freedom above all else and holds that any government beyond a minimal "night watchman" state violates rights. Through the arguments of Friedrich Hayek, Milton Friedman, and especially Robert Nozick, he explores the moral case for unregulated markets and self-ownership, then identifies deep tensions within the libertarian framework.

## Inequality and the Question of Redistribution
The Forbes 400 — with Bill Gates at $57 billion and the bottom threshold at $1.3 billion — illustrates America's extreme wealth concentration. Many consider this unjust and favor redistribution via taxation. Libertarians disagree: inequality is not inherently unjust if it arises through free exchange and voluntary transactions.

## The Libertarian Core: Self-Ownership
Libertarians oppose three types of state intervention:
1. **Paternalism:** Laws forcing people to protect themselves (helmet laws, seatbelts) violate the right to take personal risks.
2. **Morals legislation:** Using law to enforce majority moral or religious views on lifestyle choices violates freedom.
3. **Redistribution:** Taxing some to benefit others is coercive — a form of theft, however well-intentioned.

The moral bedrock is **self-ownership**: each person owns their own body, labor, and the fruits of that labor. If the state can take a portion of your earnings, it asserts partial ownership of you — making redistribution morally equivalent to forced labor.

## Nozick's Entitlement Theory
Robert Nozick (*Anarchy, State, and Utopia*, 1974) argues that there is no just *pattern* of distribution — no target distribution of income or wealth that justice requires. Instead, justice requires only:
1. **Justice in initial holdings** — resources were legitimately acquired.
2. **Justice in transfer** — subsequent exchanges were voluntary.

The **Michael Jordan thought experiment** illustrates this: if fans each voluntarily pay $5 more per ticket to watch Jordan, and Jordan ends up with $31 million, no one was coerced — how can it be unjust? Any attempt to redistribute Jordan's earnings to the poor violates his rights, because it treats him (and by extension all taxpayers) as partial slaves of the collective.

## Taxation as Forced Labor
Nozick's most provocative claim: "Taxation of earnings from labor is on a par with forced labor." If the state can direct 30% of your income, it effectively directs 30% of your time — asserting a property right over you. The libertarian sees a moral continuum from taxation → forced labor → slavery.

## Objections to Libertarianism

### The "Forced to Choose" Problem
For many people, market choices are not truly free — they reflect economic desperation rather than genuine preference. A worker who agrees to dangerous conditions for low pay is not freely choosing; necessity coerces them as surely as law would.

### Taxation as Social Prerequisite
Even wealthy individuals like Bill Gates owe their fortunes partly to social infrastructure: legal systems protecting property rights, public education that produced a skilled workforce, roads, security. The self-made billionaire is a myth; self-ownership cannot justify ignoring the social conditions that made wealth creation possible.

### The Consensual Harm Problem
Libertarianism's commitment to consent creates uncomfortable conclusions: Should consensual cannibalism be legal? Self-enslavement? Sandel points to a real case — a German man who advertised for a willing victim and was provided one — to ask whether the consent framework can bear the full moral weight libertarians place on it.

## Takeaway
Libertarianism captures something important: individual rights matter and coercive redistribution requires justification. But it relies on an implausibly atomistic view of the self — as if we generated our talents and wealth independently of society. And it cannot fully account for why some apparently consensual arrangements remain morally troubling. The next chapters explore alternative frameworks.
