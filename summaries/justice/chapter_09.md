# Chapter 9: What Do We Owe One Another? — Dilemmas of Loyalty

## Overview
Sandel explores the moral basis of **collective responsibility** and **communal obligations** — duties we may have not by virtue of individual consent but by virtue of membership in a community with a shared history. He challenges the liberal "moral individualist" view that we are only responsible for what we personally choose, and argues for a richer conception of the self embedded in particular communities and narratives.

## Apologies and Reparations
A wave of official apologies in recent decades tests the limits of individual-consent morality:
- Germany's reparations to Holocaust survivors and apologies to Israel.
- Japan's contested acknowledgment of enslaving "comfort women" during WWII.
- Australia's 2008 official apology to the Aboriginal "stolen generation" — children forcibly removed from families for decades.
- Virginia's 2007 apology for slavery; the U.S. House's 2008 resolution on slavery and Jim Crow.

## The Moral Individualist Objection
The standard liberal response to apologies for historic wrongs: "I wasn't alive then. I didn't do it. How can I be held responsible?"

Henry Hyde (opposing slavery reparations): "I never owned a slave. I never oppressed anybody."
John Howard (opposing Australia's apology): "I do not believe the current generation should formally apologize and accept responsibility for the deeds of an earlier generation."

This is **moral individualism**: our obligations arise only from our own voluntary choices. We are "free and independent selves, unbound by prior moral ties." If I didn't promise to repay my grandfather's debts, I owe nothing — however much those debts were incurred in my name.

## The Problem with Moral Individualism
Sandel argues this view is both philosophically incomplete and phenomenologically false. It fails to capture how we actually understand our identities and obligations.

Consider: if a soldier in your unit commits an atrocity in your name, most people feel some sense of collective shame — not personal guilt for what they didn't do, but a different kind of moral claim arising from shared identity. The moral individualist cannot make sense of this feeling.

Similarly, patriotism — feeling pride in your nation's achievements even if you personally contributed nothing — depends on a sense of collective identity. If collective pride is intelligible, so is collective shame and collective responsibility.

## Encumbered vs. Unencumbered Selves
Sandel draws on **communitarian philosophy** (Michael Sandel's own body of work, rooted in Aristotle and Hegel) to challenge the liberal "unencumbered self":
- Kant and Rawls imagine moral agents who can step back from all particular commitments and reason from a universal standpoint.
- But in reality, we are **constituted** by our memberships: family, religion, nation, community. These are not just preferences we happen to have — they define who we are.
- To abstract entirely from these identities in moral reasoning is not to achieve impartiality; it is to engage in a kind of self-misunderstanding.

## Narrative Identity and Moral Obligation
Alasdair MacIntyre (*After Virtue*): We understand our lives as narratives — stories with beginnings, middles, and projects inherited from the past. We are not self-made; we are characters in larger stories we didn't author.
- My obligations arise partly from the stories I inhabit: as a citizen, a child, a member of a church, a descendant of settlers or slaves.
- "I am someone's son or daughter, someone else's citizen, someone's fellow citizen."

This narrative view explains the moral weight of apologies: Germany apologizing for the Holocaust is not an admission that today's Germans committed the crimes, but a recognition that the German *nation* — a continuous moral community — bears a relationship to those events that obliges some response.

## Political Obligation and Communal Ties
The same logic applies to the obligation to obey the law. Most citizens never explicitly consent to their government, yet most feel some genuine (not merely prudential) obligation to their political community. Sandel argues this is best explained not by hypothetical contract (Rawls) or tacit consent (Locke) but by a genuine sense of membership and solidarity in a shared community.

## The Tension: Solidarity vs. Justice
Communal obligations can conflict with universal justice. Sandel acknowledges this risk: strong community loyalty can produce in-group favoritism, nationalism, and the exclusion of outsiders. The challenge is to honor particular commitments without abandoning the universal moral claims that all persons deserve respect.

## Takeaway
We are not atomistic individuals who incur obligations only through explicit consent. We are members of communities that shape our identities and generate genuine moral claims on us — including obligations to acknowledge wrongs done in our name and to bear some share of our community's moral inheritance. Justice must be able to speak to these communal dimensions of the self.
