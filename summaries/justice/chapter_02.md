# Chapter 2: The Greatest Happiness Principle — Utilitarianism

## Overview
Sandel examines **utilitarianism** — the doctrine that the right action is whatever maximizes overall happiness (pleasure minus pain). He presents its founding logic through Jeremy Bentham, tests it against powerful objections, and shows why many moral philosophers ultimately find it insufficient despite its intuitive appeal.

## The Lifeboat Case
In 1884, four shipwrecked sailors drifted at sea for weeks. Captain Dudley eventually killed the weakest crew member, cabin boy Richard Parker, so the others could survive by consuming his flesh. The three survivors were rescued and tried in England. Both admitted to the killing.

This case crystallizes two competing moral frameworks:
- **Utilitarian:** Killing one to save three maximizes total lives; Parker was dying anyway and had no dependents — the calculation seems to favor the act.
- **Rights-based:** Parker was used as a mere instrument without consent; human beings cannot be sacrificed for aggregate benefit regardless of calculations.

## Bentham's Utilitarianism
Jeremy Bentham (1748–1832) argued that pleasure and pain are the "sovereign masters" governing human behavior, and that the principle of utility — maximizing net happiness — should govern law and governance as well. His logic: since every moral argument ultimately appeals to human welfare, utility is the only genuine foundation of ethics.

Bentham proposed applying this logic to social problems, including a self-financing workhouse for beggars: removing them from the streets reduces discomfort to passersby, and the beggars themselves would be housed and fed (even if against their will). The example illustrates how utilitarian thinking, taken seriously, can justify coercive or dehumanizing policies when the aggregate numbers favor them.

## Two Major Objections

### Objection 1: Individual Rights
Utilitarianism can sanction horrors if the numbers work out. Sandel offers vivid examples:
- **Romans and Christians:** If millions of spectators derive immense pleasure from watching Christians being killed, a pure utilitarian cannot easily condemn the practice.
- **Torture and terrorism:** The "ticking bomb" scenario — torturing one suspect to save thousands — follows utilitarian logic, but many feel torture is intrinsically wrong regardless of consequences.

The objection: morality is not only about aggregate outcomes; it also concerns how *individuals* are treated. Rights and dignity may be non-negotiable, independent of the utility calculation.

### Objection 2: Higher and Lower Pleasures (Mill's Revision)
John Stuart Mill (1806–1873) tried to rescue utilitarianism by distinguishing *quality* of pleasures, not just quantity. Intellectual and moral pleasures are "higher" than bodily ones; "it is better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied." But this revision creates a problem: to rank pleasures by quality, we must appeal to a standard *other* than pleasure itself — undermining the utilitarian's claim that happiness is the sole measure.

The **experience machine** thought experiment (Robert Nozick): If you could plug into a machine that simulates all the pleasures of a fulfilling life with no real effort or risk, most people would refuse. Why? Because what matters is not just feeling good but *actually doing* things, *actually being* a certain kind of person. This suggests that happiness is not all that matters morally.

## Why Utilitarianism Persists
Despite its weaknesses, utilitarian thinking is deeply embedded in economics, public policy, and everyday moral reasoning. Cost-benefit analysis, welfare economics, and most regulatory frameworks operate on utilitarian assumptions. Sandel acknowledges this power while insisting that it cannot exhaust the moral domain.

## Takeaway
Utilitarianism is seductive — it is systematic, egalitarian (everyone's pleasure counts equally), and action-guiding. But it fails to adequately protect individual rights and misrepresents what we care about in life. A theory of justice must take rights seriously in ways that utility calculations cannot capture.
