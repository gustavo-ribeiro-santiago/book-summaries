# Chapter 8: Who Deserves What? — Aristotle

## Overview
Sandel presents Aristotle's theory of justice as a *teleological* framework: to determine the just distribution of a good, we must first understand the *purpose* (telos) of that good and identify the virtues required to fulfill it excellently. Unlike modern theories that try to be neutral about the good life, Aristotle insists that justice and virtue are inseparable.

## The Cheerleader Case
Callie Smart was a high school freshman cheerleader with cerebral palsy who was removed from the squad when officials required all members to perform gymnastic routines — splits, tumbles, cartwheels. Parents of other cheerleaders opposed her inclusion, claiming gymnastic ability was essential to cheerleading.

This case raises two questions:
1. **What qualifications are required to cheer well?** Is the purpose of cheerleading to perform gymnastics, or to inspire school spirit? Callie excelled at the latter.
2. **Why did other parents feel threatened?** If cheerleading can be done from a wheelchair, the gymnastic prowess their daughters displayed is no longer the *essential* excellence of the role — reducing the honor it confers.

The episode perfectly illustrates Aristotle: you cannot decide who deserves a position without first asking what virtues the position honors and what it is for.

## Aristotle's Teleological Framework
**Key principle:** Justice means giving people what they deserve. But what they deserve depends on the *telos* (purpose) of the good being distributed.

**The flute example:** Who deserves the best flutes? Not the richest buyer, not the most virtuous person generally — but the best *flute player*. The reason is not merely that better players produce better music (a utilitarian point), but that flutes *exist to be played well*. The purpose of the good determines who merits it.

This extends to social and political goods:
- **Tennis courts:** Should go to those who can make the best use of them — excellent players.
- **A Stradivarius violin:** Displayed in a collector's living room rather than played by Itzhak Perlman feels like a loss — because violins are *for* playing.

## Justice, Honor, and Political Life
Aristotle applies teleological reasoning to justice in the *polis* (city-state). The purpose of political life is not merely security or economic exchange — it is to enable citizens to live well, to cultivate virtue and achieve *eudaimonia* (human flourishing).

Therefore:
- Political offices should go to those with civic virtue and the capacity to serve the common good.
- Distributing offices to the wealthy or well-born (on grounds unrelated to political excellence) is unjust.
- The question "who deserves what?" always requires asking "what excellences are relevant?"

## The Slave Argument: Aristotle's Moral Blind Spot
Aristotle notoriously argued that some people are "slaves by nature" — suited only to follow, not to lead. This is used to justify the institution of slavery. Sandel uses this as an important lesson: Aristotle's teleological method is sound, but it can be badly misapplied if we hold mistaken empirical beliefs about human nature. Injustice often consists not in a wrong principle but in a wrong description of *who counts as what kind of person*.

The lesson for today: teleological reasoning is powerful, but the content of the telos can embed unjust assumptions. Debates about justice always involve contested empirical claims as well as contested moral ones.

## The Liberal Objection to Aristotle
Modern liberal theories (Kant, Rawls) try to remain neutral about the good life — they seek principles of justice that don't depend on any particular conception of human excellence or virtue. The worry: who decides what virtues are relevant? Imposing a vision of the good through law risks tyranny.

Aristotle's reply (via Sandel): we can't actually avoid this question. Every policy and institution reflects some implicit view of what's valuable. The choice isn't between a value-laden and a value-neutral conception of justice — it's between an explicit and an implicit one.

## Takeaway
Aristotle's teleological framework is compelling in cases like flute distribution and cheerleading, where the purpose of the role is relatively clear. It becomes more controversial when applied to political institutions, where reasonable people disagree about human excellence and the good life. Sandel does not endorse Aristotle wholesale but uses him to challenge the liberal assumption that justice can be separated from virtue.
