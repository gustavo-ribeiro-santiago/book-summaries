# Chapter 6: The Case for Equality — John Rawls

## Overview
Sandel presents John Rawls's *A Theory of Justice* (1971) — perhaps the most influential work of political philosophy of the twentieth century. Rawls attempts to derive principles of justice from a hypothetical contract struck behind a "veil of ignorance," producing a framework that neither utilitarianism nor libertarianism can match for its egalitarian force.

## The Problem with Actual Contracts
Most social contract theories (Locke, Rousseau) appeal to consent as the basis of political obligation. But Sandel shows that actual contracts are morally limited: they can reflect superior bargaining power, ignorance, or desperation. The mere fact of agreement doesn't make terms fair. Even constitutional framings can encode injustice (e.g., the U.S. Constitution's acceptance of slavery).

## Rawls's Thought Experiment: The Veil of Ignorance
Rawls asks: what principles of justice would rational people choose if they didn't know their place in society? Behind a **veil of ignorance**, no one knows their:
- Social class, income, or wealth
- Race, gender, or ethnicity
- Natural talents or disabilities
- Conception of the good life or religious beliefs

From this **original position** of equality, no one can rig the principles in their favor. The resulting principles would be genuinely fair because they are chosen without bias.

## The Two Principles of Justice
Rawls argues that rational people behind the veil would choose two principles:

**Principle 1 — Equal Basic Liberties:** Each person is to have the most extensive system of equal basic liberties compatible with a similar system for all (free speech, religion, assembly, right to vote, etc.). This principle takes priority over welfare considerations.

**Principle 2 — The Difference Principle:** Social and economic inequalities are just only if they:
(a) are attached to positions open to all (fair equality of opportunity), and
(b) work to the greatest benefit of the least advantaged members of society.

The difference principle does not require perfect equality, but it demands that any inequality must improve the situation of the worst-off. The justification: behind the veil, you might turn out to be at the bottom of society, so you would only accept a system that ensures the bottom is as well off as possible.

## Why Not Utilitarianism?
Behind the veil, Rawls argues, rational persons would *not* choose utilitarianism. You wouldn't want a system where you might be sacrificed for the aggregate benefit. (You might be the Christian thrown to the lions, or the scapegoat whose suffering makes thousands happy.) The veil of ignorance makes people risk-averse in a way that rules out utilitarian gambles.

## Why Not Libertarianism?
Rawls also rejects the libertarian view that natural talents justify whatever income the market assigns. Behind the veil, you don't know whether you'll be born talented or not — and your talents are, in any case, the product of genetic and social luck you didn't earn. The distribution of natural talents is "arbitrary from a moral point of view." A just society shouldn't allow these arbitrary factors to determine life prospects without any compensating principle.

## Moral Limits of Consent
Sandel deepens the analysis by examining when consent is and isn't sufficient to create moral obligations:
- A baseball card trade between a savvy older sibling and a naive younger one may be voluntary but still unfair.
- A $50,000 toilet repair contract with a confused elderly widow is technically consented to but morally invalid.
- Consent *without* mutual benefit (or with grossly unequal knowledge/power) does not generate genuine moral obligation.

Rawls's hypothetical contract solves this: by imagining *ideally fair* conditions for agreement, he identifies principles that real contracts may fail to achieve.

## Hume's Critique and Rawls's Response
David Hume argued that we sometimes have obligations of reciprocity even without explicit consent (e.g., if your neighbors build a roof over your house while you're asleep, you may owe them something). This shows that obligations can arise from benefits received, not just voluntary agreement. Rawls draws on this intuition: the principles chosen in the original position reflect what we owe one another as participants in a shared social enterprise.

## Takeaway
Rawls provides one of the most compelling answers to "what is a fair society?" His framework respects individual liberty while demanding that economic arrangements benefit everyone — especially those at the bottom. Its weakness: the abstraction of the original position may obscure the particular community memberships and moral commitments that actually shape our sense of justice.
