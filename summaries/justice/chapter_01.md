# Chapter 1: Doing the Right Thing

## Overview
Sandel opens with a vivid real-world case — price gouging after Hurricane Charley in Florida — to introduce the three foundational frameworks through which societies debate justice: **welfare**, **freedom**, and **virtue**. These three lenses reappear throughout the entire book.

## The Price Gouging Debate
After Hurricane Charley (2004), sellers charged $10 for bags of ice normally costing $2, and contractors demanded $23,000 to clear a fallen tree. Florida's attorney general prosecuted these cases under anti-gouging law. Free-market economists, however, argued that high prices are beneficial: they incentivize suppliers to bring goods to the disaster zone faster, and they reflect voluntary exchange between buyers and sellers.

This debate maps onto three competing moral arguments:
- **Welfare argument (utilitarian):** High prices bring more goods to market, maximizing overall benefit — but opponents counter that the poor are priced out of basic necessities, worsening aggregate welfare.
- **Freedom argument (libertarian):** Markets reflect voluntary choices and shouldn't be overridden — but opponents argue that people fleeing a hurricane are not truly free; their purchases are coerced by necessity.
- **Virtue argument:** Greed is a vice; exploiting neighbors in crisis reflects a failure of character. A good society discourages such behavior through law, affirming the civic value of shared sacrifice.

## The Purple Heart Debate
Sandel uses a second case — whether soldiers with post-traumatic stress disorder should qualify for the Purple Heart — to show that many policy disputes are really debates about **what virtues deserve honor**. The medal's purpose is to honor sacrifice, not bravery. The real disagreement is whether psychological wounds represent the same kind of sacrifice as physical ones, revealing that justice often requires us to assess competing conceptions of character.

## Three Theories of Justice
The chapter introduces the book's central framework:
1. **Maximize welfare** (utilitarian): The right action produces the greatest good for the greatest number.
2. **Respect freedom** (libertarian/Kantian): The right action honors individual rights and consent.
3. **Promote virtue / honor the good life** (Aristotelian): The right action rewards genuine excellence and cultivates civic character.

## Key Insight
Modern political debate tends to suppress the virtue argument — it seems too "judgmental." But Sandel argues that our deepest moral intuitions are often expressions of outrage at undeserved gain or unrecognized sacrifice. A fully honest account of justice must engage all three frameworks.

## Takeaway
Justice is not a single thing. It involves simultaneously asking: What maximizes well-being? What respects rights? And what promotes virtue and the common good? The rest of the book explores each tradition in depth.
