# Chapter 7: Arguing Affirmative Action

## Overview
Sandel uses the contentious debate over affirmative action to test the leading theories of justice developed in previous chapters — utilitarianism, libertarianism, Rawlsian egalitarianism, and Aristotelian virtue. The chapter reveals how each framework leads to different conclusions, and shows that debates about justice cannot always be resolved by a single principle.

## The Hopwood Case
Cheryl Hopwood, a white applicant with a 3.8 GPA and 83rd-percentile LSAT score, was rejected by the University of Texas Law School while minority applicants with lower scores were admitted. She sued, claiming discrimination. The university defended its policy on diversity grounds: equipping minority graduates to serve in Texas courts, the legislature, and law firms serves a civic purpose.

## Three Arguments for Affirmative Action

### 1. Correcting for Testing Bias
Standardized test scores may underpredict the academic potential of students from disadvantaged backgrounds. A 700 SAT from a student in a failing inner-city school may indicate more promise than the same score from a graduate of an elite prep school. This rationale does not challenge the goal of admitting the most promising students — it simply seeks the most accurate measure of promise.

### 2. Compensating for Past Wrongs
Affirmative action as reparation for the legacy of slavery, segregation, and discrimination. This argument faces a tough objection: the beneficiaries (often middle-class minority students) are not necessarily those who suffered most, and those who "pay" the compensation (rejected white applicants like Hopwood) are not those who perpetrated the injustice.

### 3. Promoting Diversity
The most widely used justification. A diverse student body benefits everyone educationally — students learn more from people with different backgrounds, experiences, and perspectives. It also serves a civic purpose: producing a racially diverse professional class better able to serve a diverse society. This rationale is not about rewarding victims; it is about advancing a legitimate educational and civic goal.

## Does Affirmative Action Violate Rights?
The principled objection: using race as a factor in admissions treats applicants as means to social ends rather than as individuals deserving equal treatment.

Ronald Dworkin responds: What *right* has Hopwood been denied? The right to be judged solely on academic merit? But universities routinely consider non-academic factors — geographic diversity, athletic ability, musical talent, family legacy. Being from Idaho or being a cellist can help your application. If we accept these preferences, why not racial diversity?

The anti-preference reply: racial classifications are *different* because they invoke a history of stigma and subordination. Even benign racial preferences may reinforce racial consciousness and generate resentment.

## The Aristotelian Lens: What Is the University For?
Sandel introduces Aristotle's teleological framework: to determine what admissions policy is just, we must ask what the university is *for*. If its purpose is purely to rank applicants by test scores and grades, Hopwood's claim is strongest. But if its purpose is also to create an educated citizenry and serve democratic society, then a broader set of qualifications — including race, geography, and character — may be relevant.

The debate over affirmative action is partly a debate about the purpose of higher education — an inherently normative and contested question.

## The Moral of the Story
No single theory settles the affirmative action debate:
- Libertarians oppose it as a violation of individual rights.
- Utilitarians accept it if it maximizes educational and social welfare.
- Rawlsians disagree among themselves depending on whether they focus on the difference principle or on fair equality of opportunity.
- Aristotelians ask what virtues and civic purposes a university should honor.

## Takeaway
Affirmative action forces us to confront fundamental questions: What is merit? What is the purpose of education? How do we balance individual rights against collective goods? These are not merely empirical questions but philosophical ones — and honest engagement requires bringing our moral frameworks to the surface, not pretending to reason from neutral ground.
