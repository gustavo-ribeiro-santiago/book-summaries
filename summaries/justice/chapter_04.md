# Chapter 4: Hired Help — Markets and Morals

## Overview
Sandel investigates whether markets are *always* neutral and efficient allocators, or whether some goods are corrupted when bought and sold. He examines two contested cases — military service and surrogate motherhood — to argue that the case for free markets rests on two distinct claims (freedom and welfare) that may conflict with civic virtue and the common good.

## The Volunteer Army vs. Conscription
The U.S. Civil War draft allowed wealthy draftees to pay $300 (a near-year's wages) or hire substitutes to fight in their place. This spawned the famous slogan "rich man's war, poor man's fight." The system was morally criticized even though — or because — it was voluntary.

Today's all-volunteer military operates similarly: those who don't want to serve hire others via the labor market. Sandel asks: if the Civil War system was unjust because it allowed the affluent to buy their way out, isn't the volunteer army unjust for the same reason?

### Libertarian/Utilitarian Case for the Volunteer Army
- Conscription forces people to risk their lives against their will — a form of slavery.
- Voluntary exchange in the military labor market benefits both parties (soldier gets pay; public gets defense) without coercing anyone.
- Eliminating barriers to mutually beneficial trades maximizes welfare.

### Two Objections

**1. Fairness and False Freedom:** When economic options are severely limited, "voluntary" enlistment may not reflect genuine preference but economic necessity. The poorest enlist not because they want to serve but because they have no better alternatives. This means market conscription and legal conscription both coerce — just through different mechanisms.

**2. Civic Virtue and Shared Sacrifice:** Handing off civic obligations to those willing to be paid corrupts those obligations. Michael Walzer's argument: military service is a civic duty. When wealthy citizens pay others to fulfill it, they degrade both the duty and their relationship to the community. A democracy in which the privileged never share the risks of war may not remain a true democracy.

## Markets and Surrogacy
The chapter's second case examines commercial surrogacy — paying a woman to carry and bear a child. Courts and legislatures have debated whether surrogacy contracts are enforceable.

The famous **Baby M case** (1986): Mary Beth Whitehead signed a contract to carry a baby for William Stern, then refused to relinquish the child. The New Jersey Supreme Court voided the contract, ruling it exploitative and contrary to public policy — surrogacy treats children as commodities and women's reproductive capacities as for-hire services.

### Competing Arguments:
- **Pro-market:** If a woman freely agrees to be a surrogate for payment, why shouldn't that contract be enforced? Refusing to honor it paternalistically denies her autonomy.
- **Anti-market:** Some goods — children, reproductive labor, intimacy — are degraded when subjected to market valuation. The norms appropriate to family life (love, unconditional commitment) are undermined when commercial norms intrude. Selling a child is wrong even if buyer and seller both consent.

## The Core Distinction: Corruption vs. Coercion
Most objections to markets focus on coercion (were the terms truly voluntary?). Sandel introduces a more fundamental objection: some market exchanges are wrong not because they are coerced but because they **corrupt** the good being exchanged by subjecting it to market norms.

Paying someone to be a friend, or to apologize on your behalf, doesn't produce friendship or authentic apology — the market logic defeats the purpose. Similarly, using financial incentives to fill civic roles may hollow out the civic meaning of those roles.

## Takeaway
Markets are powerful tools, but they are not morally neutral. Before allowing a market in some good or service, we need to ask two questions:
1. Is the exchange truly voluntary, or does inequality make it coercive in practice?
2. Does commodifying this good corrupt or degrade its value?

Justice requires deciding which goods should be allocated by markets and which should be protected from them.
