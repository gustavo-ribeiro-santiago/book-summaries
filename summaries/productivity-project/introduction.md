# Introduction & A New Definition of Productivity

## Overview

Chris Bailey introduces the book through two lenses: the backstory of his "Year of Productivity" project, and the reframing of what productivity actually means in the modern knowledge economy.

## The Backstory: A Year of Productivity

- Bailey graduated from Carleton University in Ottawa with two full-time job offers — and turned both down to spend a year intensively researching and experimenting with productivity, funded by $10,000 in savings.
- For 365 days he read academic papers and books, interviewed productivity experts, and ran personal experiments — some extreme: meditating 35 hours a week, working 90-hour weeks, living in total isolation, drinking only water for a month, watching 70 hours of TED Talks in a week.
- The result is this book: 25 of the most impactful productivity tactics he discovered, every one of which he personally uses.

## The Three Ingredients of Productivity

Bailey's central thesis: in the modern knowledge economy, productivity is no longer about how efficiently you work — it's about how much you **accomplish**. That requires managing three things:

1. **Time** — what you commit to and when
2. **Attention** — how much of your focus you bring to what you're doing
3. **Energy** — how much mental and physical fuel you have

All three are inseparably linked. Time without attention produces poor-quality output. Attention without energy collapses quickly. Energy without prioritized time is squandered.

The old factory-era metric — hours worked × widgets produced — no longer applies. Most modern work is intellectual and discretionary. You have more control over what you work on than ever before, which means *what* you choose to do matters far more than *how long* you work.

## The Monk vs. The Cocaine-Fueled Stock Trader

Bailey uses this pairing to illustrate the ideal pace:
- The **monk** moves at a snail's pace — deliberate but unproductive because too slow to accomplish much.
- The **cocaine-fueled stock trader** moves frantically — stimulated but unproductive because too hurried to identify what actually matters.
- The most productive people work **somewhere between** — fast enough to get things done, slow enough to work deliberately and with intention.

## The Meditation Insight

Early in his project, Bailey quit his daily 30-minute meditation practice to "get more done." The result was paradoxical: he became *less* productive. Working frantically on autopilot, he lost the deliberateness that made him effective.

**Core insight**: working on autopilot — automatically responding to whatever lands in your inbox — is the enemy of productivity. Working deliberately, with clear intention about what matters, is what separates productive people from busy ones.

## Key Takeaway

Productivity is not about doing more faster. It is about accomplishing more of what matters by managing your time, attention, and energy with intention. The rest of the book is a toolkit for doing exactly that.
