## Overview

This section focuses on the primary ways we waste time: procrastination, short-sightedness about consequences, and technology-driven distraction. Bailey approaches each with self-awareness, research, and practical countermeasures.

## Chapter 5: Cozying Up to Ugly Tasks — The Science of Procrastination

Even during his most productive week (the TED interview week), Bailey tracked 6 hours of procrastination. His conclusion: **procrastination is universal and nothing to be ashamed of** — but understanding its mechanics helps you fight it.

### Why We Procrastinate: The Six Triggers

Research identifies six task attributes that make procrastination more likely. The more attributes a task has, the more aversive it becomes:

1. **Boring**
2. **Frustrating**
3. **Difficult**
4. **Unstructured or ambiguous**
5. **Lacking personal meaning**
6. **Lacking intrinsic rewards** (not fun or engaging)

Your highest-impact tasks almost always have several of these triggers — that's why they're hard, and why they're valuable. Doing your taxes and writing a novel have almost every trigger. Watching Netflix has none.

### How to Cope with Ugly Tasks

- **Lower your resistance level**: Don't ask yourself to work on an aversive task for hours — ask yourself to do it for just 10 minutes. Starting is the hardest part; once you begin, resistance often fades.
- **Connect tasks to your "why"**: Even a boring task becomes more tolerable when you understand why it matters to your deeper goals.
- **Work on aversive tasks during high-energy periods**: When your energy is low, your brain's resistance to unpleasant tasks increases. Schedule ugly tasks during BPT.
- **Future self thinking**: Procrastination is essentially borrowing against your future self. Every task you put off today is a burden you're placing on tomorrow-you.

## Chapter 6: Meet Yourself From the Future — Visualizing Your Future Self

A key driver of procrastination is **temporal discounting** — the psychological tendency to heavily discount future rewards and costs relative to immediate ones.

- Research shows that when we think about our future selves, the same brain regions activate as when we think about strangers. We literally feel emotionally disconnected from our future selves.
- This is why we eat the cookie even though we want to be healthy next month — the future "me" who wants to be healthy feels like a different person.

### Practical Countermeasures

- **Write a letter to your future self**: Articulating what you want for yourself in 6 months or a year makes the future you feel more real, which makes sacrificing for them more motivating.
- **Vividly imagine your future self**: Athletes use visualization; you can apply it to productivity. Imagining yourself having completed a project makes you more likely to start it.
- **Project task lists forward**: When setting daily tasks, also ask: "What will tomorrow-me wish I had done today?"

## Chapter 7: Why the Internet Is Killing Your Productivity

The internet is engineered to be maximally addictive — designed by billion-dollar companies whose business model depends on capturing your attention as often as possible.

- The average person checks their smartphone 150+ times per day and spends several hours on social media and news sites.
- Every unplanned visit to a distracting site costs more than just the time spent there — **resuming focus** after an interruption takes an average of 23 minutes.
- **Email** is among the worst offenders: constant checking fragments the day into short bursts, preventing the deep focus that complex work requires.

### Practical Countermeasures

- **Internet blockers**: Tools like Freedom, Cold Turkey, or SelfControl block distracting sites during focused work sessions.
- **Schedule internet use**: Instead of checking email and social media continuously, batch it into 2–3 designated windows per day.
- **Make distraction harder**: Log out of social media accounts so accessing them requires active effort. Turn off all non-critical notifications.
- **Phone in a different room**: Physical distance from your phone significantly reduces checking behavior.
- **Create "distraction-free" work zones** — times or places where internet use is off-limits.

## Key Takeaways for Part Two

1. Procrastination is human — but understanding the six triggers lets you design around them rather than fighting willpower alone.
2. You're emotionally disconnected from your future self; deliberately bridge that gap with letters, visualization, and forward-planning.
3. The internet is optimized against your productivity. You need active, structural defenses — not just willpower.
