# Part I: Where I'm Coming From — Chapters 4–6 (1983–2015)

## Overview

These three chapters cover Bridgewater's resurrection and transformation into the world's largest hedge fund, Dalio's pioneering work in systematic/algorithmic trading, global client relationships including an early deep connection with China, and the difficult succession challenges of the 2010s.

## Chapter 4: My Road of Trials (1983–1994)

- Rebuilt Bridgewater from scratch after the 1982 crash. Started with six employees; grew by selling research packages for $3,000/month.
- **The computerized decision system**: Inspired by early personal computers, Dalio began writing down trading criteria as explicit rules, then back-testing them against a century of historical data. The result: a real-time decision machine that processes 100M+ datasets. He found the computer — running his own logic without emotion — consistently outperformed his in-the-moment intuition.
- Key insight: "Rather than forecasting changes in the economic environment and shifting positions in anticipation, we pick up changes as they occur and move our money to keep in markets that perform best in that environment."
- Won World Bank pension account with zero AUM — pure credibility. Soon added Mobil Oil, Singer, and others. Became the top-performing U.S. bond manager in the world.
- **China**: Visited Beijing in 1984; donated to the embryonic securities regulatory infrastructure. Began a 30+ year relationship with China's financial development.
- Developed the concept of "alpha overlay" — separating passive market beta from active bet alpha — which became foundational to modern institutional investing.
- Created "All Weather" portfolio concept: a portfolio that performs across all economic environments by balancing risk across four regimes (inflation/deflation × growth/recession).

## Chapter 5: The Golden Age (1995–2010)

- Pure Alpha and All Weather strategies became globally recognized. Bridgewater grew into managing money for sovereign wealth funds, central banks, pension funds.
- **The 2008 Financial Crisis**: Bridgewater anticipated the crisis years in advance using their debt/deleveraging models. Published *How the Economic Machine Works* and the *Template for Understanding Debt Crises* — which they shared with policymakers including the U.S. Treasury, Fed, and IMF.
- While most funds lost 30–50%, Pure Alpha returned +9.4% in 2008 and All Weather remained stable. This cemented Bridgewater's global reputation.
- Dalio's management of the firm began to grapple with scale: how to maintain the radical transparency culture as headcount grew past 1,000.
- Developed *Principles* as an internal document — first posted publicly in 2010 after the crisis brought media attention. Downloaded 3 million times.

## Chapter 6: Returning the Boon (2011–2015)

- Began formal succession planning — Dalio aimed to transition from CEO while maintaining his role as co-CIO.
- Elevated Greg Jensen and Eileen Murray to co-CEO positions.
- Continued building the "idea meritocracy" tooling: Baseball Cards, Dot Collector, issue logs, and other systematic feedback and decision-weighting tools.
- Tensions between the demands of being founder-leader and the need to build an institution that would outlast him began to surface.

## Themes

- Systematic decision-making beats intuition at scale — the insight that drove Bridgewater's edge.
- Alpha and beta are separable; the institutional investment industry was fundamentally restructured by this insight.
- Succession from founder to institution is one of the hardest problems in organizational life.
