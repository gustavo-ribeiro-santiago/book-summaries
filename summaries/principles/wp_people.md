# Work Principles: Getting the People Right (WP7–9)

## Overview

"Culture and its people are symbiotic." The three principles in this section cover the most important organizational variable: who you bring in, how you evaluate them honestly, and how you continuously develop or move on from them.

---

## WP7: Remember That the WHO Is More Important Than the WHAT

- The most common management mistake: focusing intensely on *what* should be done while neglecting *who* should be responsible for it.
- "When you know what you need in a person to do the job well, and you know what the person you're putting into it is like, you can pretty well visualize how things will go."
- **Responsible Parties (RPs)**: The most critical choices are which people bear responsibility for outcomes and machines at the highest levels. Get these right and most other things take care of themselves.
- Dalio's goal as a manager: "to be like a conductor of people, many of whom can play their instruments better than I can." The best conductor doesn't need to be the best musician — they need to be the best at orchestrating.
- When you inherit a problem or underperformance, start by asking: "Do I have the right person in this role?" before redesigning the process.

### The RP Framework
- RPs are personally responsible for achieving goals and tasks — not just for trying.
- Structure incentives so that RPs experience the consequences of outcomes they produce.
- Everyone should have someone they report to — even owners and founders are accountable to investors, clients, and employees.

---

## WP8: Hire Right, Because the Penalties for Hiring Wrong Are Huge

- Most organizations hire badly — interviewers unconsciously favor people like themselves rather than people who fit the role.
- **Values, abilities, and skills** are the three layers to assess, in that order:
  1. *Values*: Do they share the organization's core values? This is hardest to change.
  2. *Abilities*: Do they have the mental and behavioral attributes for this role and future roles?
  3. *Skills*: Do they have the technical knowledge for the job? This is easiest to teach.
- The interview process is not a one-time event — it extends through the early months of employment.
- **Reference checks are essential.** Most interviewers ask the wrong questions; probing for specific past behaviors with specific former colleagues reveals far more than any answer in a controlled interview.
- "Warts and all" transparency during recruiting: let candidates honestly assess what they're getting into. This reduces mismatch and increases retention of the right people.
- The cost of a bad hire is enormous — not just salary, but time, morale, team dynamics, and the difficulty of removing someone once they're established.

### Practical Hiring Principles
- Hire for the person's trajectory, not just their current state — is this person growing?
- Seek people who have demonstrated they can do *exactly* what you need, or who have demonstrated the relevant attributes in an adjacent domain.
- Match the person's strengths to the role's requirements systematically — use actual data (e.g., psychometric assessments, track records) not gut feel alone.

---

## WP9: Constantly Train, Test, Evaluate, and Sort People

- Employment is an ongoing process of mutual assessment, not a one-time contract.
- **Training**: Make expectations explicit. Invest in helping people understand what great performance looks like in their role.
- **Testing**: Regularly stretch people with challenges slightly beyond their current capacity — this reveals their ceiling and accelerates growth.
- **Evaluating**: Use objective data — metrics, peer assessments, case studies — to continuously update your understanding of what each person is like.
- **Sorting**: Place people in roles that match their actual attributes. This is not punitive — it's the right outcome for both the individual and the organization.

### Addressing Underperformance
- The worst approach: tolerate underperformance while quietly becoming resentful.
- The right approach: identify the root cause — is it values (not fixable), abilities (partially fixable), or skills (fixable)? Then address accordingly.
- "If they can't do the job after being trained and given time to learn, get rid of them; if they can, promote them."
- A person in the wrong role is not a failure as a person — they may be excellent in a different context. Helping them find that context (inside or outside your organization) is the humane and effective response.

### The Personal Inventory
- Encourage people to build an honest self-assessment: what are my genuine strengths? What are my genuine weaknesses? What roles leverage the former and require little of the latter?
- Self-awareness is the highest predictor of eventual success. People who can accurately assess themselves continue improving; people who can't keep making the same mistakes.

---

## Key Takeaway

The "People Right" principles are about building a rigorous, honest, and compassionate system for putting the right people in the right roles — and continuously verifying and improving that match. Most organizational failure is fundamentally a people problem disguised as a strategy or process problem. Getting this right is the highest-leverage thing any leader can do.
