# Introduction — Principles: Life and Work

## Overview

Ray Dalio opens by calling himself a "dumb shit" who doesn't know much relative to what he needs to know — and credits his success entirely to an approach based on principles. The introduction frames the entire book: what principles are, why they matter, and how this book is structured.

## Key Concepts

- **What Principles Are**: Fundamental truths that serve as foundations for behavior that gets you what you want. Applied again and again to similar situations, they allow better decisions without reinventing the wheel each time. Having good principles is like having good recipes for success.
- **Being Principled**: To be principled means consistently operating with principles that can be clearly explained. Most people can't do this — and very rarely write them down. Dalio wishes he could know the principles that guided Einstein, Jobs, Churchill, da Vinci.
- **Where Principles Come From**: Through experience and reflection, from others (parents, traditions, religions), or adopted as packages (legal frameworks). The risk is adopting principles without thinking about them, which leads to inconsistency.
- **First Principle**: "Think for yourself to decide (1) what you want, (2) what is true, and (3) what you should do to achieve #1 in light of #2 — and do that with humility and open-mindedness."
- **How Dalio Learned His**: Through a lifetime of mistakes, reflection, and iterating. Key meta-principles discovered: make believability-weighted decisions; operate by principles; systemize your decision making. He eventually embedded his decision rules into algorithms that could run in parallel with his own judgment — and found the computer outperformed his intuition.
- **Book Structure**:
  - *Part I: Where I'm Coming From* — autobiography of mistakes and lessons
  - *Part II: Life Principles* — overarching principles for individuals
  - *Part III: Work Principles* — how Bridgewater's idea meritocracy works

## Core Message

Principles are the most leveraged thing a person can develop: they convert experience into transferable, repeatable decision-making tools. Dalio's journey from an ordinary kid to founder of the world's largest hedge fund was driven not by genius but by systematically learning from failure, writing down principles, and refining them over decades.
