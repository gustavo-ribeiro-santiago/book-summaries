# Part I: Where I'm Coming From — Chapters 7–8 & Bridge to Principles (2016–2017)

## Overview

The final chapters of Part I cover Dalio's most recent transition — temporarily returning as co-CEO in 2016 to stabilize Bridgewater's leadership — and then his broader philosophical reflection on what he has learned from a life of striving. The section closes with the bridge text that introduces his approach to principles.

## Chapter 7: My Last Year and My Greatest Challenge (2016–2017)

- Despite Dalio stepping down as CEO, Bridgewater's management (under Greg Jensen and Eileen Murray as co-CEOs) showed strain: tech and recruiting areas slipping while investment performance remained excellent.
- Greg Jensen — Dalio's mentee of 20 years — was overstretched carrying both co-CEO and co-CIO roles simultaneously. Dalio admits this was his biggest management mistake: placing someone he loved in an impossible dual role.
- The resolution was painful but followed Bridgewater's principles: the Management and Stakeholders Committees deliberated transparently and voted. Greg stepped back to focus on CIO; Dalio temporarily rejoined as co-CEO.
- Media portrayed it as a bitter power struggle; Dalio insists it was the idea-meritocratic process working as designed.
- Advice from management expert Jim Collins: governance needs only two things — capable CEOs and a capable system to replace them if they fail.
- Dalio built formal governance structures for the first time, ensuring Bridgewater could operate at its standards regardless of who led it. Stepped down from co-CEO in April 2017.

## Chapter 8: Looking Back from a Higher Level

- With experience, every new crisis becomes "another one of those" — classifiable and approachable with prior-developed principles, rather than a unique emergency.
- Reality is a "gorgeous perpetual motion machine" of cause-effect relationships. Pain is nature's signal that there's something important to learn.
- **On success and happiness**: The incremental benefits of wealth and status fall off quickly. Having basics well — sleep, relationships, food, sex — matters far more than wealth beyond sufficiency. Being on top gives options but also burdens.
- Most importantly: *"The satisfaction of success doesn't come from achieving your goals, but from struggling well."* The child star, lottery winner, and early-peaking athlete rarely end up happy — without a new, larger struggle, victory leaves a void.
- Dalio's current stage: "my desire to succeed has given way to a desire to help others succeed." Passing on principles is like passing on DNA — it lives beyond the individual.

## Bridge: Principles

- Everything happens because of cause-effect relationships repeating and evolving over time — from galaxies to markets to people.
- The key skill: look to patterns, understand the cause-effect machinery driving them, and build principles for dealing with them.
- Reality rewards you when your principles work and punishes you when they don't — a natural feedback loop for refinement.

## Themes

- Founder transitions are among the hardest organizational challenges — humility and process are the only tools.
- Success is a means to struggle, not an end. The struggling itself is where life's meaning lives.
- The purpose of everything — including this book — is evolution: helping others compound the understanding that took a lifetime to build.
