# Part I: Where I'm Coming From — Chapters 1–3 (1949–1982)

## Overview

The first three chapters cover Ray Dalio's life from childhood through the founding of Bridgewater and its near-catastrophic early collapse in 1982. These years forged his most foundational beliefs about markets, failure, and the importance of calibrated humility.

## Chapter 1: My Call to Adventure (1949–1967)

- Grew up on Long Island in a middle-class family; poor rote memory but deeply curious and entrepreneurial from childhood.
- At 12, began investing with caddying money — bought Northeast Airlines by accident and tripled his money when it was acquired. Thought markets were easy; they weren't.
- The 1960s U.S. was aspirational, the economy booming, stocks quadrupling. The common belief: managing the economy had become a science.
- First lesson in market psychology: prices reflect expectations, not fundamentals in isolation.

## Chapter 2: Crossing the Threshold (1967–1979)

- Entered C. W. Post College; loved learning for the first time because he could study what interested him.
- Began meditating (Transcendental Meditation) — a lifelong habit that he credits for calm, open-minded thinking.
- Majored in finance; discovered commodity futures trading due to low margin requirements.
- Witnessed Nixon's 1971 dollar-gold decoupling: government officials promised no devaluation, then devalued. Learned never to believe such assurances.
- Started Harvard Business School; worked on NYSE floor. Watched the stock market *rise* on the day Nixon broke Bretton Woods — his first encounter with the counterintuitive nature of markets.
- After HBS, worked briefly at New York brokerage, then commodities trading. Started Bridgewater out of his two-bedroom apartment in 1975 with $0 capital.
- Bridgewater's first form: research and consulting to institutional clients on commodity price exposures.

## Chapter 3: My Abyss (1979–1982)

- 1981: Dalio publicly predicted a depression would follow loose monetary policy. He was on TV, writing newsletters, building a reputation as a bold forecaster.
- He was dead wrong. The Fed's tightening killed inflation, and the 1982 stock market launched one of history's greatest bull markets. Meanwhile Mexico's near-default triggered a global debt relief — deflationary crisis averted.
- Lost virtually all his money. Had to borrow $4,000 from his father to pay bills.
- Most painful professional experience of his life — but he calls it one of the best things that ever happened to him.
- **Key lesson**: "I was so broke I couldn't muster enough money to pay for an airplane ticket." But the experience transformed his confidence into humility. It permanently shifted his question from *"I know I'm right"* to *"How do I know I'm right?"*

## Themes

- Markets are humbling — no matter how smart your thesis, reality is the final arbiter.
- Failure in public is painful but forces genuine recalibration.
- The entrepreneurial instinct to build independently versus working within institutions sets the trajectory for everything that follows.
