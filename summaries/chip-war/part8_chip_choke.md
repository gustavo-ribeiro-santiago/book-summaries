# Part VIII: The Chip Choke (Chapters 49–54)

## Overview
The Trump and Biden administrations transformed American semiconductor policy from passive "run faster" to active "chip choke" — using export controls to cut off China's access to American chip technology. The campaign against Huawei, the prosecution of Fujian Jinhua for IP theft, and sweeping export control reforms attempted to halt China's semiconductor ambitions. The result is a new Cold War fought with export regulations rather than nuclear weapons.

## "Everything We're Competing On" (Ch. 49)
Intel CEO Brian Krzanich showed up at a White House meeting with "a palpable sense of fear in his eyes." China's $250 billion in chip subsidies alarmed U.S. chipmakers but they dared not say so publicly — China was their biggest market. The Obama administration was slow to act, producing a report in 2016 that recommended "run faster" while the U.S. position was visibly eroding. Trump's National Security Council, led by Matt Pottinger, concluded that "everything we're competing on in the twenty-first century… all of it rests on the cornerstone of semiconductor mastery." The NSC began using export controls — previously reserved for nuclear and missile technology — against commercial chip companies.

## Fujian Jinhua (Ch. 50)
**Fujian Jinhua**, a Chinese DRAM startup backed with $5 billion in government funding, needed manufacturing knowledge it couldn't acquire legally. It conspired with Taiwan's UMC to hire engineers from **Micron** and steal its DRAM design files. Employee Kenny Wang downloaded 900 "Micron Confidential" files and googled "clear computer use records" to cover his tracks. Micron sued UMC and Jinhua. In an extraordinary counter-move, a court in Fujian Province — the same province as Jinhua — banned Micron from selling 26 products in China using patents Jinhua filed based on stolen Micron technology. The U.S. responded by blacklisting Fujian Jinhua from buying U.S. chips or equipment, effectively ending its operations before it produced a single chip. The episode illustrated both the scale of Chinese IP theft and the power of American export controls.

## The Assault on Huawei (Ch. 51)
The Trump administration's attack on Huawei was the most sweeping use of export controls in decades. First, Huawei was added to the Entity List in 2019, blocking it from buying U.S. chips. Huawei had stockpiled chips in anticipation and initially survived. The administration then issued a **"foreign direct product rule"** — any chip made anywhere in the world using American equipment, software, or designs required a U.S. license before being sold to Huawei. Since TSMC uses American EUV machines (ASML), American chip design software (Cadence, Synopsys), and American intellectual property (ARM), this rule meant TSMC could no longer produce chips for Huawei. Huawei's smartphone business collapsed. Its Kirin chip program was effectively ended. A single regulatory change severed China's most advanced chip company from the global supply chain.

## China's Sputnik Moment? (Ch. 52)
Xi Jinping publicly described the chip restrictions as a "Sputnik moment" for China. The government doubled down on semiconductor subsidies, pouring more money into SMIC, CXMT (memory), and equipment companies. Chinese chip designers scrambled to find alternatives to U.S. design software. SMIC made progress on 7nm chips without EUV, using a technique called "multi-patterning" that requires many more manufacturing steps — less efficient but not impossible. China's chip industry would make progress, but the gap to the frontier was enormous and growing. ASML, under pressure from the Dutch and U.S. governments, refused to ship EUV machines to China. Without EUV, Chinese chipmakers were limited to older process nodes.

## Shortages and Supply Chains (Ch. 53)
The COVID-19 pandemic revealed the semiconductor supply chain's fragility to the general public. Auto companies that cut chip orders in early 2020 found they couldn't reorder — fabs had filled their capacity with consumer electronics. A fire at a Japanese fab, ice storms in Texas, and COVID lockdowns in Malaysia compounded the disruption. Factory shutdowns from Detroit to Wolfsburg forced politicians to explain chip supply chains in press conferences. The CHIPS Act — providing $52 billion in U.S. subsidies for domestic semiconductor manufacturing — was passed in 2022. Intel, TSMC, and Samsung all announced new U.S. fabs. But building a leading-edge fab takes 3–5 years. The vulnerabilities identified by the crisis would take years to address.

## The Taiwan Dilemma (Ch. 54)
The book's ultimate tension: Taiwan makes ~37% of the world's new computing power and essentially all the most advanced chips, but Beijing considers Taiwan a renegade province. China has never renounced the use of force to achieve "reunification." A blockade or invasion would devastate the global chip supply — and the global economy. The U.S. has committed to defend Taiwan, but the commitment is deliberately ambiguous. Both Beijing and Washington understand that Taiwan's chip industry is simultaneously a reason to fight and a reason not to fight: destroying TSMC would eliminate the prize everyone is competing over. Morris Chang — born in China, educated at Harvard and MIT, built America's chip industry, founded TSMC — embodies the impossibility of decoupling: his life story is the interconnection of the system that both sides now want to unwind.

## Key Themes
- **Export controls are the new weapon** of great-power competition: more targeted and reversible than military force, but potentially as devastating to a technology company as a missile strike.
- The **Huawei case** demonstrated that America's chokehold on semiconductor design tools, IP, and equipment is a genuine strategic asset — and that it can be wielded.
- China's chip self-sufficiency campaign will make partial progress, but the gap to the frontier is enormous: **getting to 7nm without EUV is possible; getting to 3nm without it is not**.
- The **Taiwan dilemma** is unsolvable in the short term: the most strategic technology asset on earth sits in the most contested geographic location on earth.
- The CHIPS Act represents a bipartisan consensus that the era of passive "run faster" is over — but rebuilding domestic manufacturing capability is a decade-long project, not a quick fix.
