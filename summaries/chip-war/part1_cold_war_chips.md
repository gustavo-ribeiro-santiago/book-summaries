# Part I: Cold War Chips (Chapters 1–6)

## Overview
The chip industry was born from the convergence of World War II's industrial legacy, Cold War military spending, and the creative energy of a small cluster of engineers in California. This section traces the invention of the transistor and the integrated circuit, and shows how military contracts — not consumer demand — funded the industry's crucial early years.

## From Steel to Silicon (Ch. 1)
World War II was decided by industrial output — steel, aluminum, ships, and bombs. But already by 1945 it was clear that a new era of precision weaponry was coming. Early computers used vacuum tubes — lightbulb-sized switches that were unreliable and room-filling. ENIAC, built for the U.S. Army in 1945 with 18,000 vacuum tubes, averaged one malfunction every two days. A smaller, faster, cheaper switch was desperately needed.

## The Switch (Ch. 2)
William Shockley at Bell Labs theorized a "solid state valve" using semiconductor materials (silicon, germanium) that could control electrical current without moving parts. In December 1947, his colleagues Walter Brattain and John Bardeen built the first working **transistor** — a device that could amplify signals and act as an on/off switch. The transistor was far smaller and more reliable than vacuum tubes. Shockley, furious his colleagues had beaten him, locked himself in a hotel room and designed an improved junction transistor. Bell Labs licensed the transistor for $25,000 — giving the entire electronics industry access to the invention.

## The Integrated Circuit (Ch. 3)
The transistor enabled computers, but wiring thousands of transistors together created catastrophic complexity. Two inventors independently solved this in 1958–59:
- **Jack Kilby** at Texas Instruments: built multiple components on a single piece of germanium, connected by tiny wires.
- **Bob Noyce** at Fairchild Semiconductor: used Jean Hoerni's "planar process" to embed transistors directly into silicon and connect them with deposited metal lines — no wires at all. More reliable and far easier to miniaturize.

Both invented the **integrated circuit** (chip). Fairchild Semiconductor's eight founders — including Noyce and Gordon Moore — became the nucleus of Silicon Valley. Their company would spawn dozens of offspring firms, including Intel.

## Liftoff (Ch. 4)
Noyce's integrated circuits were initially 50x more expensive than discrete components. They needed a buyer who valued reliability and small size more than price. They found one: **the U.S. military**. Sputnik (1957) triggered massive Pentagon spending on rockets, missiles, and electronics. The Apollo program's guidance computers used Fairchild's chips. By 1965, the U.S. military bought 72% of all integrated circuits. Without these defense contracts, the chip industry could not have scaled fast enough to bring costs down.

Gordon Moore observed in 1965 that the number of transistors per chip was doubling roughly annually — **Moore's Law** — predicting exponential growth in computing power. This prediction drove an industry-wide commitment to relentless miniaturization.

## Mass Production (Ch. 5)
Chips were only useful if they could be manufactured reliably at scale. This required obsessive attention to "yield" — the percentage of chips on a wafer that work. Every particle of dust, impurity, or temperature variation could destroy chips. Engineers like Morris Chang and Andy Grove developed manufacturing disciplines to improve yields systematically. Charlie Sporck drove Fairchild to offshore assembly to Asia — first to Hong Kong, then across Southeast Asia — cutting costs dramatically. This began the semiconductor industry's global supply chain.

## "I Want to Get Rich" (Ch. 6)
Silicon Valley's culture was unlike any corporate environment before it. Fairchild's engineers left to found competing companies, taking knowledge and talent with them. Stock options gave even assembly-line workers a stake in success. The market for chips expanded from military to industrial to consumer applications. Texas Instruments shipped the first pocket calculator. The combination of military funding, entrepreneurial culture, and relentless cost reduction created an industry that would reshape the world.

## Key Themes
- The chip industry was created by **military demand**, not consumer markets.
- **Innovation and manufacturing excellence** were inseparable — brilliant designs only mattered if they could be built reliably and cheaply.
- Silicon Valley's advantage was cultural as much as technical: the willingness to job-hop, start companies, and share knowledge accelerated progress.
- Moore's Law was not a law of physics but a **self-fulfilling commitment** — engineers drove progress because they believed progress was possible.
