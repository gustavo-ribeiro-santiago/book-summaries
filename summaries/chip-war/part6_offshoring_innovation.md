# Part VI: Offshoring Innovation? (Chapters 35–41)

## Overview
The 2000s and 2010s brought a paradox: American chip design flourished as never before, while American chip manufacturing largely disappeared. The rise of the fabless model — design in America, manufacture in Asia — created fabulously profitable chip companies (Nvidia, Qualcomm, Apple silicon) while transferring manufacturing expertise and capacity to Taiwan and Korea. Meanwhile, Intel missed the mobile revolution and began its long decline.

## "Real Men Have Fabs" (Ch. 35)
AMD founder Jerry Sanders famously declared "real men have fabs" — believing that owning manufacturing facilities was essential to staying competitive. But economics was making fabs increasingly difficult to justify. Advanced fabs now cost $20 billion. The DRAM industry had already consolidated to three producers (Samsung, SK Hynix, Micron). Logic chip manufacturing was heading the same way — toward extreme consolidation at the frontier. Most analog chips could still be made at older, cheaper processes, but leading-edge logic required constant capital reinvestment that only the largest foundries could sustain.

## The Fabless Revolution (Ch. 36)
**Nvidia** — founded in 1993 by Jensen Huang and two colleagues at a Denny's in San Jose — became the paradigm of fabless success. Nvidia designed graphics chips (GPUs) but outsourced manufacturing to TSMC. Its 2006 launch of CUDA software (free, but only runs on Nvidia chips) turned GPUs into general-purpose parallel computing engines. This laid the foundation for Nvidia's dominance in AI hardware. Without the fabless model, Nvidia would never have had the capital to invest billions in software. **Qualcomm** similarly designed chips for the mobile revolution (CDMA, then 4G/5G) without ever building its own fabs.

## Morris Chang's Grand Alliance (Ch. 37)
As the generation that built Silicon Valley aged out, their replacements were more MBA than PhD. Companies like AMD spun off their manufacturing operations — AMD's fabs became **GlobalFoundries**, with Abu Dhabi's sovereign wealth fund as primary investor. The U.S. government's Committee on Foreign Investment (CFIUS) waved the sale through, seeing no national security issue. In retrospect, this was a major mistake: GlobalFoundries struggled to keep up with Moore's Law and eventually dropped out of the leading-edge race, ceding the field entirely to TSMC and Samsung. Morris Chang, still smoking his pipe in Taiwan, extended TSMC's lead relentlessly.

## Apple Silicon (Ch. 38)
Apple's decision to design its own chips — starting with the A-series in 2010 — created what are arguably the world's most advanced processors. Apple designs the chips in California, but manufactures them exclusively at TSMC in Taiwan. The A14, powering the iPhone 12, contained 11.8 billion transistors made at a 5nm process. Apple's chip team produced designs that outperformed Intel's processors in both speed and power efficiency. This success came entirely from the combination of world-class design (Silicon Valley) and world-class manufacturing (Taiwan). It also meant that Apple's most critical product — the processor in every iPhone — depended entirely on TSMC's continued operation.

## EUV (Ch. 39)
**Extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography** is the most important enabling technology in modern chipmaking. Using light with a 13.5 nanometer wavelength (compared to 193nm for conventional deep-UV lithography), EUV can print features small enough for 7nm, 5nm, and smaller chips. ASML spent over a decade and billions of dollars developing EUV machines, each of which costs over $100 million. The machines are so complex that they contain components from hundreds of suppliers across dozens of countries. A single EUV machine ships in 40 freight containers. Without EUV, the most advanced chips simply cannot be made. ASML is the only company on earth that makes them. This single chokepoint in the supply chain became a critical lever of geopolitical power.

## "There Is No Plan B" (Ch. 40)
As chip supply chains became more concentrated, their fragility became more apparent. A single earthquake in Taiwan, a fire in a Japanese fab, or ice storms in Texas could cause months of disruption. When COVID hit in 2020, it scrambled supply and demand signals simultaneously — auto companies canceled chip orders, then couldn't reorder when demand surged, causing factory shutdowns worldwide. The chip shortage of 2020–21 revealed how deeply the global economy depended on a handful of factories in East Asia. Politicians who had never thought about semiconductors suddenly had to explain to voters why new cars weren't available. "There is no plan B" if TSMC goes down — it makes chips for which there is no other supplier.

## How Intel Forgot Innovation (Ch. 41)
Intel's decline is one of the most analyzed failures in business history. After dominating PC and server chips for two decades, Intel missed mobile — ARM-based chips from Qualcomm and Apple's A-series outperformed Intel's power-hungry x86 designs. Intel's manufacturing, once the world's best, fell behind TSMC's. The company repeatedly promised new manufacturing processes but delivered late. Manufacturing problems delayed chip generations, allowing AMD (now a fabless designer, manufacturing at TSMC) to close and then exceed Intel's performance. Intel remained a profitable giant, but its aura of invincibility was gone. The company that had defined Moore's Law was struggling to keep up with it.

## Key Themes
- The **fabless revolution** enabled an explosion of chip design innovation in America, but at the cost of manufacturing expertise, which migrated to Asia.
- **TSMC's dominance** was self-reinforcing: every fabless company that chose TSMC strengthened TSMC's manufacturing capabilities, which attracted more fabless customers.
- **EUV lithography** created a single, irreplaceable chokepoint: all advanced chips require EUV, and only ASML makes EUV machines.
- The offshoring of manufacturing was celebrated as efficiency, but it created catastrophic supply chain vulnerabilities — visible only when the system failed.
- Intel's decline showed that even the most dominant company is not immune to the consequences of complacency and missed transitions.
