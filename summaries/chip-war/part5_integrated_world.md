# Part V: Integrated Circuits, Integrated World? (Chapters 29–34)

## Overview
The 1990s brought globalization to its apex in the chip industry. Taiwan's TSMC reinvented the business model of semiconductor manufacturing. China opened up economically but remained technologically backward. The world's chip supply chains became more efficient, more interconnected — and more concentrated in a handful of irreplaceable companies.

## "We Want a Semiconductor Industry in Taiwan" (Ch. 29)
Taiwan's powerful technology minister K. T. Li hired Morris Chang in 1985 with a blank check to build a world-class chip industry. Chang had spent decades at Texas Instruments mastering semiconductor manufacturing. His radical idea: a **pure-play foundry** — a company that manufactures chips for customers without designing any of its own. TI's executives had rejected this idea in 1976. Now, backed by Taiwanese government capital (48% stake), Chang founded **TSMC** in 1987. Gordon Moore told him it wasn't a good idea. Chang proved him spectacularly wrong. TSMC gave every chip designer a reliable, trustworthy manufacturer. By never competing with customers, it became everyone's partner. Its economics were powerful: the more chips it produced, the better its yields and the lower its costs. Consolidation was inevitable, and TSMC won.

## "All People Must Make Semiconductors" (Ch. 30)
China had all the ingredients for a chip industry — a vast workforce, educated scientists, a huge domestic market — but Mao's Cultural Revolution destroyed them. Scientists were sent to peasant farms. Foreign technology was rejected. Mao literally declared "all people must make semiconductors" as if transistors could be forged by agricultural laborers. By the time Deng Xiaoping's reforms arrived, China's chip industry was more than a decade behind its neighbors. Meanwhile, Taiwan, South Korea, and Southeast Asia were pulling peasants off farms and into semiconductor jobs. China spent decades trying to catch up from this self-inflicted disaster.

## "Sharing God's Love with the Chinese" (Ch. 31)
In the late 1990s, Silicon Valley executive **Richard Chang** (no relation to Morris) was recruited to found **SMIC** — Semiconductor Manufacturing International Corporation — to bring advanced chipmaking to China. He described his mission as "sharing God's love with the Chinese." SMIC built capability and attracted talent, but it struggled with profitability, management interference from the Chinese state, and bruising intellectual property lawsuits from TSMC. China subsidized SMIC heavily, but the state's involvement in corporate governance created constant inefficiencies. SMIC remained years behind TSMC in manufacturing capability.

## Lithography Wars (Ch. 32)
**Photolithography** — printing circuit patterns onto silicon using light — became the most critical and contested technology in chipmaking. As transistors shrank, the wavelength of light used to etch them had to shrink too. The machines that did this became extraordinarily expensive and technically complex. American firms like GCA had dominated in the 1980s but collapsed under mismanagement. **ASML** — a Dutch company formed from a Philips and ASM International joint venture — gradually took over the lithography market. Japanese firm Nikon competed fiercely. The lithography race was a proxy war for semiconductor leadership: whoever could print the smallest features controlled the cutting edge.

## The Innovator's Dilemma (Ch. 33)
Intel's dominance of PC microprocessors seemed unassailable, but the company faced Clayton Christensen's "innovator's dilemma" in multiple directions. Each generation of chips required more investment to produce — advancing Moore's Law was getting brutally expensive. Meanwhile, new types of chips for new devices (mobile phones, graphics, networking) were being developed by fabless startups relying on TSMC. Intel's vertically integrated model — designing and manufacturing its own chips — became a competitive disadvantage as manufacturing grew more capital-intensive. Intel kept winning the PC market but missed mobile entirely, a failure that would haunt it for decades.

## Running Faster? (Ch. 34)
As the 1990s ended, America's semiconductor leadership appeared secure. American companies dominated chip design, chip architecture (x86, ARM), electronic design automation software, and chipmaking equipment. The U.S. government's policy was simple: **"run faster"** — maintain the lead through innovation, not protection. Washington assumed that technological diffusion was inevitable and that trying to prevent other countries from acquiring chip technology was futile. This consensus — that globalization was irreversible and that it served American interests — would go largely unchallenged until the 2010s.

## Key Themes
- **TSMC's foundry model** was a revolutionary business innovation: by separating chip design from manufacturing, it democratized chip design and enabled a wave of fabless startups.
- China's semiconductor failure was largely **self-inflicted** — Maoist ideology destroyed the scientific and industrial base that neighboring countries were building.
- **ASML's rise** in lithography showed that dominance in one critical piece of the supply chain — equipment — could be as powerful as dominance in chip manufacturing itself.
- Intel's near-monopoly in PC chips gave it enormous power but also created complacency that would prove costly when mobile computing arrived.
- The "run faster" philosophy worked while America was the only serious innovator — but as China's capabilities grew, it would prove dangerously complacent.
