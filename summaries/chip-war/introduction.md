# Introduction: The World's Most Critical Technology

## Overview
The book opens on August 18, 2020 — the USS *Mustin* sails through the Taiwan Strait, bristling with computerized weaponry, while the U.S. Commerce Department imposes export controls on Huawei. These two events — a warship and a regulatory ruling — frame the central thesis: the fate of nations is now determined not by steel and gunpowder but by silicon and semiconductors.

## Chips Are Everything
Every modern device — smartphones, cars, data centers, missiles — runs on semiconductors. The chip industry now produces more transistors per day than there are cells in the human body. A single advanced chip (Apple's A14) contains 11.8 billion transistors. The cost of a transistor has fallen by a billionfold since 1958. Without this extraordinary compounding of computing power, the internet, cloud computing, AI, and modern military weapons simply would not exist.

## The Concentration Problem
The global semiconductor supply chain is shockingly concentrated:
- **TSMC** (Taiwan) produces nearly all the world's most advanced logic chips.
- **ASML** (Netherlands) manufactures 100% of EUV lithography machines — without which cutting-edge chips cannot be made.
- **Two Korean firms** (Samsung, SK Hynix) produce 44% of the world's memory chips.
- Chips from Taiwan provide **37% of the world's new computing power** each year.

By comparison, OPEC's 40% share of world oil seems diversified. A single earthquake, missile strike, or naval blockade could trigger devastating disruptions to the global economy.

## The China Challenge
China spends more money importing chips than oil. Its entire digital economy — surveillance systems, AI, e-commerce, smartphones — runs on foreign semiconductors. Xi Jinping has concluded this dependency is a strategic vulnerability and has launched a multi-hundred-billion-dollar campaign to build a domestic semiconductor industry. The U.S. responded with the Entity List, restricting Huawei's access to American chip technology. When the restrictions took effect, Huawei's global expansion halted.

## The Taiwan Dilemma
TSMC's most advanced fab sits a few dozen miles from where the USS *Mustin* sailed. Beijing considers Taiwan a renegade province. If China were to blockade or invade Taiwan, the impact on global chip supply — and therefore on the global economy — would be catastrophic. Taiwan's semiconductor dominance is not an accident but the deliberate result of decades of industrial policy, investment, and integration with Silicon Valley.

## The Book's Argument
Drawing on archives from three continents and over 100 interviews, Miller argues that semiconductors have defined modern history — shaping military power, economic development, and geopolitical competition more than any other technology. To understand where we are headed, we must trace how we got here: from the first transistors in a Bell Labs lab to the trillion-dollar industry whose control is now the central prize of great-power competition.
