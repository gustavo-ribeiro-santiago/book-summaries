# Part VII: The Big Picture (Chapter 21)

## Chapter 21: Leading for the Long Future

## Overview
In the final section, Goleman zooms out from individual attention and organizational leadership to the largest scale: how might the capacities developed throughout the book — inner focus, empathy, systems thinking — be applied to humanity's most urgent and intractable challenges? The chapter is part policy analysis, part moral philosophy, and part manifesto for a new kind of leadership.

## The Long-Term Thinking Deficit
Goleman opens with his uncle, nuclear physicist Alvin Weinberg, who was fired as director of Oak Ridge National Laboratory for refusing to stop raising alarms about reactor safety and nuclear waste — concerns that later events vindicated. Weinberg's story illustrates the political and institutional forces that punish long-horizon thinking: the incentive structures of politics (election cycles), business (quarterly earnings), and human cognition (present bias) all systematically discount the future.

The U.S. National Intelligence Council projected in a 2025 scenario report: ecological impacts creating resource scarcity, demand for energy and water outstripping supply, geopolitical shocks and disruptions. These projections were largely ignored by government — because **no agency is charged with acting for the long term**.

Cognitive psychologist Elke Weber documented that the reversal time for initially unpopular long-term decisions averages just 9–15 months. NYC's smoking ban, British Columbia's carbon tax — both despised initially, both popular within a year. Leaders willing to endure short-term political pain for long-term benefit face an informational problem: most constituents cannot calculate long-term benefits, but can feel short-term costs immediately.

## Reshaping Systems
Sustainable change requires not tweaking behavior within a system but **reshaping the system itself**:
- **Change at the margins**: recycling, efficiency improvements — important but insufficient.
- **System shifts**: abandoning fossil fuels, restructuring food systems, redesigning economic incentive structures.

Unilever CEO Paul Polman announced at Davos that the company would halve its environmental footprint by 2020 and source agricultural raw materials from 500,000 smallholder farms across Africa, Southeast Asia, and Latin America — directly connecting one of the world's largest consumer goods companies to rural poverty reduction while securing supply chain resilience. This is what Goleman calls **"conscious capitalism"**: orienting a company's performance around all stakeholders (employees, communities, customers, environment) rather than only shareholders. Studies consistently show these companies outperform purely profit-oriented competitors on financial metrics as well.

## Big-Picture Leadership
Goleman describes three levels of leadership:
1. **Good-enough leadership**: operates within given system parameters, executes a defined mission, addresses immediate problems.
2. **Great leadership**: defines a mission, acts at multiple levels, addresses systemic problems, does not accept systems as given but works to transform them.
3. **Wisdom leadership**: acts on behalf of society itself rather than a particular group, operates with a time horizon spanning generations, sees all people as "We" rather than "Us and Them." Examples: Jefferson, Lincoln, Gandhi, Mandela, Buddha, Jesus.

The Dalai Lama's self-queries for checking motivation, offered as a practical tool for leaders:
- Is this for me, or for others?
- For the benefit of the few, or the many?
- For now, or for the future?

## The Focus Imperative
The largest lens for focus encompasses global systems, considers the needs of the powerless and poor, and looks far ahead in time. The paradox of the Anthropocene — human systems are disrupting the global systems that support life — requires a new kind of leadership attention: one that integrates self-awareness, empathy, and systems thinking at civilizational scale.

Peter Senge: "The bigger your horizon, the bigger the system you can see." Short time horizons don't just miss future risks — they systematically ignore the feedback loops and second-order effects that make present interventions fail.

## The Triple Focus Applied Globally
The book's organizing framework — inner focus, other focus, outer focus — scales from individual excellence to civilizational leadership:
- **Inner focus** at scale: leaders who act from genuine values rather than political calculation or personal interest.
- **Other focus** at scale: compassion that extends to the powerless, the distant, and the unborn — groups with no political leverage.
- **Outer focus** at scale: systems understanding that encompasses geopolitical, economic, ecological, and social systems in their interactions.

Goleman quotes Larry Brilliant: "Civilizations should be judged not by how they treat people closest to power, but rather how they treat those furthest from power — whether in race, religion, gender, wealth, or class — as well as in time."

## Final Message
The book ends with the Dalai Lama's exhortation at an MIT conference on global systems: "We have the capacity to think several centuries into the future. Start the task even if it will not be fulfilled within your lifetime. Offer a positive vision, with enthusiasm and joy, and an optimistic outlook."

In the end, Focus is not just a book about individual performance but about what happens when the hidden driver of excellence — our capacity to direct attention wisely — is brought to bear on the challenges that matter most. Everything depends on where we choose to look.
