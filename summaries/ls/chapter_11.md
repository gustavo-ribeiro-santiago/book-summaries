# Chapter 11: Adapt

## The Paradox of Speed

Building a startup requires speed — but the Lean Startup also requires **quality**. These are not opposites: they are allies. Defects introduced for speed create rework later that *costs* speed. Toyota's paradox: "Stop production so that production never has to stop."

The challenge is building an organization that automatically adjusts its processes to current conditions — what Ries calls an **adaptive organization**. Rules and processes should emerge from the work itself, not be imposed top-down.

## The Five Whys

The foundational tool for building an adaptive organization is **Five Whys** — a technique from Taiichi Ohno and the Toyota Production System. When a problem occurs, ask "why?" five times to trace the symptom back to its human, systemic root cause.

A classic example from IMVU:
1. A new release disabled a feature. *Why?* A server failed.
2. The server failed. *Why?* An obscure subsystem was used incorrectly.
3. It was used incorrectly. *Why?* The engineer didn't know how to use it.
4. He didn't know. *Why?* He was never trained.
5. He wasn't trained. *Why?* His manager doesn't believe in training new engineers.

A purely technical problem revealed a management culture issue. The fix is a **proportional investment**: small problems get small fixes; large problems get larger fixes. An hour of training investment now prevents months of recurring failures.

## Automatic Speed Regulator

Five Whys acts as a natural governor: the more problems you have, the more you invest in process improvements. As those investments pay off, problems decrease and the team speeds up again. This ties the rate of progress to *learning*, not just execution.

Two simple rules to start:
1. **Be tolerant of all mistakes the first time.**
2. **Never allow the same mistake to be made twice.**

## The Curse of the Five Blames

When Five Whys goes wrong, it becomes **Five Blames**: teams point fingers instead of analyzing root causes. The antidote:
- Everyone affected by the problem must be in the room.
- Senior management must model the mantra: "If a mistake is this easy to make, shame on us for building a system that allows it."
- Start with narrow, low-stakes problems to build the muscle before tackling high-stakes ones.

## QuickBooks: From Waterfall to Adaptive

Intuit's QuickBooks had always shipped on a one-year cycle — plan for 3-4 months, build for 6-9 months, launch. In Year 1 of a reform effort, the team shipped a complete online banking redesign that was technically perfect but took customers **four to five times longer** to reconcile transactions. The NPS score dropped 20 points — the first time in the product's history. The feedback loop was so slow that by the time the problem was discovered, it was too late to fix for nine months.

Over three years, the QuickBooks team restructured around:
- **Smaller teams** (from 15+ to five-person cross-functional squads)
- **Shorter cycles** (features built end-to-end in ~6 weeks)
- **Customer involvement from inception** — engineers, not just product managers, talk to customers
- **Technical infrastructure changes** — a virtualization system allowing safe live testing without risking customer data

The result: higher customer satisfaction, more units sold, and a continuous improvement culture that will outlast any individual launch.

## Key Takeaway

> "Adaptive processes force you to slow down and invest in preventing the kinds of problems that are currently wasting time. As those preventive efforts pay off, you naturally speed up again."

You cannot trade quality for time. The Five Whys ensures that every investment in prevention is proportional to the actual pain it addresses — building a process that gets better and faster together.
