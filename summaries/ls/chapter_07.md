# Chapter 7: Measure

## Why Accounting Matters for Startups

Standard accounting is built for established businesses with stable operating histories. It is useless for evaluating startups — which are too unpredictable for standard forecasts. What startups need is **innovation accounting**: a system that objectively proves whether they are learning how to grow a sustainable business.

Innovation accounting works in three steps:
1. **Establish the baseline**: Use an MVP to gather real data on the startup's current metrics — conversion rates, retention, revenue per user.
2. **Tune the engine**: Run experiments targeted at improving specific drivers of the growth model.
3. **Pivot or persevere**: If experiments are consistently moving metrics toward the ideal, continue. If not, make a fundamental change.

## The Danger of Vanity Metrics

The most seductive trap in startup measurement is **vanity metrics** — numbers that go up and to the right but don't reflect whether the business is actually improving. Total registered users, total revenue, total page views — these feel good but hide the truth.

IMVU's board presentations showed a beautiful hockey-stick curve of gross customers. But the cohort analysis told a different story: **the conversion rate of each new batch of customers was flatlined for seven months**. Despite thousands of product improvements, the percentage of new customers who became paying users stayed stuck near 1%.

## Cohort Analysis: The Gold Standard

Instead of cumulative totals, **cohort analysis** tracks the behavior of each independent group of customers who encounter the product in a given period. This reveals whether the actual experience is improving, regardless of whether gross numbers are growing (which can be explained by acquiring more users, not by building a better product).

IMVU's cohort analysis showed that their hard work wasn't moving the needle — which forced the honest conversation that led to their critical pivot.

## Actionable Metrics vs. Vanity Metrics

For a metric to be **actionable**, it must demonstrate clear cause and effect. If you can't explain exactly what actions would replicate the result, it's a vanity metric.

**Grockit** (an online test prep company) discovered this the hard way. Despite disciplined agile development, they were measuring gross usage numbers — total students, total questions answered. These fluctuated seemingly on their own, making it impossible to judge whether product changes were having any effect. Switching to cohort-based metrics and **split-test experiments** (A/B tests) for every new feature immediately revealed which investments mattered and which didn't.

## The Three A's of Good Metrics

1. **Actionable**: Shows clear cause and effect, allows decisions to be made.
2. **Accessible**: Written in plain language ("people," not "page hits"), available to every employee.
3. **Auditable**: Data can be verified against reality; employees can spot-check with real customers.

## Kanban for Validated Learning

Grockit adopted a **kanban** system requiring that user stories pass through four states: backlog → in development → done → validated. The crucial addition: a story is not "done" until it has been *validated* — meaning a split test has shown it changed customer behavior. This prevented the team from endlessly building features that nobody cared about.

## Key Takeaway

> "Only 5 percent of entrepreneurship is the big idea, the business model, the whiteboard strategizing, and the splitting up of the spoils. The other 95 percent is the gritty work that is measured by innovation accounting."

Innovation accounting is what happens during the "photo montage" that movies skip. It is the system that tells the difference between genuine progress and success theater.
