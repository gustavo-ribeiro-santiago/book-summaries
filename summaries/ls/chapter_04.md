# Chapter 4: Experiment

## From Alchemy to Science

The Lean Startup reconceives every startup activity as a **scientific experiment**. A true experiment begins with a clear hypothesis, makes predictions, and tests those predictions empirically. Startup experimentation is guided by the startup's vision — the goal is to discover which parts of that vision are sound and which are wrong.

## The Value Hypothesis and Growth Hypothesis

Every startup must answer two core questions:
- **Value hypothesis**: Does the product actually deliver value to customers once they're using it?
- **Growth hypothesis**: How will new customers discover the product, and will the mechanism for growth work?

These are the two most important leap-of-faith questions any startup faces.

## Examples of Experiments as Products

**Zappos**: Instead of building a massive shoe warehouse and logistics operation upfront, Nick Swinmurn photographed inventory at local stores and offered them online. If a customer bought, he purchased the shoes at full price and shipped them. This tested the single most critical assumption — that people would buy shoes online — at minimal cost.

**Hewlett-Packard volunteer program**: Ries applies the same framework to a corporate social innovation initiative, showing how to break a grand plan into testable hypotheses and run small experiments in weeks rather than planning for 18 months.

**Village Laundry Services (India)**: Mounted a washing machine on a pickup truck to test whether people would pay to have laundry cleaned on-site. Found answers about pricing, speed preferences, and customer concerns in a week for under $8,000.

**Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)**: A new government agency could start with a simple hotline number using low-cost tools and targeted flyers in a few city blocks — at trivial cost — before committing to a $500M budget and full staff.

## An Experiment Is a Product

The experiment is not just a test — it is the first product. It attracts early adopters, surfaces real problems, and creates a specification for what must be built based on what is actually working, not what might work.

## Key Takeaway

> "The question is not 'Can this product be built?' In the modern economy, almost any product that can be imagined can be built. The more pertinent questions are 'Should this product be built?' and 'Can we build a sustainable business around this set of products and services?'"

Every startup activity — every feature, every marketing campaign — is an experiment designed to achieve validated learning. If you cannot fail, you cannot learn.
