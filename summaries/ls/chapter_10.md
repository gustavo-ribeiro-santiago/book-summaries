# Chapter 10: Grow

## The Engine of Growth

Growth in a startup is not an accident — it is powered by a specific mechanism Ries calls the **engine of growth**: the feedback loop that drives sustainable new customer acquisition. Sustainable growth has one defining rule:

> **New customers come from the actions of past customers.**

There are four ways past customers drive growth: word of mouth, side effects of product use, funded advertising (when revenue exceeds acquisition costs), and repeat purchase.

## The Three Engines

### 1. The Sticky Engine of Growth
Products designed for long-term retention: customers who start using them are expected to keep using them indefinitely. The key metric is **churn rate** — the fraction of customers who stop using the product each period. If new customer acquisition exceeds churn, the product grows through compounding.

*Example*: A database vendor or collectibles marketplace. Growth stalls when churn ≈ acquisition rate. The counterintuitive insight: to fix growth, **don't invest more in marketing — invest in improving retention for existing customers**.

### 2. The Viral Engine of Growth
Growth happens as a side effect of normal product use, without customers consciously trying to spread the word. The key metric is the **viral coefficient**: how many new customers does each new customer bring in? A coefficient > 1 produces exponential growth; a coefficient < 1 fizzles out.

*Example*: Hotmail added "Get your free email at Hotmail" to every outgoing message. Within six months: 1 million users. Eighteen months later: 12 million users, sold to Microsoft for $400M.

Viral products often don't charge users directly (charging adds friction), instead monetizing through advertising or other indirect means.

### 3. The Paid Engine of Growth
The startup acquires customers by spending money, as long as the **customer lifetime value (LTV)** exceeds the **cost per acquisition (CPA)**. The marginal profit (LTV − CPA) determines how fast the engine turns. To grow faster: increase LTV or lower CPA.

*Example*: IMVU eventually succeeded with paid advertising because it served teenagers and low-income customers that other companies dismissed, and developed techniques (mobile billing, cash mail-in) to monetize them. This let IMVU outbid competitors for the same advertising slots.

## Engines Determine Product/Market Fit

Marc Andreessen's concept of **product/market fit** — the moment a startup finds widespread customers who resonate with the product — can be made rigorous through engines of growth. Each engine has quantifiable metrics that indicate proximity to product/market fit:
- A sticky startup with a viral coefficient of 0.9+ is close.
- A sticky startup measuring compounding growth rate improvements over time is on track.

If you have to ask whether you've achieved product/market fit, you haven't.

## When Engines Run Out

Every engine eventually exhausts its customer base. Early-adopter markets are finite. When growth slows, companies that relied on vanity metrics think the product development team stopped working. The real cause: the engine has run its course, and no new engine is ready.

This is why companies must simultaneously **tune the current engine** and **develop new sources of growth** for when the current engine runs dry.

## Key Takeaway

> "Startups don't starve — they drown."

There are always too many ideas. The engine of growth framework gives startups a small set of metrics to focus on, preventing the paralysis of trying to optimize everything at once.
