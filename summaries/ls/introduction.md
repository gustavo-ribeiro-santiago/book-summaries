# Introduction

## The Core Premise

Eric Ries opens with a confession: his first startup failed not for lack of a great product or smart team, but because he lacked the *process* to turn good ideas into a sustainable company. This sets up the book's central claim — **startup success can be engineered, learned, and taught**. It is not a matter of luck, genes, or being in the right place at the right time.

## Entrepreneurship Is Management

Ries challenges the cultural split between "entrepreneurship" (cool, creative) and "management" (dull, bureaucratic). He argues these must converge. A startup is a human institution, not just a product — and institutions require disciplined management. The antidote to both rigid traditional planning and chaotic "just do it" thinking is a new kind of management built for extreme uncertainty.

## Origins: IMVU and the Lean Startup

At IMVU, Ries and his co-founders deliberately broke convention: they shipped a terrible, buggy minimum viable product early, charged for it immediately, and changed it dozens of times a day based on what they learned from real customers. This unorthodox approach — borrowed from lean manufacturing, customer development, agile development, and design thinking — became the foundation of the **Lean Startup** method.

## The Five Principles

The Lean Startup rests on five pillars:

1. **Entrepreneurs are everywhere** — anyone building something under extreme uncertainty qualifies, regardless of company size or sector.
2. **Entrepreneurship is management** — startups need a discipline specifically designed for uncertainty.
3. **Validated learning** — the primary unit of startup progress is *learning* what customers really want, demonstrated through rigorous experimentation.
4. **Build-Measure-Learn** — the core feedback loop: turn ideas into products, measure customer response, learn whether to pivot or persevere.
5. **Innovation accounting** — a new accounting system to measure real progress, set meaningful milestones, and hold innovators accountable.

## Why Startups Fail

Two traps destroy most startups: (1) over-reliance on polished plans and market research that assume a stable environment startups don't have, and (2) the "Just Do It" school that mistakes chaos for creativity. Both waste the scarcest resource — **human time, passion, and skill**.

## Key Takeaway

> "Startup success can be engineered by following the right process, which means it can be learned, which means it can be taught."

The book's goal is to provide that process — not a collection of tactics, but a principled framework for continuous innovation.
