# Chapter 6: Test

## The Minimum Viable Product

A **Minimum Viable Product (MVP)** is not the smallest possible product — it is the fastest way to get through the Build-Measure-Learn feedback loop with the minimum amount of effort. Its goal is to begin learning, not end it. An MVP tests fundamental *business hypotheses*, not just design or technical questions.

Groupon started as a WordPress blog skinned with the company's name. Coupons were handmade PDFs emailed from Apple Mail. That was enough to prove the concept and launch the fastest-growing company in history to $1 billion in sales.

## Why First Products Aren't Meant to Be Perfect

Early adopters — the customers who matter for an MVP — prefer an incomplete product. They want to be first. They fill in gaps with their imagination. They are suspicious of something too polished. **Additional features beyond what early adopters need is waste**.

Apple's original iPhone lacked copy-paste, 3G, and corporate email. Google's original search worked only for Stanford-related topics. These weren't failures — they were deliberate MVP launches targeting early adopters.

## Three MVP Techniques

**1. The Video MVP (Dropbox)**
Drew Houston couldn't demo Dropbox's technology in prototype form — the product required deep OS integration too complex to fake. Instead, he made a 3-minute video demonstrating the *concept* with in-jokes that resonated with tech early adopters. The beta waitlist jumped from 5,000 to 75,000 overnight. The video *was* the MVP.

**2. The Concierge MVP (Food on the Table)**
Instead of building software, CEO Manuel Rosso found one customer at a grocery store and hand-delivered a personalized weekly meal plan and shopping list in exchange for $9.95/week. No database, no algorithm, no chefs on staff. Each new customer taught them what the product needed to do. Only when they were too busy to take on more customers did they start automating.

**3. The Wizard of Oz MVP (Aardvark)**
Max Ventilla and Damon Horowitz built a question-answering service where customers thought they were interacting with AI — but humans were doing all the routing behind the scenes. This let them test whether customers would use the product before investing in the hard AI problem. Aardvark eventually sold to Google for $50M.

## Quality and the MVP

The right quality principle for startups: **if we don't know who the customer is, we don't know what quality is.** A low-quality MVP that reaches real customers and generates real feedback is infinitely more valuable than a polished product built on speculation. IMVU's "stationary avatar" was ridiculed internally — customers called it "more advanced than The Sims."

## Speed Bumps

Common objections to building MVPs:
- **Legal/patent risks**: Real in some industries; weigh against learning benefits.
- **Competitor fears**: Irrelevant. If a competitor can outexecute you once they know the idea, you're already in trouble. The only way to win is to learn faster.
- **Brand risk**: Launch under a different brand name. Obscurity is an advantage.

## Key Takeaway

> "The lesson of the MVP is that any additional work beyond what was required to start learning is waste, no matter how important it might have seemed at the time."

Remove any feature, process, or effort that does not directly contribute to the learning you seek.
