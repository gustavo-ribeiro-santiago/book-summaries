# Chapter 5: Leap

## Strategy Is Built on Assumptions

Every business plan rests on a set of assumptions — and hidden among the mundane, well-established facts are a handful of **leaps of faith**: assumptions so critical that the entire venture depends on them being true. If they are correct, a huge opportunity awaits. If false, the startup risks total failure.

Most leaps of faith are disguised as analogies: "Just as X worked in market Y, our technology will work in market Z." These analogies make businesses seem less risky to investors, but they obscure the true test that needs to happen.

## Facebook's Two Leaps of Faith

When Facebook raised early capital with 150,000 users and minimal revenue, skeptics called it a return to dot-com excess. But two data points validated the key leaps:
1. **Value hypothesis**: More than half of users returned *every single day* — exceptional engagement proving real value.
2. **Growth hypothesis**: Facebook spread to nearly three-quarters of Harvard in one month with zero marketing spend, proving viral growth was real.

## Analogs and Antilogs

Venture capitalist Randy Komisar's framework for grounding strategy:
- **Analogs**: Previous companies that validated key assumptions (Sony's Walkman proved people would listen to music in public with earphones, so Jobs didn't need to test that for the iPod).
- **Antilogs**: Companies whose failure identified assumptions that *do* need testing (Napster proved people would download music for free, meaning Jobs had to solve the payment problem).

The best strategies identify which assumptions are proven by analogs and which remain genuine leaps of faith requiring testing.

## Genchi Gembutsu: Go and See

The Toyota principle of *genchi gembutsu* ("go and see for yourself") is one of the most important in the Lean Startup. Startups must get out of the building and talk to real customers to understand whether their leaps of faith reflect reality.

Toyota's Yuji Yokoya drove more than 53,000 miles across North America to understand what minivan buyers actually wanted — discovering insights (like "kids rule the back two-thirds of the vehicle") that no desk research would have surfaced.

## The Customer Archetype

Early customer contact doesn't aim for definitive answers — it aims to build a **customer archetype**: a humanized picture of the target customer and their real problems. This guides product development decisions without locking in assumptions that haven't been tested.

## Analysis Paralysis vs. Just Do It

Two equally dangerous failure modes:
- **Just do it**: Skip analysis entirely, build immediately, and delude yourself that a few customer conversations validate the whole direction.
- **Analysis paralysis**: Endlessly refine plans without testing them, substituting whiteboard strategy for real data.

The answer to both lies in the next chapter: the minimum viable product.

## Key Takeaway

> "For startups, the role of strategy is to help figure out the right questions to ask."

Strategy is not a plan to execute — it is a map of assumptions to test. Getting out of the building to test the most critical leap-of-faith assumptions is the most valuable early activity a startup can do.
