# Chapter 12: Innovate

## Large Companies Can Sustain Innovation

The conventional wisdom — that large companies inevitably lose their capacity for innovation — is wrong. The challenge is structural, not inherent. With the right organization and management philosophy, even large companies can simultaneously manage existing businesses and create genuinely disruptive new ones.

## Three Requirements for Innovation Teams

Whether inside a startup or a large corporation, innovation teams need three things:

1. **Scarce but secure resources**: Too much budget is as harmful as too little. Startup teams need less capital overall, but that capital must be *absolutely protected* from mid-course budget cuts or reallocation.
2. **Independent authority**: Full autonomy to develop, test, and ship products within their mandate — without layers of approval that slow the Build-Measure-Learn loop. Cross-functional from the start.
3. **Personal stake in the outcome**: Not necessarily financial. Credit, recognition, and ownership of the outcome — the knowledge that "my name is on the door" — are powerful motivators.

## The Innovation Sandbox

To enable experimentation without threatening the parent organization, Ries recommends an **innovation sandbox**: a defined space where autonomous teams can run experiments freely, subject to clear constraints:

- Experiments can affect only sandboxed parts of the product or specific customer segments.
- One team sees each experiment through end to end.
- Experiments have a defined time limit and maximum customer impact.
- Every experiment is evaluated using the same **five to ten actionable metrics**.
- Teams must monitor for catastrophic outcomes and abort if needed.

The sandbox protects both the startup team *and* the parent organization. Unlike secret skunkworks labs (which breed paranoia and are rarely sustainable), the sandbox is transparent — everyone can see what's being tested, and everyone learns innovation accounting in the process.

## Managing the Portfolio

Companies must manage four types of work simultaneously:
1. **R&D / exploration**: Early-stage experimentation in the innovation sandbox.
2. **Growth and scaling**: Taking a proven innovation mainstream.
3. **Optimization**: Incremental improvements, operational excellence, cost reduction.
4. **Legacy / infrastructure**: Mission-critical products that must keep running.

The mistake most companies make is promoting the innovators *with* their products — so the person who was great at discovery ends up managing an optimization team. The solution: **let products move between teams, not people**. Employees can choose to go with the product or stay and start something new.

## Entrepreneur as Job Title

Innovation should be a viable career path inside large organizations. Managers who lead teams through the Lean Startup methodology deserve a career track — not just a temporary role before they're absorbed into functional departments.

## Becoming the Status Quo

One of the hardest moments in an innovator's career: when their radical ideas become *the establishment*. New employees must be trained in the Lean Startup approach. New challengers will propose returning to waterfall, using more or less QA, ignoring data. Every suggestion deserves the same rigorous evaluation that built the system in the first place — not dogmatic defense of the status quo, but experimental validation.

## Key Takeaway

> "Rather than copy what others have done, techniques such as the Five Whys allow you to build something that is perfectly suited to your company."

The Lean Startup is a framework, not a blueprint. The goal is an organization capable of continuous innovation — one that can cultivate new growth as each current engine eventually runs out.
