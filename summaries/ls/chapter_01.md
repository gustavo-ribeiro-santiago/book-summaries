# Chapter 1: Start

## Entrepreneurial Management

Startups require management — but not the traditional kind. Traditional management was built for stable, predictable environments. Startups operate under extreme uncertainty: they don't yet know who their customer is or what their product should be. Applying old-school management to a startup leads to either bureaucracy or chaos.

Ries introduces the **Lean Startup** as a framework that borrows from lean manufacturing (Toyota Production System), which emphasized small batches, just-in-time production, eliminating waste, and respecting the creativity of workers. The key adaptation: instead of measuring productivity by physical goods produced, startups measure it by **validated learning**.

## The Engine of Growth Metaphor

Ries uses Henry Ford's car as a metaphor for a startup:

- **The engine** represents the startup's internal growth loop — every product improvement, feature, and marketing experiment attempts to tune this engine.
- **The steering wheel** represents the Build-Measure-Learn feedback loop — the constant course corrections that keep the startup pointed toward its vision.

A rocket ship must be perfectly calibrated at launch; a car is steered continuously. Most startup business plans look like rocket ships — they assume everything can be predicted in advance. The Lean Startup teaches founders to drive instead.

## Vision, Strategy, Product

Ries distinguishes three levels:

- **Vision**: the startup's true north — rarely changes.
- **Strategy**: the business model, product roadmap, customer hypothesis — pivots when evidence demands it.
- **Product**: the end result of the strategy — constantly optimized ("tuning the engine").

## The Build-Measure-Learn Loop

The fundamental activity of a startup is:
1. **Build** a minimum viable product to test assumptions.
2. **Measure** how customers actually respond.
3. **Learn** whether to pivot (change direction) or persevere.

The goal is to accelerate through this loop as fast as possible, minimizing the wasted effort that comes from building something nobody wants.

## Key Takeaway

> "The goal of a startup is to figure out the right thing to build — the thing customers want and will pay for — as quickly as possible."

Speed of learning, not speed of building, is the defining competitive advantage of a Lean Startup.
