# Chapter 16: The Capitalist Creed

## Overview
The modern economy is defined by one word: **growth**. For most of history per capita production stayed flat — global wealth grew only because of more land and more people. Since 1500, global production has gone from $250 billion to $60 trillion, while per capita output rose from $550 to $8,800 a year. What unlocked this? **Credit** — the willingness to bet on a richer future.

## The Bakery Example
Harari illustrates with a simple story:
- A contractor (Stone) deposits $1M in cash at Greedy's bank.
- A chef (McDoughnut) borrows $1M to build a bakery, paying Stone with that loan.
- Stone deposits the money — and now his bank balance shows $2M, even though the bank still holds only $1M in cash.
- Two months later, McDoughnut borrows another $1M, transfers it to Stone. Stone's balance: $3M. Actual cash in bank: still $1M.

US banking law lets banks loan **$10 for every $1 they actually hold**. About 90% of all the money in our bank accounts isn't backed by physical currency. If everyone tried to withdraw at once, every bank in the world would collapse.

This is not fraud. It is **trust in the future**. The gap between bank balances and cash is filled by Mrs. McDoughnut's not-yet-built bakery — a future that everyone bets will exist.

## The Premodern Trap
Before modern times, credit existed but was scarce, short-term, and high-interest. The reason was philosophical, not technical: **people believed the pie was fixed.** Wealth was zero-sum: my gain was your loss. Past generations were assumed to have been better off, the future to be worse or the same. This is why Jesus said it was easier for a camel to pass through the eye of a needle than a rich man to enter heaven — if the pie is fixed, getting rich means stealing from others.

With this worldview, lending was a poor bet. Without lending, new ventures couldn't be financed. Without new ventures, the economy stayed flat. Without growth, the assumption of stagnation was confirmed — a self-fulfilling prophecy.

## The Idea of Progress
The Scientific Revolution broke the prophecy by introducing the idea of **progress**: investment in research could produce real new wealth. Discoveries could expand the pie. New trade routes don't ruin old ones; new products don't displace old products — they create new tastes. **I can become rich without you becoming poor.**

Once people believed the pie could grow, they began to extend credit. Credit financed new ventures. New ventures produced growth. Growth reinforced trust. More trust meant more credit. The cycle has now been running for 500 years.

## Adam Smith's Revolution
In 1776, Adam Smith published *The Wealth of Nations*, arguing that when a businessman profits, he reinvests in more employees and production, expanding the whole pie. The selfish pursuit of private profit, Smith argued, produces collective wealth. **Egoism is altruism.** Greed is good. The rich are not the despoilers of society but its benefactors — *as long as they reinvest their profits in productive enterprise*.

This is the difference between **capital** and **wealth**:
- Capital = money invested in production (a factory, a research lab, a stock holding).
- Wealth = money buried, hoarded, or spent on non-productive consumption (Scrooge's chest, a pharaoh's pyramid, a pirate's treasure).

The medieval nobility had wealth and spent it on tournaments, banquets, and cathedrals. The modern CEO has capital and reinvests it. The new ethic: **"The profits of production must be reinvested in increasing production."**

## Capitalism as Religion
Capitalism began as economic theory but became **a comprehensive ethic and worldview** — a religion by Harari's earlier definition. Its central dogma is that economic growth is the supreme good (or at least the precondition for justice, freedom, and happiness). When asked how to bring justice to Zimbabwe or Afghanistan, a capitalist will explain that what's needed first is a thriving middle class — meaning, more growth.

## Capitalism and Science
Modern science is funded almost entirely by capitalist governments and corporations. The first question asked of any research project is, "Will this produce growth?" Conversely, capitalism depends utterly on science — its claim of perpetual growth flies in the face of physical reality unless scientists keep finding new resources, materials, and technologies. The economic crisis since 2008 has been "patched" by central banks printing trillions of dollars, betting that biotechnology and nanotechnology will produce real new value before the bubble pops.

## Columbus Searches for an Investor
European imperialism was financed by credit, not (mainly) by taxation. Columbus was rejected by Portugal, Italy, France, and England before convincing Ferdinand and Isabella of Spain. The Spanish gamble paid off — gold from the Americas, sugar from the Caribbean, silver from Potosí.

The **magic circle of imperial capitalism**:
1. Credit finances new exploration.
2. Exploration produces colonies.
3. Colonies produce profits.
4. Profits build trust.
5. Trust enables more credit.

## The Dutch Empire
The Spanish Empire was financed through royal taxation. Spanish kings frequently defaulted on debts and seized property of foreign merchants. The Dutch, by contrast, were sticklers for repaying loans on time, and their independent courts protected private property even against the government. Capital flowed into Amsterdam.

Within 80 years, the tiny, swampy Netherlands had defeated mighty Spain at sea, built a global empire, and become the richest country in Europe — financed by an enormous network of private investors. The **Dutch East India Company** (VOC, founded 1602) was the world's first joint-stock corporation traded on a stock exchange. At its peak it was a private army-and-navy that conquered Indonesia for its shareholders.

The British East India Company followed suit, conquering most of India for private investors. **For two centuries, vast parts of the world were ruled by joint-stock corporations, not by states.**

## When Capital Becomes Cruel
This system led to enormous human suffering. The Atlantic slave trade — perhaps the largest single human atrocity in history — was a textbook capitalist enterprise. Investors funded slave ships, ships brought slaves from Africa to the Americas, slaves produced sugar in the Caribbean, sugar enriched the investors. Around 10 million Africans were enslaved; about a fifth died in transport. No one in the chain hated Africans personally; the trade was driven by indifference, not malice. **The free market does not protect against indifference.**

## The Cult of the Free Market
The 19th-century capitalist creed claimed that markets work best with minimum interference. Profits should be tax-free, government regulations minimized, courts and police limited to defending property rights. By this logic, neither slavery nor sweatshops were grounds for government intervention — those were private business decisions.

Harari's central critique: **"There is simply no such thing as a market free of all political bias."** Even the most basic markets require property law, contract enforcement, courts, and police — all government institutions. And many of the most catastrophic events in modern history — slavery, the Bengal famines under the East India Company, the Belgian Congo — happened precisely because markets were allowed to operate "freely" without political constraint.

## Key Insight
Capitalism is more than an economic system. It is a religion of perpetual growth, sustained by trust in the future and dependent on continuous scientific discovery. It has produced unprecedented wealth — and unprecedented exploitation. The next chapter examines how it transformed the material world through the Industrial Revolution.
