# Chapter 11: Imperial Visions

## Overview
Empires have been "the world's most common form of political organization for the last 2,500 years." They are not just engines of brutality — they are also the chief mechanism by which small cultures were absorbed into larger ones, producing the cultural unification of humankind. Harari challenges modern anti-imperial reflexes and argues we cannot disown our imperial inheritance because we *are* its products.

## What Is an Empire?
An empire has two defining features:
1. **Rules over many distinct peoples** (twenty or thirty separate cultures), not just two or three.
2. **Flexible, expandable borders** — it can swallow new territories without changing its basic identity.

Empires need not be huge (the Athenian Empire was smaller than modern Greece) or autocratic (the British Empire was a democracy). Origin doesn't matter either: the Habsburg Empire was built through marriages.

## The Numantian Lesson
In 134 BC, the Roman general Scipio Aemilianus besieged the Iberian town of Numantia until its defenders burned the town and killed themselves rather than surrender. Modern Spanish patriots revere the Numantians as ancestors. But there's an irony: those patriots speak **Spanish** (descended from Latin), follow **Roman Catholicism**, use **Roman law**, and know the Numantians' story only because **Roman historians** recorded it. "The victory of Rome over Numantia was so complete that the victors co-opted the very memory of the vanquished."

This is the standard pattern. The Numantians, Etruscans, Samnites, Lusitanians, Gauls, Aramaeans, Phoenicians, Edomites — hundreds of peoples — have vanished completely. Today's "indigenous" survivors (Jews, Armenians, Georgians) are exceptions that prove the rule.

## The Critique of Empire
Two common modern objections to empire:
1. **They don't work.** Historically false. Empires are remarkably stable; conquered peoples have an abysmal record of regaining independence except after external invasion or elite split.
2. **They are evil.** Empires did indeed commit horrors: wars, enslavement, deportation, genocide. The Roman destruction of Caledonia (Scotland) was so brutal that the chieftain Calgacus reportedly said, "To plunder, slaughter, and robbery they give the lying name of empire; they make a desert and call it peace."

But the same imperial structures funded Cicero, Seneca, Augustine, the Taj Mahal, Haydn, and Mozart. Most modern languages, religions, and political systems descend from empires. To reject empire entirely is to reject most of human culture.

## "It's for Your Own Good"
The first known empire (Sargon's Akkad, ~2250 BC) ruled in the name of glory and conquest. But around 550 BC, **Cyrus the Great of Persia** introduced a revolutionary innovation: ruling for the benefit of the ruled. He claimed responsibility for all his subjects, including the Jews — whom he famously allowed to return from Babylonian exile and rebuild their temple, even funding them.

This **inclusive imperial vision** was startling. *Homo sapiens* is naturally xenophobic — the Dinka of Sudan call themselves "people" (others are not people), the Nuer call themselves "original people," the Yupiks call themselves "real people." But from Cyrus onward, empires claimed universal jurisdiction with universal responsibility.

In China, the **Mandate of Heaven** doctrine did something similar: legitimate authority is universal by definition; political fragmentation was seen as chaos and injustice. This is why Chinese unification has reasserted itself again and again after every collapse.

The benevolent imperial vision passed from Cyrus → Alexander → Roman emperors → Muslim caliphs → Indian dynasts → Soviet premiers → American presidents. The Mauryans claimed they were spreading Buddha's teachings; the Spanish, the true faith; the British, liberalism and free trade; the Soviets, the dictatorship of the proletariat; modern Americans, democracy and human rights. The rhetoric varies; the structure is identical.

## When "They" Become "Us"
Empires actively spread common cultures — for administrative convenience (one law, one currency, one language) and for legitimacy (we are bringing civilization). Cultural assimilation was rarely chosen — usually it was painful and traumatic. Conquered peoples lost their gods, languages, and traditions, often without being accepted by the imperial elite even after generations of effort.

An Iberian noble a century after the fall of Numantia might speak fluent Latin, build a Roman villa, and recite Virgil from memory — and still be treated as a semi-barbarian. Mohandas Gandhi, with a London law degree, was thrown off a South African train for sitting first class.

But over enough time, the boundary dissolves. Romans eventually granted citizenship to Iberians, Gauls, Syrians. By the second century AD, emperors with Iberian, Libyan, Syrian, and Arab ancestry ruled Rome. The Arab Empire absorbed Egyptians, Persians, and Berbers, who in turn redefined "Arab" identity. Han Chinese identity now encompasses dozens of formerly distinct peoples — over 90% of China's population sees themselves as Han.

## The New Global Empire
The modern decolonization wave is in some ways the continuation of imperial assimilation. Indians, Africans, Arabs, and Chinese rejected Western *rule* — but adopted Western *ideas* (human rights, self-determination, democracy, capitalism, even nationalism). They learned the colonizers' languages and use them to demand independence. Today there is a single global civilization with regional variations, not separate civilizations in conflict.

The next stage may be a genuinely global empire — though not under any single nation. The challenges of nuclear weapons, climate change, AI, and biotechnology demand global solutions that the nation-state cannot deliver.

## Key Insight
Empires built the modern world. They did so through extraordinary violence and oppression — but also through ideas, infrastructures, and cultural fusions that survived their political collapse. Our languages, religions, legal systems, art, and self-understanding are imperial inheritances. The honest reckoning with empire is not to condemn or celebrate it but to recognize how thoroughly we are its products.
