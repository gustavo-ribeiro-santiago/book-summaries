# Chapter 18: A Permanent Revolution

## Overview
The Industrial Revolution did far more than transform energy and materials — it remade society itself. Three deep shifts define modern life: (1) the **disappearance of the natural rhythm of time** in favor of industrial schedules; (2) the **collapse of family and community**, replaced by state and market; and (3) the paradox that an era of constant revolution has nonetheless been **the most peaceful in human history**.

## Humans Take Over the Planet
A startling biomass calculation: the world's 7 billion humans together weigh about 300 million tons. Our domesticated animals (cattle, pigs, sheep, chickens) weigh 700 million tons. **All remaining wild large mammals combined weigh less than 100 million tons.** There are 1.5 billion cattle vs. 80,000 giraffes; 400 million dogs vs. 200,000 wolves; billions of humans vs. 250,000 chimpanzees. Children's books still show giraffes and lions, but the real world is mostly humans, livestock, and concrete.

The danger ahead isn't running out of resources (energy and materials keep expanding) but **ecological collapse** — the planet becoming less hospitable to our own species through climate change, pollution, and biodiversity loss.

## Modern Time
Traditional agricultural life ran on **natural time** — the sun's arc, the seasons, harvest cycles. People rarely knew the year, never knew the hour. A medieval peasant asked "what year is it?" would be as bewildered as if asked "what's your IP address?"

Industry shattered this. Assembly lines require **uniform precision**: if one worker is late, every machine stalls. Schools, hospitals, government offices, pubs — all eventually conformed to the factory schedule.

The crucial enabler was **public transportation**. By the early 1800s, each British town had its own local time — London was 20 minutes ahead of Liverpool, 10 minutes ahead of Canterbury. With horse carriages, no one cared. With trains, it became chaos. In 1847 British train companies synchronized all timetables to Greenwich. In 1880 Parliament made it law: **for the first time in history, an entire country lived by a single artificial clock.**

Today, the typical household has more clocks than an entire medieval country. You have to make a conscious effort *not* to know the time.

## The Collapse of Family and Community
This is, in Harari's view, the single deepest social revolution in human history. For over a million years, humans lived in small kin-based communities. Family and local community were:

- Welfare system
- Health system
- Education system
- Construction industry
- Trade union
- Pension fund
- Insurance company
- Bank
- Police force

When you got sick, your family cared for you. When you got old, your children supported you. When conflicts arose, kin and community mediated. Less than 10% of needed goods were bought in markets. Most kings stayed out of daily life — even Ottoman sultans allowed family vendettas to substitute for police courts.

This was not a romantic paradise. Families and communities could be brutally oppressive. But there was nowhere else to go. A person who lost their family in 1750 was "as good as dead."

## The Deal: "Become Individuals"
In just two centuries, states and markets dismantled this ancient structure. The new deal they offered was irresistible:

> "Become individuals. Marry whomever you desire, without asking permission from your parents. Take whatever job suits you. Live wherever you wish. You no longer depend on your family or community. We — the state and the market — will take care of you instead. We will provide food, shelter, education, healthcare, welfare, jobs, pensions, insurance, and protection."

Romantic novels portray the individual as fighting against state and market, but this is exactly backwards. **The state and the market are the parents of the modern individual.** Schools, hospitals, banks, insurance companies, police forces, courts, mortgage lenders — the entire infrastructure of "individual" life is built by the state and the market.

## Imagined Communities
The family didn't vanish entirely — it kept its emotional functions (intimacy, child-rearing). The local community has been replaced by **imagined communities**: groups of millions of strangers who imagine they share an identity. The two major types are:

1. **The Nation** (the state's imagined community). German nationalism asks 80 million strangers to imagine themselves as one family. Most modern nations were created within the last two centuries — Syria, Lebanon, Iraq, and Jordan were drawn on maps by British and French diplomats in 1918.

2. **The Consumer Tribe** (the market's imagined community). Madonna fans, Manchester United fans, vegetarians, environmentalists — defined not by birth or geography but by what they buy and consume.

## Permanent Revolution
Traditionally, "social order" meant stability — things were the way they had always been. **Today the social order is permanently in flux.** Every year is revolutionary. The Internet went from nothing to indispensable in 20 years. Even the most conservative politicians promise "reform"; no one promises to keep things as they are.

## Peace in Our Time
The most counterintuitive consequence: the era of permanent revolution has also been **the most peaceful in human history**. Despite WWI, WWII, the Holocaust, and dozens of regional wars, mass statistics tell a clearer story:

- In 2000, wars killed 310,000 people; violent crime killed 520,000. Together: **1.5% of all deaths**.
- More people died in car accidents (2.25% of deaths) than by violence.
- In 2002, after 9/11, **more people committed suicide (873,000) than were killed by terrorists, soldiers, and criminals combined (741,000).**

Compare with the past:
- Simple farming societies: 15% of deaths from violence (25% of male deaths).
- Medieval Europe: 20–40 murders per 100,000 people per year.
- Modern Europe: 1 murder per 100,000 per year. Global average: 9.
- Amazon tribes living without states: 25–50% of men die in violence.

**The decline of violence is largely due to the rise of the state.** Strong states monopolize violence, taking it away from local feuds.

## Imperial Retirement and the Pax Atomica
Since 1945, international violence has fallen to historic lows. The European empires gave up their colonies peacefully (something unprecedented for collapsing empires). Wars of conquest have almost ceased. France has not invaded Italy in seven decades, despite plenty of opportunity. Nuclear weapons make great-power war catastrophically irrational. Economic interconnection makes conquest unprofitable.

This may be temporary. The Pax Atomica could collapse. But for the last 70 years, we have lived in the most peaceful epoch our species has ever known.

## Key Insight
Modernity is paradox. We are more powerful and more peaceful than any generation in history — yet also more alienated, more anxious, more individually adrift. We have replaced the suffocating intimacy of family and village with the impersonal scale of nation and market. Whether the trade was worth it is the question of the final chapter.
