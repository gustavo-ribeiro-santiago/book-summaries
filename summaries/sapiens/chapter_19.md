# Chapter 19: And They Lived Happily Ever After

## Overview
After 500 years of revolutions in science, industry, economics, and society, humanity has gained unimaginable power and wealth. **But are we happier than our ancestors?** Historians have studied almost everything except this — and the question turns out to be far more complex than any of our political ideologies admit.

## Three Standard Stories
- **The progressive story**: Power increases, capabilities expand, happiness must follow. We are happier than our medieval ancestors; they were happier than Stone Age foragers.
- **The romantic story**: Power corrupts; modern civilization separates us from our true nature; every invention puts another mile between us and Eden.
- **The middle path**: Until 1500, progress mostly made things worse; since then it has gradually improved life through medicine, declining violence, and (recently) the end of mass famine.

Harari finds all three inadequate. The progressive story ignores the cost paid by peasants, slaves, conquered peoples, and animals. The romantic story ignores that child mortality fell from 33% to 5%. The middle path makes its case from a tiny, recent sample (mostly post-1850, mostly Western) — and ignores the ecological catastrophe building beneath us.

## Measuring Happiness
Modern psychology defines happiness as **"subjective well-being"** — what a person feels about their own life. It's measured by questionnaires asking people to rate statements like "I am pleased with my life."

Findings:

### Money
Money brings happiness — **but only up to a point.** A single mother going from $12,000 to $500,000/year experiences a major long-term boost. A CEO going from $250,000 to $500,000 gets a few weeks of excitement, then her happiness returns to baseline. Above subsistence, more money rapidly stops mattering.

### Illness
Illness depresses happiness in the short term. But unless the illness is degenerative or constantly painful, people **adapt**. Diabetics rate their happiness as highly as healthy people after they adjust. People who lose limbs in accidents recover to nearly their pre-accident happiness within two years.

### Family and Community
These matter more than money or health. People in strong families and tight communities are significantly happier — regardless of wealth. **Marriage is especially powerful.** An impecunious invalid in a loving family may well be happier than an alienated billionaire.

This is troubling for modern society: we have replaced strong families and communities with state and market, which provide goods but not belonging.

## The Real Secret: Expectations
The most important finding is that happiness depends not on what you have but on **the gap between what you have and what you expect**. If you want a bullock-cart and get one, you're happy. If you want a Ferrari and get a Fiat, you're miserable — even though the Fiat is better than any bullock-cart.

This explains why lottery winners and accident victims end up similarly happy: the lottery winner's expectations balloon to match her wealth, the accident victim's expectations shrink to match her loss.

This is also why **modernity is not making us happier**. Medical advances arrived alongside ever-rising expectations of comfort. Mass media and advertising relentlessly push our expectations of beauty, wealth, and excitement upward. A medieval peasant did not feel ugly because he compared himself to fifty villagers, half of them old and toothless. A modern teenager compares herself to airbrushed supermodels on Instagram.

Third World discontent, Harari argues, is fueled not only by poverty but by **exposure to First World standards**. Egyptians under Mubarak ate better than under any pharaoh — and rose up because they were measuring themselves against Americans.

## Chemical Happiness
Biologists offer a more radical answer: happiness is **biochemistry**. Serotonin, dopamine, oxytocin. Winning the lottery doesn't make you happy — the chemical rush in response to winning the lottery does. And that rush is short-lived because evolution designed us that way: permanent happiness would have killed off the genetic line of people too content to seek food or sex.

The body has a "happiness thermostat." Some people are born with the dial set to 8; others to 5. External events shift you up or down by a point or two, but the thermostat pulls you back. **Two centuries of dramatic progress have left the thermostat unchanged.**

If this is right, history doesn't matter for happiness. The French Revolution executed the king and abolished feudalism, but average French serotonin levels didn't change. The Industrial Revolution gave us electricity and antibiotics, but the cheerful biochemistry types were always happy and the gloomy ones were always miserable.

The implication is dystopian: the only path to real happiness is **biochemical manipulation** — Prozac, soma, neural implants. Aldous Huxley's *Brave New World* (1932) imagined a society where everyone takes a daily dose of "soma" and is supremely content. We are uneasy with this vision, but it's hard to say exactly why if happiness is just chemistry.

## The Meaning of Life
Beyond pleasure, there is **meaning**. People who feel their lives are significant tolerate enormous suffering — Christian martyrs, scientific researchers, parents raising children. People who feel meaningless suffer despite comfort. But meaning, like everything else Harari has examined, is a **fiction we choose to believe**.

A mother raising children sees her life as meaningful — children are the future, raising them is sacred. From a less invested view, she's just spreading genes and exhausting herself. A pious Muslim sees his life as meaningful — submitting to Allah is the highest purpose. A secular humanist sees his life as meaningful — contributing to human progress. None of these meanings is objectively true. **All meanings are imagined.**

So the question of whether we are happier than our ancestors reduces to: **are our delusions more satisfying than theirs?** The medieval Christian believed in heaven; the modern consumer believes in retirement. Neither is real outside the human mind. Both can make life feel worth living.

## The Buddhist View
A third tradition — Buddhism — offers a different answer. Suffering arises not from external conditions but from **craving**. Whatever you have, you want more. Whatever you lack, you suffer. The path is not to chase pleasure (which always fades) but to **let go of craving altogether**.

In Buddhist terms, happiness is not a feeling but a state — the cessation of the endless cycle of want. From this view, neither modernity's pleasures nor antiquity's hardships matter much. What matters is the inner relationship to experience.

This may be why so many privileged moderns turn to meditation, mindfulness, and Buddhism: with all the external problems solved, the internal restlessness remains.

## Key Insight
We do not know whether we are happier than our ancestors. **We do not know how to measure happiness across time.** We have built a civilization of unprecedented power and material wealth on the assumption that more is better — yet our biochemistry, our expectations, our meaning-making, and our craving may not have changed at all.

The book's most uncomfortable suggestion is that history has spent 70,000 years arranging the external world, while the internal world — the one that actually feels — has gone unexamined.
