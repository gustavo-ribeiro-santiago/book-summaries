# Chapter 15: The Marriage of Science and Empire

## Overview
Modern science and modern imperialism grew up together. Each empowered the other. Captain James Cook's voyages illustrate the dynamic: scientific expeditions that just happened to be military operations, or military operations that just happened to be scientific. The two were inseparable.

## Cook's Voyage: Science, Empire, and Death
In 1768, the Royal Society sent astronomer Charles Green to Tahiti to observe the 1769 transit of Venus — a measurement that would let scientists calculate the Earth-Sun distance. The expedition included botanists, artists, eight scientists in all, and was commanded by the experienced navigator (and naval officer) **James Cook**, equipped with 85 well-armed marines and a hold full of cannon.

The mission produced extraordinary results:
- Accurate measurement of the solar system's scale.
- A cure for scurvy. Cook proved Dr. James Lind's 1747 finding — that citrus fruits cured the disease that had killed 2 million sailors over three centuries. Cook fed his crew sauerkraut and fresh produce; he lost no one to scurvy.
- Vast new geographic, botanical, zoological, and anthropological data.

It also produced **genocide**. Cook claimed Australia for Britain. The Aborigines, who had lived in Australia for over 40,000 years, lost up to 90% of their population in the century that followed. The Tasmanians — every man, woman, and child — were systematically hunted to extinction by European settlers, the last "evangelized" survivors placed in a missionary camp where they refused to learn, stopped having children, and chose death. The corpses were collected by anthropologists, measured, dissected, and displayed in museums. Truganini, the last Tasmanian, had her skeleton returned for burial only in 1976; her skin and hair samples were held by the Royal College of Surgeons until 2002.

**Was Cook's ship a scientific expedition with a military escort, or a military expedition with scientists tagging along?** Both. Inseparably.

## Why Europe?
A central puzzle of modern history: how did Europe — a poor, peripheral fringe of Eurasia — come to dominate the world? In 1750, Asia produced 80% of global wealth. China and India alone produced two-thirds. Europe was an economic dwarf. By 1900, Europe controlled most of the planet. By 1950, Europe + the U.S. were over half of global production while China's share had collapsed to 5%.

The standard answer — superior European technology — fails for the early period. In 1770, when Cook reached Australia, Europeans had no real technological edge over Chinese, Ottomans, or Mughals. The Chinese under Admiral **Zheng He** had launched seven enormous fleets between 1405 and 1433 — armadas of 300 ships and 30,000 men, vastly larger than anything Columbus would ever command. Zheng visited Indonesia, Sri Lanka, India, Arabia, East Africa.

But then, in the 1430s, China's emperor canceled the program. The fleet was dismantled. Knowledge was lost. Chinese rulers refocused on the Middle Kingdom's immediate borders. **Zheng explored. He did not conquer.**

The Chinese, Persians, and Ottomans could have built railroads, steam engines, and machine guns. The technology was relatively simple and could be bought or copied. What they couldn't copy quickly were the **values, myths, institutions, and habits of mind** that made Europe an explore-and-conquer culture.

## The Mentality of Conquest
European empires were unlike any in history. Earlier conquerors — Romans, Mongols, Arabs, Aztecs — sought wealth and power. They assumed they already understood the world. European imperialists did something new: they set out to **discover and conquer** simultaneously. The scientist and the conqueror shared the same mindset:

- "I don't know what's out there."
- "I must go and find out."
- "What I learn will make me master of the world."

When Napoleon invaded Egypt in 1798, he brought 165 scholars. They founded Egyptology and revolutionized the study of religion, linguistics, and botany. In 1831 the Royal Navy sent HMS *Beagle* to map South America for military purposes — and accepted a 22-year-old Cambridge graduate as the geologist: **Charles Darwin**. The theory of evolution was born from imperial cartography.

## Empty Maps
The most striking sign of the new mentality: **maps with blank spaces**. Earlier world maps from every culture were "complete" — unfamiliar areas were filled with imaginary monsters or simply ignored. From the 15th century, European maps began showing **what they did not know** as empty space. This was a psychological and ideological breakthrough.

Columbus in 1492 was still a medieval man — he refused to admit that he had found a new continent unknown to Scripture, insisting until his death that he'd reached India. **Amerigo Vespucci** was the first modern man: he said, "We don't know," and the new continent was named "America" in his honor.

The blank spaces drew Europeans like magnets. Within a few generations, expeditions had circumnavigated Africa, crossed the Pacific, and colonized the Americas. The world transformed from a series of separate histories into a single global system.

## The Apollo Story
A telling apocryphal story: as the Apollo 11 astronauts trained in the American desert, they met an old Native American man. He asked them to memorize a message for the moon spirits in his tribal language. Back at base, they had it translated. It said: **"Don't believe a single word these people are telling you. They have come to steal your lands."**

The Apollo missions are the direct descendants of Cook's voyage. The combination of science and conquest is now reaching into the cosmos.

## Key Insight
Modern science is not a culturally neutral discovery. It emerged from — and remained entangled with — European imperialism. The same "willingness to admit ignorance" that drove Newton and Darwin also drove the conquest of Australia, the destruction of Tasmania, and the European colonization of every habitable continent. Science and empire shared the same engine: **curiosity about the unknown combined with the will to master it**.
