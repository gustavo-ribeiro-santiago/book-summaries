# Chapter 12: The Law of Religion

## Overview
Alongside money and empire, **religion** is the third great unifier of humankind. Religion's job is not just spiritual — it gives "superhuman legitimacy" to the fragile imagined orders that hold large societies together. Without it, the laws of Hammurabi would have been just opinions; with it, they became eternal truths handed down by gods.

## What Counts as Religion?
Harari defines religion as **"a system of human norms and values founded on a belief in a superhuman order."** Two criteria:
1. A **superhuman order** that humans did not invent and cannot change. (Football has rules, but FIFA can change them — so football isn't a religion.)
2. **Binding norms and values** derived from that order. (Belief in fairies isn't a religion unless it dictates how to live.)

To become a unifier of humankind, a religion needs two more qualities:
- **Universal** — its truths apply everywhere.
- **Missionary** — it actively spreads.

Most ancient religions were neither. Local animist and polytheist religions had no interest in converting outsiders. Universal missionary religions first appeared in the first millennium BC — and changed history.

## From Animism to Polytheism
The Agricultural Revolution triggered a religious revolution. Foragers were animists, treating plants and animals as **equal partners** in a spiritual community. Farmers, who *owned* their plants and animals, couldn't sustain that egalitarianism. Animals and plants became property.

But farmers also could not fully control their crops or flocks — fertility, weather, and disease were beyond them. So **gods** rose to fill the gap. Gods mediated between humans and the now-mute animals and plants. Most early religion was contractual: humans offered sacrifices, gods provided rain, fertility, and victory.

As kingdoms and trade networks grew, local spirits gave way to **polytheism** — a pantheon of gods whose powers transcended any one valley.

## The Logic (and Tolerance) of Polytheism
A common misunderstanding: polytheists believe in many independent gods. In fact, most polytheists also recognized a single supreme power (Fate, Atman, Olodumare) behind all the lesser gods — but considered it **disinterested**, beyond mundane concerns. The lesser gods, with their partial powers, biases, and interests, were the ones you bargained with.

This makes polytheism **inherently tolerant**. There's no problem accepting other peoples' gods alongside your own. The Romans absorbed Cybele and Isis. The Aztecs let conquered peoples keep their gods alongside Huitzilopochtli. Empires conquered, but they did not convert.

The one exception: the Romans persecuted Christians not because of their beliefs but because Christians refused to participate in civic rituals — refused to acknowledge other gods at all. In three centuries, polytheistic Rome killed perhaps a few thousand Christians. Over the next 1,500 years, **Christians killed millions of fellow Christians** in disputes over doctrine. On the St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre alone (1572), French Catholics killed 5,000–10,000 Protestants in 24 hours — more than Rome killed in 300 years.

## Monotheism
Eventually, polytheists became so attached to particular gods that they began believing theirs was *the only* god — combining the supreme power's universal authority with a partial god's personal interest. This was monotheism.

- **Akhenaten** (~1350 BC, Egypt): the first known monotheist; his reform failed and died with him.
- **Judaism**: monotheist but local — God's interest is the Jewish people only.
- **Christianity** (via Paul of Tarsus): monotheism + universalism + missionary zeal. Took over the Roman Empire in one of history's strangest twists.
- **Islam** (7th century): the same model, even more rapidly successful.

Monotheists tend to be **more fanatical and missionary than polytheists**, because if your god is the only god, all other religions must be false and must be eliminated.

## Hidden Polytheism Inside Monotheism
In practice, monotheism never replaced polytheism — it absorbed it. Christianity has its **pantheon of saints**: St. George for England, St. Martin for France, St. Florian for chimney sweeps, St. Apollonia for toothaches. Many of these saints were old pagan gods rebranded (St. Brigit was once the Celtic goddess Brigid).

## Dualism
**Dualistic religions** (Zoroastrianism, Manichaeanism, Gnosticism) posit two opposing powers — Good and Evil — locked in cosmic war. Dualism has a powerful advantage: it solves the **Problem of Evil**. Why do bad things happen to good people? Because an independent evil force does them.

But dualism faces its own **Problem of Order**: who makes the laws under which Good and Evil fight? Monotheism explains order but struggles with evil; dualism explains evil but struggles with order. Most monotheistic religions quietly smuggle dualism back in — through Satan, the Devil, demons, the war for human souls. None of this is in the Old Testament; all of it comes from Zoroastrian and Gnostic sources.

What we call "monotheism" today is actually a kaleidoscope of monotheist, dualist, polytheist, and animist elements jumbled together. The technical term is **syncretism**.

## The Natural-Law Religions
Beginning in the first millennium BC, a different kind of religion emerged that **dispenses with gods entirely**: Buddhism, Jainism, Daoism, Confucianism, Stoicism, Cynicism, Epicureanism. These hold that the superhuman order is **natural law**, not divine will. Gods, if they exist, are subject to those laws like everything else.

Buddhism's central insight: **suffering arises from craving.** The universe has no plan and no creator — there's just the laws of cause and effect, and the practice of letting go of desire.

## Modern Religions Without Gods
The natural-law category extends to modern ideologies — **liberalism, communism, capitalism, nationalism, Nazism**. These are religions by Harari's definition: each holds a superhuman order (laws of history, of markets, of biology, of nations) and derives binding norms from it. Calling them religions makes Westerners uncomfortable, but the structure is the same: an unchallengeable framework, a vision of salvation, a moral code, missionary zeal.

## Key Insight
Religion is not opposed to reason or progress — it is the **glue that makes large-scale cooperation possible** by placing fundamental laws beyond human dispute. Modern people who think they've outgrown religion are often just adherents of natural-law religions instead — and these have proven every bit as violent and missionary as the older theistic kind.
