# Chapter 2: The Tree of Knowledge

## Overview
Around 70,000 years ago, *Homo sapiens* began doing things no other species had ever done: inventing boats, making art, conducting trade, practicing religion, and driving every other human species to extinction. Harari calls this transformation the **Cognitive Revolution** — a sudden change in how Sapiens thought and communicated, likely caused by a genetic mutation that rewired the brain.

## What Made Sapiens Language Different
Many animals have language. Bees signal food locations; monkeys have different calls for "eagle" vs. "lion." Parrots can mimic any sound. What made Sapiens language unique was not its sounds or vocabulary but **what it could describe**.

Two strengths emerged:
- **Flexibility**: Combining limited sounds into infinite meaningful sentences (where the lion is, what trail to take, what to do).
- **Gossip**: Tracking the trustworthiness, relationships, and reputations of dozens of people — essential for cooperation in large groups. Most human communication, even today, is gossip.

But the single most revolutionary feature of Sapiens language is something neither of those theories captures: **the ability to talk about things that don't exist**. Legends, myths, gods, religions, nations, money, corporations — none of these have physical reality. Sapiens are the only animal that can describe and believe in collective fictions.

## The Legend of Peugeot
The chapter's central example: Peugeot SA, a French automaker employing 200,000 people, exists not as a building or a fleet of cars but as a **legal fiction** — a story everyone agrees to believe. If every Peugeot car were destroyed, the company would still exist. If every employee died, it could hire new ones. Peugeot exists because lawyers, courts, and citizens all behave as if it does.

This is no different from how priests "create" the body of Christ through ritual, or how Hammurabi's gods "ordained" his code of laws. The mechanism in both cases is the same: telling stories convincing enough that millions of strangers behave as if the story is true.

## The Power of Imagined Realities
This ability to create shared fictions allowed Sapiens to break through the cognitive ceiling that limits other primates. The natural group size for cooperation based on personal acquaintance is about 150 people (this is roughly the size of a chimp troop, a small village, or an effective military company). Beyond that, gossip and personal trust break down.

**Shared myths solved this problem.** Two Catholics who have never met can cooperate because both believe in Christ's sacrifice. Two lawyers who have never met can collaborate because both believe in the law. Two soldiers can fight together because both believe in their nation. Without shared fictions, no kingdom, religion, corporation, or trade network larger than 150 people could exist.

## Bypassing the Genome
Most animals' social behavior is encoded in DNA — change requires genetic mutation, which takes millennia. Sapiens, by contrast, can change their behavior overnight by **changing their stories**. In 1789, the French population shifted from believing in divine right of kings to believing in popular sovereignty, with no genetic change required. A Berliner born in 1900 lived through five radically different political systems with the same DNA.

This is why Sapiens outpaced Neanderthals: in a one-on-one fight, the Neanderthal won; but in groups of hundreds cooperating around shared myths, Sapiens were unstoppable.

## Key Insight
The Cognitive Revolution is where **history declares its independence from biology**. To understand human events from this point forward, biology alone is insufficient. We must study the imagined realities — the religions, ideologies, nations, and economic systems — that organize Sapiens cooperation. The ability to invent and believe in shared fictions is the single most important fact about our species.
