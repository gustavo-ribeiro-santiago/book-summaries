# Chapter 9: The Arrow of History

## Overview
History has a direction. Despite endless local variation and apparent chaos, the long arc bends toward **unity** — fewer, larger, more interconnected human cultures. Harari opens Part Three (*The Unification of Humankind*) by examining how cultures change and why globalization was inevitable.

## Culture Is Internal Contradiction
Older anthropologists treated cultures as static, harmonious wholes — "Samoan culture" or "Tasmanian culture" as eternal essences. Today we know the opposite: cultures are dynamic, and they change not just from external pressure but from their own internal contradictions.

Two examples:
- **Medieval Europe** held both Christianity (humility, turn the other cheek) and chivalry (honor, violence, glory). The contradiction was never resolved, but trying to resolve it produced the Crusades, the Templars, and the legends of King Arthur.
- **Modern liberalism** holds both *equality* and *individual freedom*. The two contradict: equality requires constraining freedom, freedom produces inequality. All political history since the French Revolution is a series of attempts to balance them. American Democrats and Republicans, Dickensian capitalism and Soviet communism — all are variations on this irresolvable tension.

**Cognitive dissonance is not a defect of human culture. It is its engine.** "Consistency is the playground of dull minds."

## The Direction of History
Take a long enough view — millennia, not centuries — and the pattern is unmistakable. Around 10,000 BC, Earth held thousands of separate human worlds. By 2000 BC, only hundreds. By 1450 AD, the Afro-Asian "mega-world" contained about 90% of humanity, while four smaller worlds (Mesoamerica, the Andes, Australia, Oceania) held the rest.

Then, over three centuries, the Afro-Asian giant swallowed everything:
- Mesoamerica (1521, Spanish conquest of the Aztecs)
- The Oceanic world (Magellan, 1520s)
- The Andes (1532, Pizarro vs. the Incas)
- Australia (1788, British settlement)
- Tasmania (last to fall, ~1803)

Today the whole planet shares the same geopolitical system (sovereign states), the same economic system (capitalism), the same legal system (human rights and international law), and the same scientific worldview.

## No Authentic Cultures Left
"Ethnic" cuisines turn out to be globalized inventions: Italian tomato sauce, Polish potatoes, Indian chilies, and Swiss chocolate all rely on Mexican crops that arrived after 1492. The Plains Indian on a horse — the iconic image of "authentic" Native American culture — was a creation of the *post-1492* introduction of European horses. There is no "authentic" culture untouched by global flows.

## The Universal Orders
But cultural mixing alone doesn't explain unification. Three universal orders, all originating in the first millennium BC, broke through the deep human tendency to divide the world into "us" and "them":

1. **Monetary**: the universal economic order.
2. **Imperial**: the universal political order.
3. **Religious**: the universal religions (Buddhism, Christianity, Islam).

Each of these is the subject of the next three chapters.

## Key Insight
*Homo sapiens* evolved as a tribal species. For most of our history, the categories "us" and "them" structured all moral thinking — your fellow tribe members deserved everything; outsiders deserved nothing. The universal orders were a revolutionary reframing: for the first time, anyone could potentially be "us." That reframing made global unification possible.
