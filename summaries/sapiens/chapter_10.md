# Chapter 10: The Scent of Money

## Overview
Money is humanity's most successful and universal fiction. People who share no language, religion, or political loyalty can still cooperate through money — because money is "the most universal and most efficient system of mutual trust ever devised."

## Beyond Barter
Foragers had no money: bands were small enough that an economy of favors, obligations, and occasional barter sufficed. Small farming villages worked the same way. But once cities and kingdoms arose with full-time specialists (shoemakers, doctors, vintners, lawyers), exchange became impossible without money:

- In barter with 100 commodities, you'd need to memorize **4,950 exchange rates**; with 1,000 commodities, **499,500 rates**.
- Even with the math figured out, barter requires both parties wanting what the other has at the same time.

Centralized "from each according to ability, to each according to need" experiments failed badly (Soviet Union); money proved a better solution.

## Money Was Invented Many Times
Money is "a purely mental revolution," not a technological one. Anything people will accept as a measure of value can be money:
- **Sumerian barley money** (~3000 BC) — the first known. Standardized in *sila* bowls. Even salaries were paid in barley.
- **Cowry shells** — currency in Africa, Asia, and Oceania for 4,000 years; still accepted as tax payment in British Uganda in the early 20th century.
- **Cigarettes** in modern prisons and POW camps — Auschwitz survivors describe price lists in cigarettes.
- **Silver shekels** in Mesopotamia (mid-3rd millennium BC) — 8.33 grams of silver, no inherent food value but easier to store and transport.
- **Coins** — first struck by King Alyattes of Lydia around 640 BC. Standardized weight + ruler's stamp guaranteeing purity.

Today over 90% of the world's $60+ trillion in money exists only as digital records on computer servers. Physical cash is the rare form.

## How Money Works: Trust
Cowry shells, gold coins, and dollar bills have no inherent value — they work because *we trust the figments of our collective imagination*. The trust is recursive:

> "I believe in dollars because my neighbors do. They believe because I do. We all believe because our king demands them in taxes, and our priest demands them in tithes."

This is why financial systems are politically fragile — a crisis of confidence can collapse a currency overnight. The dollar carries the slogan "In God We Trust" because that, plus the Treasury Secretary's signature, is literally all that backs it.

## The Gospel of Gold
Why did Spaniards, Indians, Muslims, and Chinese — cultures that disagreed about almost everything — all trust gold? The economic answer: once trade links two regions, supply and demand equalize the price of any transportable good. If gold is rare and prized in Region A but cheap and ignored in Region B, merchants will arbitrage until both regions value it the same. Other people's belief in gold becomes sufficient reason to want it yourself.

This is the crucial point: **religion asks you to believe in something; money asks you to believe that other people believe in something.** This makes money much easier to spread across cultures than religion or ideology.

## The Price of Money
Money is also dangerous. By converting everything into universal exchange, it dissolves the local economies of favors and obligations that bound communities together. Things that should not be for sale — loyalty, love, justice, salvation — become commodified. Medieval prostitutes used money to buy church indulgences; soldiers convert blood into tuition.

"Money corrodes local traditions, intimate relationships, and human values, replacing them with the cold laws of supply and demand."

## Key Insight
Money is "the apogee of human tolerance" — more open-minded than any religion, more universal than any empire. Even sworn enemies will use each other's currency (medieval Christians and Muslims both used coins bearing the other's religious symbols). This trust system was the single most important technology in unifying humanity into one economic world — but it came at the price of subordinating everything else to its logic.
