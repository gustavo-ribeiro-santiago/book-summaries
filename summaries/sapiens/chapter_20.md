# Chapter 20: The End of *Homo Sapiens*

## Overview
For 4 billion years, every organism on Earth evolved through natural selection. No species — not even Sapiens — was designed by an intelligent creator. But at the dawn of the 21st century, this is changing. **Homo sapiens is now beginning to replace natural selection with intelligent design.** This may be the most consequential biological event since the appearance of life itself — and it could mean the end of *Homo sapiens* as we know it.

## From Natural Selection to Intelligent Design
The first crack appeared 10,000 years ago when farmers selectively bred chickens and cattle — accelerating natural processes but not introducing truly new characteristics. Today's biotechnology breaks even that limit.

In 2000, Brazilian artist Eduardo Kac commissioned a **green fluorescent rabbit** named Alba — produced by inserting jellyfish DNA into a rabbit embryo. Alba could never have arisen through natural selection. She is the herald of a new era in which life is shaped by deliberate human design.

Harari identifies three paths by which this transformation may unfold:

### 1. Biological Engineering
Direct manipulation of genes and biology. Already used routinely:
- Bacteria engineered to produce insulin and biofuels.
- Frost-resistant potatoes carrying Arctic fish genes.
- Cows engineered to resist udder disease.
- "Genius mice" with dramatically improved memory.
- Worms with 6× normal lifespan.
- Voles whose monogamy gene has been isolated — raising the possibility of genetically engineering social behavior in mammals (including humans).

A team in Russia, Japan, and Korea is working to bring back the **woolly mammoth** by inserting reconstructed mammoth DNA into elephant eggs. Professor George Church at Harvard has proposed bringing back **Neanderthals** by implanting reconstructed Neanderthal DNA into a Sapiens egg, for about $30 million. Surrogate volunteers have already come forward.

If we can make genius mice, why not genius humans? If we can make monogamous voles, why not faithful humans? The Cognitive Revolution that made Sapiens dominant probably involved only small changes to brain structure. Another small change could trigger a **Second Cognitive Revolution** — and end *Homo sapiens* as a species.

### 2. Cyborg Engineering
Cyborgs combine organic and inorganic parts. In a sense, almost all of us are already cyborgs — pacemakers, eyeglasses, hearing aids, smartphones extend or supplement our biology.

Current research:
- **Bionic ears** that convert sound into nerve signals.
- **Retinal implants** that restore partial sight to the blind.
- **Bionic arms** like those used by Jesse Sullivan and Claudia Mitchell — controlled by thought alone. Researchers at Duke have monkeys (Aurora, Idoya) operating bionic limbs *located in other cities* through brain implants.
- **Locked-in syndrome patients** are being given electrodes that may translate brain signals into speech.

The most radical project is a **two-way brain-computer interface** allowing computers to read brain signals and send signals back. Once feasible, this could connect multiple brains into an "Inter-brain-net," where one cyborg could literally remember another's experiences. What happens to the self when minds become collective? "Such a cyborg would no longer be human, or even organic."

### 3. Inorganic Life
Genetic programming aims to create software that learns and evolves independently of its creator. Computer viruses already do this in a primitive form, mutating and evolving as they replicate. As they become more sophisticated, they may meet any reasonable definition of "life."

The **Human Brain Project**, founded in 2005, aims to recreate a complete human brain inside a computer within a decade or two. If successful, life will burst out of organic chemistry into the entire universe of computation. A digital mind raises terrifying questions: if you back up your brain to a hard drive, is the copy you? If you delete it, is that murder?

## The Singularity
Physics defines the Big Bang as a **singularity** — a point where all known laws break down. We may be approaching another singularity, beyond which all our concepts (self, gender, love, hate, individuality) become meaningless. Science fiction can't depict it because no human imagination can: trying to portray a super-cyborg's life is like staging *Hamlet* for Neanderthals.

## The Gilgamesh Project
The ancient Mesopotamian epic of Gilgamesh tells of a king who searched for immortality. The modern **Gilgamesh Project** — to conquer death itself — is now the flagship of science. It justifies almost every controversial research project: gene editing, brain implants, artificial intelligence. "We are doing it to cure diseases and save lives." Who could object?

But the Gilgamesh Project is inseparable from the **Frankenstein Project** — the creation of beings fundamentally unlike ourselves. You cannot defeat death without remaking what we are.

## The Frankenstein Prophecy
We tell ourselves the Frankenstein story as a warning: don't play God, or your creation will destroy you. But Harari reads it differently. The deeper meaning is that **Sapiens are nearing the end**. The pace of biological and computational engineering means that within decades or centuries, we will be replaced — not necessarily by anything hostile, but by something so different from us that our concepts cannot even apply to it.

We comfort ourselves with the thought that we might create a monster that we then defeat — implying that humans are the apex of being. We are not. Our successors will likely look at us "as condescendingly as we look at the Neanderthals."

## What Do We Want to Want?
Most people prefer not to think about it. Bioethics asks "What is forbidden?" but this misses the point. We cannot stop Gilgamesh. The technology is too useful, the diseases too terrible, the dream of immortality too powerful.

The only thing we can try to do is to **influence the direction**. Harari concludes with the deepest question of all. Since we may soon be able to engineer our very desires, the real question is not "What do we want to become?" but:

> **"What do we want to want?"**

The fact that this question even arises proves we have entered an entirely new era — one whose ethical structures we have not yet built.

## Key Insight
After 4 billion years of natural selection, Sapiens are about to become the first species to deliberately design their own successors — or to engineer themselves out of existence. We are no longer the masters of the natural world: we are about to leave it. The question of how Sapiens history ends may turn out to be the most important question Sapiens ever asked. And we are not asking it.
