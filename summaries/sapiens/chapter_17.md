# Chapter 17: The Wheels of Industry

## Overview
The modern economy demands perpetual growth. Growth demands continually expanding energy and raw materials — yet humanity's energy and material consumption has grown 100-fold without ever truly running out. The Industrial Revolution is, at its core, a revolution in **energy conversion**: learning to transform one kind of energy into any other kind we need.

## The Old Energy Regime
For most of history, humans depended on a single form of energy: the **muscle** (theirs and their animals'). Muscles ran on food. Food was plants. Plants captured solar energy through photosynthesis. So essentially, all human activity was solar-powered through a chain ending in plant calories.

There were other energy sources — burning wood, wind for sailing, water for milling — but humans had **no way to convert** one form of energy into another. Wind could push a sailboat but couldn't smelt iron. Heat from a fire couldn't grind a millstone. The body was the only conversion device.

This dependence on solar/plant/muscle energy structured all of pre-industrial history. Seasons of growing and harvesting dictated everything from peasant labor to royal warfare.

## The Steam Engine
For thousands of years humans watched kettles boil and saw their lids jump — heat being converted to motion — without grasping the implications. The first breakthrough was gunpowder (9th-century China), but it took 600 years to develop effective artillery.

The real revolution came in 18th-century British coal mines. Coal seams flooded with water, and miners needed a way to pump it out. The **steam engine** was the answer: burn coal → boil water → expanding steam pushes a piston → piston pumps water. Heat had become motion. The earliest engines were absurdly inefficient, but in a coal mine, coal was free.

The conceptual leap was bigger than the engine itself. If steam could pump water, why not power textile looms? Once steam ran looms, why not vehicles? In 1825 the first locomotive ran from a mine to a port. By 1850 Britain had **tens of thousands of kilometers of rail**. The world had been rewired.

## The Cascade of Conversions
Once humans grasped the principle of energy conversion, new sources fell rapidly:
- **Internal combustion engine** + petroleum = automobiles, airplanes, oil wars.
- **Electricity** = light, refrigeration, communication, computation. Two centuries ago electricity was a parlor trick; today no one can imagine life without it.
- **Nuclear** = took just 40 years from Einstein's E=mc² to atomic bombs and power plants.

The shift was psychological as much as technical. Once you accept that any form of energy can be converted into any other, the question is no longer "what energy do we have?" but "what energy do we want?"

## An Ocean of Energy
The Earth receives 3.76 million exajoules of solar energy per year. Plants capture only about 3,000 — and all human activity combined uses only about 500. We use less energy in a year than the sun delivers to Earth in **90 minutes**. The world is awash in energy: solar, nuclear, gravitational (tides). What we lack is not energy but the knowledge to harness it.

## New Materials
The same revolution transformed raw materials. **Aluminum**, discovered in the 1820s, was once more expensive than gold — Napoleon III served his most distinguished guests with aluminum cutlery while lesser visitors made do with gold. By the end of the 19th century, chemists found a cheap extraction method. Today aluminum is so abundant we wrap sandwiches in it.

Plastics, silicon, titanium — all invented or discovered in the past two centuries. A modern hand cream contains dozens of compounds, almost none of which existed before 1800.

In WWI, Germany was cut off from Chilean and Indian saltpeter (essential for explosives). Fritz Haber's 1908 discovery of how to synthesize ammonia from atmospheric nitrogen let Germany manufacture explosives **out of thin air**. (Haber also pioneered chemical weapons; he was awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1918, not in Peace.)

## The Second Agricultural Revolution
The Industrial Revolution was, above all, an agricultural revolution. **Tractors** replaced muscle. Artificial fertilizers, pesticides, hormones, and antibiotics radically increased yields. Refrigeration, shipping, and aviation allowed food to be stored for months and moved across oceans within days.

Before this transformation, 90%+ of any population farmed; today only 2% of Americans do, yet they feed the country and export surpluses. **Without industrial agriculture, the urban Industrial Revolution would have been impossible** — there wouldn't have been enough free hands to staff factories.

## Animals on the Conveyor Belt
The dark side: industrial agriculture turned animals into machines. Tens of billions of farm animals now live entire lives as parts of an assembly line. Chickens are confined four-to-a-cage with floor space of 25 × 22 cm — too small to flap their wings. Nursing sows are kept in crates so small they cannot turn around. Dairy cows stand all day in their own waste, fed by one machine and milked by another.

The cruelty here is not driven by hatred. It is **driven by indifference** — and by the same scientific worldview that reduces animals to "machines without feelings." Yet modern biology has proven beyond doubt that mammals and birds experience pain, fear, and emotional distress.

**Harlow's monkeys**: in the 1950s, Harry Harlow took infant monkeys from their mothers and gave them a choice between a wire "mother" with a milk bottle and a soft cloth "mother" with no food. The monkeys overwhelmingly chose the cloth mother — clinging for comfort while reaching over for food. Even with all material needs met, the orphaned monkeys grew up emotionally damaged.

Modern farm animals experience the same separation, isolation, and frustration of basic instincts — at a scale of billions per year. Around 50 billion animals are slaughtered annually. The most numerous mammals on earth are now humans and their livestock; all wild mammals combined are a small fraction.

## The Age of Shopping
The Industrial Revolution didn't just produce more — it produced more than demand. For the first time in history, supply outstripped what people needed. A new ethic was required: **consumerism**.

Frugality, once a universal virtue, became almost a vice. Cereal boxes urge customers to "treat themselves"; "indulgence" is now packaged as self-care. Obesity is now a bigger killer than starvation. Americans spend more on diets each year than it would cost to feed every hungry person in the world.

The capitalist-consumerist ethic is genius in its division of labor:
- **The rich are told to invest.**
- **Everyone else is told to buy.**

These are the twin commandments. Every other ethical system in history demanded difficult things — compassion, restraint, prayer, sacrifice — and most followers failed to live up to them. Capitalism-consumerism is unique: **its followers actually do what they are told.** "How do we know we'll get paradise in return? We've seen it on television."

## Key Insight
The Industrial Revolution proved that the only limit on human power is our knowledge. It also proved that humans will eagerly exploit that power without limit — converting plants into fuel, animals into protein machines, planets into resources, and human attention into purchasing power. The next chapter examines the social and political revolutions that this material transformation produced.
