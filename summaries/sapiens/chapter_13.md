# Chapter 13: The Secret of Success

## Overview
Money, empire, and religion together drove humanity toward global unification. But while that unification was probably inevitable, **the specific form it took was not**. Harari closes Part Three with a meditation on the nature of history itself: it is contingent, chaotic, and indifferent to human well-being.

## The Hindsight Fallacy
We look at the past and tell ourselves stories about why what happened *had* to happen — why Christianity took over Rome, why English became dominant, why capitalism beat communism. But the people living through those moments had no idea any of it was likely.

- In AD 306, when Constantine took the throne, Christianity was a fringe Eastern cult. Saying it would become the Roman state religion would have been as absurd as predicting today that Hare Krishna will run the USA by 2050.
- In October 1913, the Bolsheviks were a small faction. Four years later they ruled Russia.
- In AD 600, predicting that desert Arabs would soon control an empire from the Atlantic to India was patently ridiculous.

The more deeply a historian knows a period, the **harder** it becomes to explain why events unfolded as they did. Only the superficially informed find history obviously inevitable.

## "How" vs. "Why"
Historians can describe **how** Christianity took over Rome (the sequence of events). They can rarely explain **why** — that is, why this particular outcome rather than any number of others. Determinist theories (it was geography, biology, economics) usually look weaker the closer you examine the actual evidence.

This matters today. Are we headed for ecological catastrophe or technological paradise? Will China surpass the U.S.? Is fundamentalist religion the future or a temporary spasm? In every period, including ours, what's truly going on is invisible to those living through it.

## History as Level-Two Chaos
Harari distinguishes two kinds of chaotic systems:
- **Level-one chaos** doesn't react to predictions about it. The weather is level one — we can build better and better forecasting models without the weather caring.
- **Level-two chaos** *does* react to predictions, and therefore cannot be predicted accurately. **Markets and politics are level-two chaotic.**

If a perfect algorithm predicted oil would be $100 tomorrow, oil would be $100 today. If a perfect algorithm had predicted the Arab Spring, Mubarak would have headed it off — and the algorithm would have "failed." Revolutions are by definition unpredictable: a predicted revolution doesn't happen.

So why study history? **Not to predict the future** but to **widen our horizons** — to realize that our current world (capitalism, nation-states, human rights, monotheism) is not natural or inevitable. Things could have been radically different. They might still be.

## Blind Clio: History Doesn't Care About You
Even more disturbing than the unpredictability of history is its **moral indifference**. There is no proof that the cultures that succeed are the ones best for humans. There is no proof that Christianity was better for humanity than Manichaeism, or that the Roman Empire was better than what it replaced. We assume the winners must have been the right choice because we are descended from the winners' worldview.

### Cultures as Mental Parasites
Some scholars view cultures as **memes** — units of cultural information that, like genes, spread and replicate. A successful meme cares no more about its hosts than a virus cares about a body. Belief in a heavenly afterlife can compel humans to die for the cause; the human dies, but the idea spreads. **Cultures are not conspiracies. They are accidental mental infections that succeed at reproducing themselves.**

Postmodernists make a similar argument using "discourse" instead of "meme." Nationalism, they argue, spread across the 19th and 20th centuries like a virus, causing wars, oppression, and genocide — benefiting the *idea*, not the people it infected.

### Arms Races
Game theory shows how patterns of behavior that **harm everyone** can still spread. Arms races bankrupt all participants without changing the balance of power. India buys planes, Pakistan responds; Pakistan goes nuclear, India follows; on and on. Billions that could fund health and education are wasted on weapons. But each step is rational from the local point of view, so the whole pattern propagates itself.

## Key Insight
History is **contingent, chaotic, and indifferent**. It is shaped by countless forces that produce wildly different outcomes from tiny initial differences. It does not work toward human happiness or any other goal. Its winners did not necessarily deserve to win, and our world — for all that it feels inevitable — is just one of countless possible worlds.

Around AD 1500, history made its most consequential turn: **the Scientific Revolution.** Why it happened in Western Europe rather than China or India, at the midpoint of the second millennium rather than centuries earlier or later, we genuinely do not know. The next part of the book examines what that revolution unleashed — and how it brought us to the world we now inhabit.
