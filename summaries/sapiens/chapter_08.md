# Chapter 8: There is No Justice in History

## Overview
Every complex society in history has organized itself around an **imagined hierarchy** — a system of categories that grant some people power and privilege while denying them to others. These hierarchies always disavow their fictional origins and claim to be "natural and inevitable." Harari examines caste, race, wealth, and gender to show how each emerges from accidents of history, then perpetuates itself through self-reinforcing vicious circles.

## All Hierarchies Are Imagined
- **Hammurabi's Babylon**: superiors, commoners, slaves
- **The American Founders**: free vs. slave, white vs. black, men vs. women, rich vs. poor
- **Hindu India**: Brahmins, Kshatriyas, Vaishyas, Shudras, Untouchables — supposedly created from the body parts of the primeval being Purusa
- **Ancient China**: aristocrats from yellow soil, commoners from brown mud

In every case, the privileged class justifies its position through claims of divine ordination, biological superiority, or natural law. In every case, the claim is false: human laws and norms — not biology — created these distinctions.

## Why Hierarchies Persist
Hierarchies serve a function. They let strangers know how to treat each other without time-consuming personal acquaintance. A florist quickly distinguishes the partner from the messenger boy by dress and bearing. This isn't always evil, but it locks people into the categories they're born into:
1. **Talents must be developed** — and only those of high status get the chance.
2. **Even with equal talent, the rules of the game are rigged** by laws, glass ceilings, and unspoken norms.

## The Indian Caste System
About 3,000 years ago, Indo-Aryan invaders subjugated the local population and instituted a stratified system to protect their privileged status. To prevent intermixing, they created strict laws of purity: pollution by contact with a lower caste threatened the soul and the cosmos. Over time, four castes proliferated into 3,000 *jati* — but the underlying principle of birth-determined status remained. The caste system outlived its origin by **3,000 years** and remains deeply embedded in Indian society today.

## The American Racial Hierarchy
This too began with contingent circumstance:
1. Africa was closer than Asia, so slaves were cheaper to import.
2. Africa already had a developed slave trade.
3. Africans had **partial genetic immunity** to tropical diseases (malaria, yellow fever) that killed European workers in droves. Paradoxically, biological *superiority* in disease resistance became the cause of social *inferiority*.

Once slavery existed, theologians (Africans as cursed descendants of Ham), biologists (lower intelligence), and doctors (carriers of disease) provided justifications. After emancipation in 1865, the racial caste persisted through the same vicious circle: black families were poorer and less educated, so blacks held fewer professional jobs, "proving" their inferiority, justifying continued discrimination, ensuring the next generation started equally disadvantaged.

By the 1950s, racial segregation in the American South was arguably worse than just after emancipation — purity laws, lynching, and white-supremacist secret societies (the KKK) enforced separation, especially of sexual contact between black men and white women.

## The Self-Reinforcing Pattern
The vicious circle works the same way everywhere:
1. A chance historical event creates a hierarchy.
2. Stories and myths justify the hierarchy as natural.
3. Laws and norms enforce the hierarchy.
4. The disadvantaged are denied resources and opportunities.
5. Their resulting deficits are cited as proof they "deserve" their status.
6. The cycle continues for generations or millennia.

"Money comes to money, and poverty to poverty. Education comes to education, and ignorance to ignorance. Those once victimised by history are likely to be victimised yet again."

## He and She
The most universal hierarchy of all is **gender**. Almost every known society since the Agricultural Revolution has been patriarchal. Modern Western societies inherited this default from medieval Christianity and ancient Greece, where women could not vote, own property, or hold office.

Harari distinguishes:
- **Sex** (biological): male/female, defined by XX or XY chromosomes. Stable across history.
- **Gender** (cultural): the imagined roles, rights, and duties assigned to "men" and "women." Varies enormously — King Louis XIV in long wig, stockings, and heels was the very paragon of masculinity; today the same outfit would be read as feminine.

## Why Patriarchy?
The universality of patriarchy is hard to explain by pure historical accident — Aztecs, Egyptians, Chinese, and Romans all were patriarchal, despite no contact. Harari examines three biological theories — and finds **all of them unconvincing**:

1. **Muscle power**: Men are physically stronger, so they dominate. But strength and social power are inversely correlated in human societies — laborers are not rulers. Women have historically been excluded most from low-effort jobs (politics, priesthood) while doing hard manual work in fields.

2. **Aggression**: Men are more violent and thus monopolize warfare and rule. But the people who *run* wars are usually not the soldiers who fight them. Aristocrats, mandarins, bureaucrats — none required muscle or aggression. Augustus, not Alexander, founded a stable empire, through *clementia* (mildness).

3. **Reproductive strategy**: Men compete to impregnate, women must rely on men for protection during childbearing. But many species (bonobos, elephants) face the same biological pressures and develop **matriarchal** societies — females form cooperative networks, males are pushed to the margins. Why didn't Sapiens, whose entire advantage is cooperation, follow the same path?

**Harari concludes: we genuinely don't know why patriarchy is so universal.** That ignorance is itself revealing. And it's especially striking that — over the last century — gender roles have changed faster than any other hierarchy. Women now vote, lead governments, serve on Supreme Courts; same-sex marriage is legal in dozens of countries. If patriarchy were truly grounded in biology, such rapid change would be impossible.

## Key Insight
Hierarchies are not natural — they're imagined orders. They began as historical accidents, were justified by myth, and reinforced by vicious circles. The fact that we cannot explain patriarchy's universality biologically, combined with how quickly it is now collapsing, supports Harari's thesis: the most powerful structures organizing human life are fictions, and like all fictions, they can be rewritten.
