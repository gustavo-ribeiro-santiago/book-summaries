# Chapter 4: The Flood

## Overview
After the Cognitive Revolution, Sapiens spread beyond Afro-Asia for the first time, reaching Australia (~45,000 years ago) and the Americas (~16,000 years ago). Wherever we arrived, mass extinctions of large animals followed. *Homo sapiens* is, by the historical record, the **deadliest species in the history of life on Earth**.

## The Conquest of Australia
Reaching Australia required crossing open ocean and adapting overnight to a completely alien ecosystem — a feat no large mammal had previously achieved. Sapiens must have developed seafaring around 45,000 years ago in the Indonesian archipelago. The arrival in Australia was a turning point: the first time any human reached the top of the food chain on a new continent.

Within a few thousand years, **23 of 24 Australian animal species weighing over 50 kg went extinct**: giant kangaroos, marsupial lions, two-and-a-half-ton wombats (diprotodons), flightless birds twice the size of ostriches. Whole food chains collapsed.

## Climate vs. Humans
Some scholars blame climate change, but Harari argues three pieces of evidence make humans the chief culprit:

1. **Australia's climate change ~45,000 years ago was modest** — the megafauna had survived ten previous, harsher ice ages.
2. **Ocean animals were unaffected** — yet climate change should have hit them too. Only land animals died, exactly what we'd expect from terrestrial human hunters.
3. **The pattern repeated everywhere**. New Zealand's megafauna survived the same climate shift untouched — only to be wiped out 800 years ago when Maori settlers arrived. Wrangel Island's mammoths flourished for millennia, then disappeared 4,000 years ago, the moment humans arrived. The historical record makes "*Homo sapiens* look like an ecological serial killer."

## How Stone-Age People Caused Mass Extinction
Three reinforcing mechanisms:
1. **Slow reproduction**: Large animals breed slowly. Killing even one diprotodon every few months exceeded their birth rate.
2. **No learned fear**: Unlike African megafauna, which co-evolved with humans for millions of years and learned to avoid them, Australian and American animals had no time to develop fear of this strange new biped.
3. **Fire-agriculture**: Sapiens deliberately burned vast tracts of forest to create grasslands. Eucalyptus trees, which are fire-resistant, took over Australia, devastating species that depended on the older ecosystem.

## The American Extinction
Sapiens reached the Americas around 14,000 BC by crossing the Bering land bridge during the ice age. Within 2,000 years they had colonized every habitat from Alaska to Tierra del Fuego. Along the way, North America lost 34 of 47 genera of large mammals, South America 50 of 60: sabre-tooth cats, giant ground sloths, mammoths, native horses and camels, eight-ton armadillos. Petrified dung analysis shows these animals all died at exactly the moment humans arrived.

The Caribbean islands tell a particularly damning story: ground sloths survived there until **5000 BC** — exactly when humans first crossed to Cuba and Hispaniola.

## Three Waves of Extinction
Harari identifies three extinction waves caused by humans:
1. **First Wave**: Spread of foragers (Australia, Americas, islands).
2. **Second Wave**: Spread of farmers (Madagascar's giant lemurs and elephant birds; the Pacific Islands; New Zealand).
3. **Third Wave**: Industrial activity, currently underway, threatening marine megafauna in particular.

## Key Insight
The myth of an "ecologically harmonious" pre-industrial humanity is exactly that — a myth. Long before the wheel, writing, or metal tools, Sapiens drove half the planet's large terrestrial mammals to extinction. If we knew how much we had already destroyed, perhaps we'd be more motivated to protect what remains.
