# Chapter 6: Building Pyramids

## Overview
Once agriculture took hold, humanity's social and political world transformed at an accelerating pace. By 3100 BC the lower Nile was unified into Egypt. By 2250 BC Sargon founded the first empire. By 221 BC Qin China and Rome ruled tens of millions. None of this would have been possible without a new technology — not iron, not the wheel, but **imagined orders**: shared myths capable of binding millions of strangers into cooperation.

## A Smaller, More Artificial World
Farmers' worlds shrank. Foragers ranged over hundreds of square kilometers — their "home" was the landscape itself. Peasants lived in a small house surrounded by a tiny field. Their territory was artificial: forests cleared, canals dug, walls built, weeds and pests endlessly fought. Until 1400 AD, all the world's farmers occupied just 2% of Earth's land surface — and yet that small stage is where almost all "history" played out.

## Agricultural Time
Foragers lived hand-to-mouth and didn't worry much beyond the immediate future. Farmers, by contrast, lived in **deep time**: planting in spring for a harvest in fall, storing grain against next year's failure, planting olive trees their grandchildren would harvest. The seasonal cycle and constant agricultural uncertainty made anxiety about the future a permanent feature of human mental life.

This anxiety was productive: it created vast surpluses. But the surpluses were captured by emerging elites — kings, priests, soldiers, officials. Until the late modern era, over 90% of humans were peasants whose labor fed the tiny minority that "made history."

## Imagined Orders
Food surpluses and better transport made cities and kingdoms possible — but not inevitable. Sapiens evolved to live in bands of a few dozen. The mere existence of food could not get a million strangers to cooperate. The glue had to be invented. That glue was **shared myth**.

Harari develops the theme of "imagined orders" through two parallel examples:

### The Code of Hammurabi (1776 BC)
- Declared that the gods Anu, Enlil, and Marduk appointed Hammurabi to "make justice prevail."
- Divided people into three classes — superiors, commoners, slaves — with vastly different legal worth (a male commoner's eye = 60 shekels; a slave-woman's life = 20 shekels).
- Claimed these distinctions reflected eternal divine law.

### The American Declaration of Independence (1776 AD)
- Declared that "all men are created equal" with "unalienable rights" from their Creator.
- Promised cooperation through universal, eternal principles.

Both documents claim universal, immutable truths. **Both are myths.** Translated into biology:

> "We hold these truths to be self-evident, that all men evolved differently, that they are born with certain mutable characteristics, and that among these are life and the pursuit of pleasure."

Evolution is based on **difference**, not equality. There's no biological "Creator," no biological "rights," no biological "liberty." These are useful fictions — fictions that enable millions to cooperate, but fictions nonetheless.

## The Key Distinction
The brilliance of Harari's analysis is that he treats Hammurabi's hierarchy and American equality as **the same kind of thing** — equally fictional, equally functional. The Babylonian and the American would each look at the other's order and call it absurd. But both orders worked, because their populations believed in them. **"Believing in it enables us to cooperate effectively and forge a better society."**

This is what an imagined order is: not an evil conspiracy, not a useless illusion, but the only mechanism large enough to bind together the millions of strangers required to build pyramids, write codes of law, or run modern democracies.

## Key Insight
History's great cooperative structures — pyramids, empires, religions, corporations, nations — are not built on instinct or on personal acquaintance. They are built on **stories everyone agrees to tell themselves**. Change the stories, and society changes. This is the central principle that runs through the rest of the book.
