# Chapter 14: The Discovery of Ignorance

## Overview
Part Four opens with a stunning comparison: a medieval peasant who slept from AD 1000 to 1500 would have woken in a recognizable world; a sailor from 1500 who woke today would be in something incomprehensible. In just 500 years humanity has gone from 500 million to 7 billion people, from $250 billion to $60 trillion of annual production, from 13 trillion to 1,500 trillion daily calories of energy. This is the **Scientific Revolution** — and Harari argues it began with a radical and simple insight: **we do not know**.

## What Makes Modern Science Different
Earlier traditions of knowledge — Christianity, Islam, Buddhism, Confucianism — all assumed that everything important was *already known*, encoded in scriptures and ancient wisdom. Individuals might be ignorant, but the *tradition* knew. If a question wasn't answered in the holy books, it wasn't important enough to ask.

Modern science breaks with this in three ways:

1. **The willingness to admit ignorance.** Modern science is grounded in *ignoramus* — "we do not know." Even our best theories may be wrong; no claim is sacred. Darwin never said he had the final word on biology. Physicists still don't know what caused the Big Bang.

2. **The centrality of observation and mathematics.** Having admitted ignorance, science systematically gathers observations and uses mathematics to connect them into testable theories.

3. **The acquisition of new powers.** Science doesn't stop at understanding — it produces technologies. Knowledge becomes power.

This is why Harari calls the Scientific Revolution "above all, a revolution of ignorance."

## The Sociopolitical Problem
Admitting ignorance creates a problem: large societies depend on shared myths (nations, gods, rights, money) to function. If we admit those myths are tentative human inventions, how do societies cohere? Modern civilization solves this in one of two unscientific ways:

- **Dogmatize a "scientific" theory.** Nazis claimed their racial policies followed from biology; Communists claimed Marx had divined eternal economic laws. Both reduced science to absolute truth — which is what real science refuses to do.
- **Live by an explicitly non-scientific framework.** Liberal humanism dogmatically asserts that humans have unique worth and rights, even though science offers no evidence for this.

Modern culture also relies on a near-religious **faith in scientific method itself** — trust not in any one answer, but in the process of discovery.

## The Language of Mathematics
Ancient traditions told stories. Modern science writes equations. In 1687, Newton's *Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy* introduced laws that could predict the motion of any object in the universe with three simple equations. Suddenly the "book of nature" was readable in mathematics.

What couldn't be reduced to clean equations — biology, economics, psychology — was eventually conquered by a different branch of mathematics: **statistics**.

A telling story: in 1744, two Scottish Presbyterian ministers, Webster and Wallace, wanted to set up a pension fund for the widows of dead clergymen. Rather than praying or consulting scripture, they hired a math professor and applied Jacob Bernoulli's Law of Large Numbers and Edmond Halley's actuarial tables. They predicted the fund would hold £58,348 by 1765. The actual amount, in 1765, was £58,347 — off by £1. That fund is now Scottish Widows, with £100 billion in assets. *This kind of accuracy from prediction was more reliable than the prophecies of Jeremiah or St. John.*

Today, even psychology and sociology require statistics courses. Confucius and Muhammad would be bewildered.

## Knowledge Is Power
Francis Bacon's 1620 manifesto *The New Instrument* argued that **"knowledge is power."** The real test of a theory is not truth (no theory is 100% correct) but **utility** — what new things it lets us do.

Before 1500, science and technology were separate worlds. Inventions came mostly from uneducated craftsmen using trial and error — cart makers improved carts by accident, not by research. Even after Bacon, it took until the 19th century for the alliance between science and technology to become tight. By then, governments and corporations were funding research with the explicit expectation of new powers.

## The Military-Industrial-Scientific Complex
Today, war is a scientific production. World War I produced poison gas, tanks, submarines, and combat aircraft. World War II produced the V-2 rocket, jet fighters, and ultimately the atomic bomb. The Alamogordo test on July 16, 1945 marked the moment humanity gained the power to *end* its own history.

Today the U.S. Department of Defense funds nanotechnology (bionic spy-flies), brain research (thought-reading scanners), and countless other lines of work. The military-industrial complex Eisenhower warned about should really have been called the **military-industrial-scientific complex**.

## The Feedback Loop
The Scientific Revolution works through a virtuous cycle:
- **Politics and economics** fund scientific research.
- **Research** delivers new powers (medicines, weapons, technologies).
- These powers generate new wealth and political dominance.
- That wealth and dominance fund more research.

Without this alliance between science, capitalism, and empire, the Scientific Revolution would not have happened. The next two chapters trace the alliance with empire (Chapter 15) and capitalism (Chapter 16).

## Key Insight
The Scientific Revolution did not begin with a discovery. It began with an **admission**: that humans do not know what they need to know, and that the ancients did not know either. From that humble starting point came a feedback loop that has multiplied human power 100-fold in 500 years — and has put us in a position where we now have powers our institutions, ethics, and self-understanding cannot keep up with.
