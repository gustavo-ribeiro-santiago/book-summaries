# Chapter 3: A Day in the Life of Adam and Eve

## Overview
For 95% of Sapiens' existence we lived as hunter-gatherers, and our minds, bodies, and emotions were shaped by that lifestyle. Understanding the forager world is essential to understanding ourselves — but reconstructing it is hard, because foragers left few physical artifacts and modern foragers (in marginal habitats like the Kalahari) are not representative of ancient ones.

## Evolutionary Echoes
The **"gorging gene"** theory: we crave high-calorie sweet and fatty foods because in the ancestral environment such foods were rare and seasonal — eating as much as possible when available made evolutionary sense. Our DNA still thinks we live on the savannah, even when surrounded by refrigerators.

Other ancestral theories are more contentious. The **"ancient commune"** hypothesis suggests early foragers practiced collective parenting and non-monogamous mating, like bonobos — implying modern marriage and family stress arise from forcing humans into nuclear families against our biological grain. Critics argue monogamy and the nuclear family are ancient. Evidence is too thin to resolve the debate.

## The Diversity of Forager Cultures
A central point: there is **no single "natural way of life"** for Sapiens. The 5–8 million foragers alive at the dawn of agriculture lived in thousands of separate tribes with thousands of different languages, religions, and norms. A band near Oxford might have spoken a different language and held opposite values from a band near Cambridge. Cognitive flexibility produced cultural diversity.

## The Original Affluent Society
Foragers may have lived **better lives than the peasants and office workers who followed**:
- They worked fewer hours: 35–45 hours/week even in the harsh Kalahari, less in fertile zones.
- Their diet was more varied (dozens of foods), so they were less vulnerable to famine and malnutrition.
- They were less prone to infectious disease, which mostly originated in domesticated animals and dense permanent settlements.
- Their bodies were fitter, their senses sharper, and their knowledge of the natural world deeper than any modern person's.

Average life expectancy was 30–40 years, but mostly due to child mortality. Adults who survived childhood often lived to 60–80.

## The Dark Side
Forager life was not idyllic. Periods of want occurred. Child mortality was high. Modern foragers like the Aché of Paraguay killed sick old people, unwanted babies, and children deemed misshapen. Violence between bands was real. Foragers were neither angels nor monsters — they were human.

## Spiritual and Social Life
Most foragers were probably **animists** — believing rocks, trees, animals, streams, and spirits all had awareness and could communicate with humans. Unlike later theistic religions, animism had no strict hierarchy: humans were not the center of the universe, and gods did not run the world from above.

But beyond this generality, we know almost nothing about specific forager beliefs, festivals, rituals, or social structures. Some bands may have been hierarchical, some egalitarian; some peaceful, some warlike. The Sungir burial site (30,000 years old) suggests some bands had clear leaders. Other bands may have been radically egalitarian.

## Key Insight
The hunter-gatherer way of life shaped our minds, but it was extraordinarily diverse. There is no single ancestral lifestyle to return to. What we can say with confidence: foragers were generally healthier, freer, and more knowledgeable than the peasants who followed them — and the Agricultural Revolution was not the clear win we usually imagine.
