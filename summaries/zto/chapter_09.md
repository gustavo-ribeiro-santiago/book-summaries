# Chapter 9: Foundations — Zero to One

## Overview

A startup that gets its founding decisions wrong cannot be fixed later. This chapter covers the critical early decisions — choosing co-founders, structuring ownership and control, setting equity — and explains why the founding moment is qualitatively unlike anything that follows.

## Key Concepts

- **"Thiel's Law"**: A startup messed up at its foundation cannot be fixed. Beginnings are special and qualitatively different from everything that comes after. The U.S. Constitution's structure — debated intensely in summer 1787 — has been changed only 27 times since. Early decisions calcify.
- **Founding Matrimony — Choosing Co-Founders**: Choosing a co-founder is like getting married; founder conflict is as ugly as divorce. Optimism blinds people to this at the start. You must have a track record with your co-founders — you need to know how they think, work, and handle adversity before betting the company on them.
- **Ownership, Possession, and Control**:
  - *Ownership*: Who legally owns the equity (founders, employees, investors).
  - *Possession*: Who actually runs the company day-to-day (management).
  - *Control*: Who formally governs (the board).
  - These three must be explicitly designed and balanced. Misalignment between them (e.g., an investor-controlled board with employee-possessing managers) creates conflict.
- **Board Size Matters**: A board of 3 is ideal; 5 is workable; beyond 7 is effectively decorative and means the company is not really being governed. Large boards diffuse accountability and enable dysfunction to hide.
- **Cash Is Not King — Equity Aligns Incentives**: High cash compensation creates an employee mentality; equity creates an owner mentality. Founders should be paid modest salaries (Thiel paid himself $37K/year at PayPal). Everyone in a startup should be invested in the long-term outcome, not just the next paycheck. The CEO's salary sets the upper bound for everyone else.
- **Vesting and the Founding Team**: All equity should vest over time to prevent co-founders from leaving and keeping their shares. Typically 4 years with a 1-year cliff. An unvested founder who leaves is a company killer.
- **Extending the Founding**: The founding spirit must be preserved past the founding moment — the company's culture, values, and sense of mission. Once lost, it is extraordinarily hard to recover.

## Core Message

The decisions made at a company's founding — who to co-found with, how to structure ownership and control, how to compensate the team — are the hardest to undo and the most consequential. Get them right the first time, because "a startup messed up at its foundation cannot be fixed."
