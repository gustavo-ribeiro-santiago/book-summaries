# Chapter 1: The Challenge of the Future — Zero to One

## Overview

This chapter opens with Thiel's favorite interview question — "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" — and uses it to frame the entire book's project: thinking clearly about the future requires contrarian thinking, and the most important contrarian truth today is that technology matters more than globalization.

## Key Concepts

- **The Contrarian Question**: Good answers take the form "Most people believe X, but the truth is the opposite of X." This is intellectually and psychologically hard — it requires both insight and the courage to be unpopular. Most people give bad answers (statements others already agree with).
- **Two Forms of Progress**:
  - **Horizontal (1→n)**: Copying things that work — globalization, spreading proven models everywhere. China is the paradigmatic example.
  - **Vertical (0→1)**: Doing new things — technology, creating something that didn't exist before.
- **Thiel's Contrarian Answer**: Most people think globalization will define the future, but technology matters more. Spreading old ways of creating wealth in a resource-scarce world is unsustainable without new technology. Globalization without technological progress leads to devastation, not richness.
- **The Historical Pattern**: Sustained technological progress is not automatic. After millennia of slow advance, modern technology exploded from the steam engine (1760s) through roughly 1970 — but since then, meaningful progress has been mostly confined to IT. Our parents expected this progress to continue (four-day workweeks, moon vacations); it didn't, because they were wrong to treat it as automatic.
- **Why Startups?**: New technology tends to come from new ventures. Big organizations move slowly and entrench interests; lone geniuses can create art but not industries. A startup is the largest group of people you can convince of a plan to build a different future — combining collective action with the ability to actually execute new thinking.

## Core Message

The future belongs to those who create genuinely new things, not those who copy what works. The challenge of the future is to both imagine and create new technologies that can make the 21st century better than the 20th. That challenge falls to startups and the entrepreneurs who build them.
