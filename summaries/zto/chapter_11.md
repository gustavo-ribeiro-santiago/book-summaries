# Chapter 11: If You Build It, Will They Come? — Zero to One

## Overview

Silicon Valley has a deep cultural bias against sales and distribution — a bias that kills otherwise good companies. This chapter argues that distribution is as important as product, that all great businesses have mastered sales, and that the best salespeople are the ones you don't recognize as salespeople.

## Key Concepts

- **The Engineer's Bias**: Silicon Valley culture treats sales as superficial and irrational — the province of smooth-talkers, not serious technologists. This is wrong. Advertising works on everyone, including nerds. The question is not whether to sell but how.
- **Distribution Matters**: "Distribution" is everything it takes to sell a product. Without it, no product — no matter how good — reaches customers. "If you build it, they will come" is a fantasy. Getting distribution right is a core competency, not an afterthought.
- **Sales Is Hidden**: The best sales doesn't look like sales. Apple's product launches feel like events; Palantir's deals feel like government partnerships; Tesla's "word of mouth" is carefully engineered. When you don't see the selling, it's working perfectly.
- **The Key Metric: Customer Acquisition Cost vs. Lifetime Value (CLV)**: Every business needs a distribution method whose cost is less than the revenue per customer.
  - *Complex sales* (enterprise, government): High CLV per customer, small number, relationship-driven. A CEO selling personally to a few dozen customers a year works; a sales force calling on thousands doesn't.
  - *Personal sales*: Mid-range CLV; trained salespeople with defined pitch and clear ROI. The "dead zone" is $1K–$10K products where there's not enough CLV for enterprise sales but too much friction for viral.
  - *Distribution* (marketing + ads): Low CLV per customer but high volume. Works for consumer products.
  - *Viral growth*: Near-zero distribution cost; each user brings more users. PayPal's referral program, Facebook's news feed.
- **The Power Law of Distribution**: Just as one distribution channel is unlikely to work, finding *one* great channel is often enough to build a successful business. Most companies fail not because their product is bad but because they never find a scalable distribution model.
- **Everybody Sells**: Founders sell to investors, employees, customers, and press. Employees sell internally. Executives sell to boards. The refusal to acknowledge this leads to bad outcomes. A product that "sells itself" is a myth — someone is always selling it.

## Core Message

"If you build it, they will come" is one of the most dangerous ideas in Silicon Valley. Every great product needs a great distribution strategy. Find one distribution channel that works reliably — whether complex enterprise sales, direct sales, marketing, or viral growth — and build the company around it. Underestimate sales at your peril.
