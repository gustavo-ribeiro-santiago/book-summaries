# Chapter 13: Seeing Green — Zero to One

## Overview

The cleantech bubble of the 2000s is a case study in how companies fail even when they're working on real problems. Thiel uses it to introduce seven questions every startup must honestly answer — a framework that applies far beyond cleantech to any new venture.

## Key Concepts

- **The Cleantech Failure**: Over $50 billion was invested in cleantech companies; most failed catastrophically. Solyndra is the most famous ghost, but it wasn't alone — more than 40 solar manufacturers went bankrupt in 2012 alone. The problem was not government subsidies or lack of investor enthusiasm; it was that most cleantech companies neglected one or more of the seven critical questions.
- **The Seven Questions Every Startup Must Answer**:
  1. **The Engineering Question**: Can you create breakthrough technology — not incremental improvement, but something 10x better? Cleantech solar companies often offered only 2x improvement over incumbents, which wasn't enough to overcome switching costs.
  2. **The Timing Question**: Is now the right time to start your particular business? Companies that bet on solar in 2005 didn't realize that Chinese manufacturing would drive panel prices down 90% — making their business model obsolete.
  3. **The Monopoly Question**: Are you starting with a big share of a small market? Cleantech companies competed in enormous markets (global energy) from day one — the opposite of the right strategy.
  4. **The People Question**: Do you have the right team? Many cleantech CEOs were "sales guys" (a telltale sign Thiel treats as a red flag). The right team for a hard tech company is technical founders who understand the technology deeply.
  5. **The Distribution Question**: Do you have a way to deliver your product? Cleantech companies often had no clear path to customers — particularly in utility-scale markets where sales cycles are years long and incumbents are entrenched.
  6. **The Durability Question**: Will your market position be defensible in 10–20 years? Clean energy markets are commodity markets; without proprietary technology, there's no durable advantage.
  7. **The Secret Question**: Have you identified a unique opportunity that others don't see? The best cleantech companies (e.g., Tesla) had a specific, contrarian insight — Tesla's was that a premium electric sports car could subsidize mass-market vehicles while building brand and technology simultaneously.
- **Tesla as the Exception**: Tesla answered all seven questions correctly. Proprietary technology (10x better battery + drivetrain), right timing (2003, before EV market existed), started with luxury market (small and dominated), technical founder team (Elon Musk), direct sales model, long-term defensibility through brand and software, and a specific secret (most people assumed EVs had to be inferior compromises).
- **The Myth of Social Entrepreneurship**: Companies that put "social mission" first often ignore the hard business questions. Good intentions don't create value; hard technical and business work does. A company that genuinely solves an energy problem creates more social good than a company that prioritizes "impact" over execution.

## Core Message

Real problems don't guarantee real companies. The cleantech failure shows that working on something important is not enough — you must also answer the engineering, timing, monopoly, people, distribution, durability, and secret questions correctly. Any one missing answer can be fatal.
