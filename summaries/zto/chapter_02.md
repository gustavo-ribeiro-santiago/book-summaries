# Chapter 2: Party Like It's 1999 — Zero to One

## Overview

This chapter revisits the dot-com bubble of 1999–2000 — its causes, its collapse, and the four dogmas it left behind — and argues that those dogmas, while understandable reactions to excess, are the wrong lessons to draw. The most contrarian thing is not to reject the crowd but to think for yourself.

## Key Concepts

- **The Bubble in Context**: The late 1990s were not simply irrational exuberance. The global backdrop — ruble crisis, Asian financial crises, collapsing European currencies — made the internet seem like the *only* viable path forward. Dot-com mania (September 1999–March 2000) was intense but brief.
- **PayPal's Experience**: Thiel ran PayPal through the bubble — raising money amid insanity, building an email payment product, paying users $10 to sign up to achieve critical mass, and closing a $100M funding round just as the bubble popped in March 2000. The story illustrates both the madness and the genuine opportunity of the era.
- **The Four Lessons (and Why They're Wrong)**:
  - *Make incremental advances* → Actually: it's better to risk boldness than triviality.
  - *Stay lean and flexible ("iterate")* → Actually: a bad plan is better than no plan.
  - *Improve on the competition* → Actually: competitive markets destroy profits.
  - *Focus on product, not sales* → Actually: sales matters just as much as product.
- **The Peak of Clarity**: March 2000 was obviously a peak of insanity, but less obviously it was also a peak of clarity — people understood that enormously valuable new technology needed to be built and believed themselves capable of building it. That clarity should not be discarded along with the hype.
- **The Real Lesson**: Don't mechanically oppose the crowd's mistakes. Don't replace one dogma with another. Ask yourself: how much of what you know about business is shaped by mistaken reactions to past mistakes? The most contrarian thing is to think for yourself.

## Core Message

The post-crash dogmas of Silicon Valley — incremental steps, no planning, compete aggressively, product over sales — were reasonable emotional reactions to a real bubble but are systematically wrong as business principles. The entrepreneurs who will build the future need to think past both the mania and the backlash to ask what is actually true.
