# Chapter 7: Follow the Money — Zero to One

## Overview

The power law — where a small number of things generate radically more value than everything else combined — governs venture capital, startup outcomes, and career decisions alike. Understanding it is the single most important insight for anyone who interacts with the startup world, yet most people don't see it because they're not looking.

## Key Concepts

- **The Power Law Defined**: Exponential equations describe severely unequal distributions. The most destructive earthquakes are more powerful than all smaller ones combined. The biggest cities dwarf all towns combined. Monopoly businesses capture more value than millions of undifferentiated competitors. This is the law of the universe.
- **The Power Law in Venture Capital**: The best VC investment in a fund will equal or exceed all others combined. The best company in a portfolio will outperform the entire rest. A single Founders Fund investment in Facebook returned more than all other funds of that era combined. This is not the exception — it is the rule.
- **What This Means for VCs**:
  - Every investment must be a potential fund-returner. It makes no sense to invest in companies with a ceiling.
  - Diversification across many "good enough" bets is the wrong strategy. If you know your best company will dominate, put as much as possible into it.
  - The failure of most startups is irrelevant if the winners win big enough.
- **What This Means for Founders and Employees**:
  - The most important decision is which company to join, not what title or salary to negotiate. Being at a breakout company as employee #100 may beat being a co-founder of a mediocre one.
  - Don't spread yourself across many startups. One thing done obsessively well, at the right company, is worth more than portfolio diversification of time.
- **What This Means for Career Decisions**: Every individual has just one career — you can't diversify like a portfolio. The power law means that a few choices matter immensely while most are noise. Choose your company and your role with the understanding that you're making a 0 to 1 or 1 to n bet.
- **We Don't Live in a Normal World**: Most people intuitively operate with normal distributions and expected values. But startups — and careers, and many other domains — obey power laws. The outlier isn't just a slightly better outcome; it is orders of magnitude better. Failing to see this leads to systematically bad decisions.

## Core Message

The power law is the law of the startup world: a small number of companies will generate the vast majority of returns, a small number of decisions will determine the vast majority of outcomes. The implication is not to diversify but to concentrate — to find the exceptional company, the exceptional opportunity, and bet on it fully. Following the money means following the power law.
