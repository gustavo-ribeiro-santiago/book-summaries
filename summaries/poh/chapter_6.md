### **Core idea:**

**Organizations do not run on perfect rationality—they run on habits, routines, and fragile “truces” between power centers; crises can shatter bad truces and force better ones, for better or worse.**

Put simply: **your company’s real operating system is unwritten patterns—and a serious shock is sometimes the only thing strong enough to rewrite them.**

---

### **The central principle**

Drawing on **Nelson and Winter**, Duhigg portrays firms as bundles of **organizational habits**: repeated ways departments coordinate, defer, negotiate, and avoid conflict. Those routines are not evil; they are **how large groups survive complexity without debating every move**.

But habits can also **encode cowardice**: unspoken agreements to stay in one’s lane, not embarrass another department, not escalate a warning—**even when the cost is patient safety or public catastrophe**. The chapter’s moral tension is sharp: **balanced truces keep peace; they do not guarantee responsibility**.

---

### **Rhode Island Hospital: culture as a surgical risk**

At Rhode Island Hospital, a toxic hierarchy—**physicians overriding or dismissing nurses**—contributed to a catastrophic wrong-site surgery on an **86-year-old man**, who later died. The horror is not “one bad apple”; it is **a culture where challenge was unsafe**, so checks failed in the very moments checks exist to save lives.

The case study forces a painful question: **what habits does your organization reward?** Deference? Speed? Never making the star surgeon uncomfortable?

After scandal and pressure, the hospital moved toward reforms such as **video monitoring**, **checklists**, and **anonymous reporting**—attempts to **replace informal truces with explicit accountability**.

---

### **King’s Cross fire: truces that killed**

The **1987 King’s Cross Underground fire** killed **31 people**. Duhigg uses it to show how **departmental boundaries** can become moral blinders:

- A **ticket clerk** hesitated to escalate a visible fire risk—partly because habits and hierarchy discouraged crossing channels.
- **Sprinkler systems** and responsibilities were tangled; **knowledge did not convert into coordinated action**.
- The **fire brigade** insisted on its own equipment protocols while precious minutes passed—another truce-like ritual prioritizing turf over outcomes.

The fire is not a story about villains smirking in rooms; it is about **ordinary people following ordinary routines** in a system where **no one habit made “this is my job to stop” obvious enough**.

---

### **Balanced truces vs. ultimate responsibility**

Organizations need routines—**without them, nothing ships**. But Duhigg’s warning is that **internal peace is not the same as external safety**.

A “balanced truce” between departments can mean:

- We don’t fight in meetings.
- We don’t embarrass each other publicly.
- We don’t challenge the most powerful voice in the room.

That calm can coexist with **catastrophic irresponsibility** unless **someone**—a role, a process, a leader—has **clear, enforced ultimate responsibility** for the outcome that cannot be fragmented away.

---

### **Crisis as a lever—and its ethical shadow**

Rahm Emanuel’s line—**“You never want a serious crisis to go to waste”**—captures a political and managerial truth Duhigg explores: **when normal inertia is strongest, fear and publicity can briefly open a window** for habit change.

Good leaders use that window to install:

- **New reporting lines**
- **Hard tools** (checklists, cameras, audits)
- **Cultural permission** to speak up

The chapter also implies an uncomfortable corollary: **some leaders learn to prolong a sense of crisis** to push reforms that would never survive comfortable times. That can be **necessary surgery** or **manipulation**, depending on intent and truthfulness—but the mechanism is the same: **habits change faster when the old story is no longer credible**.

---

### **Design, not only accident**

The chapter title promises **accident and design**. The accidents are fires, wrong-site surgeries, deaths. The design question is: **will you wait for the accident**, or **build habits that make catastrophes less likely**—even when no headline is watching?

Design means **practicing conflict-safe challenge**, **rehearsing emergencies**, and **making escalation honorable**, not “dramatic.”

---

### **The emotional truth**

It is unsettling to realize how much of institutional life is **unspoken compromise**—and how often the people at the bottom see the risk first but cannot stop it.

The emotional takeaway is not cynicism; it is **sober responsibility**: if you lead, **your job includes protecting the truce that lets people tell you the truth**. If you follow, **your integrity may cost you comfort**—and sometimes that cost is the price of someone else’s life.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Organizations run on **habits and truces**; **crises expose weak ones**—and skilled leaders use (and sometimes sustain) urgency to **replace dangerous routines** with accountability, clarity, and responsibility that outlast the headlines.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Prevent “everyone assumed someone else would handle it” failures**

Then:

- **Name an owner** for cross-boundary risks—someone whose success metric is the outcome, not the department’s pride.
- **Train escalation** until it feels normal, not mutinous.
- **Run drills** that expose where truces hide information.

If you want to:

- **Reform a toxic culture after harm**

Then:

- **Change structures**, not only slogans (checklists, reporting, monitoring where appropriate).
- **Protect whistleblowers and nurses and frontline staff**—challenge must be safe or it will not happen.
- **Measure what you fear**, not only what flatters quarterly numbers.

If you want to:

- **Lead through crisis without burning trust**

Then:

- **Use the window of attention** for durable fixes, not permanent panic.
- **Tell the truth** about what broke—habits, incentives, power—not only “bad apples.”

If you want to:

- **Understand why smart groups do stupid things**

Then:

- **Map the truces**: who is not allowed to embarrass whom, and what truth dies in that silence.

**Serious crises can waste you—or they can force your organization to finally replace the habits that were always too dangerous to admit.**
