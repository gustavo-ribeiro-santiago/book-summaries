### **Core idea:**

**New habits don’t stick on logic alone—they stick when your brain learns to anticipate and crave a specific reward.**

Put simply: **cue + routine + reward only becomes a habit when a craving bridges the gap between trigger and payoff.**

---

### **The central principle**

Duhigg builds on the habit loop from Chapter 1 by adding the **engine**: **craving**. The cue signals possibility; the craving is the **motivational tension** that makes the routine feel *necessary* before the reward arrives.

Without craving, a behavior stays **optional**. With it, the loop tightens into something that feels automatic—because the brain starts **rehearsing the reward** in advance.

---

### **Claude Hopkins, Pepsodent, and the film on your teeth**

Hopkins sold Pepsodent by teaching people to notice a **cue**: the **film** on their teeth. The promised **reward** was **beautiful, clean teeth**—a visible, social payoff.

The campaign worked not only because it created a loop but because it gave people something **concrete to notice** and a **clear payoff story**. Many competitors had failed by selling abstract “dental hygiene”; Hopkins sold a **daily trigger** and a **mirror-check reward**.

---

### **The real secret: craving the feeling, not just the outcome**

People did not fall in love with brushing because they loved chemistry—they fell for the **tingling** Pepsodent left behind. That mild sensation became the **proxy reward** the brain learned to **want again**.

**Craving is often directed at a *sensation* or *state*** (clean mouth, minty cool, “fresh” feeling) more than at the abstract long-term benefit. If you want exercise to stick, the chapter’s logic says you must learn to **crave the immediate hit**—for many, the **endorphin rush** or the post-workout calm—not only distant goals like “health in twenty years.”

---

### **Julio the monkey and anticipating reward**

Neuroscientist Wolfram Schultz’s work (including experiments with a monkey nicknamed Julio) showed how **dopamine-related signaling** shifts: at first the brain spikes when the **reward** arrives; with training, it spikes when the **cue** appears—because the animal **anticipates** the juice.

That anticipation **is** craving in neural form: **the promise of reward becomes its own motivator**. The routine is how you close the gap between promise and delivery.

---

### **Why “knowing better” is not the same as “wanting next time”**

Education tells you *what* is good for you; craving tells your nervous system *what to reach for*. That is why campaigns that only lecture fail, and why products that attach a **felt difference**—coolness, sparkle, calm, pride—outlast abstract virtue.

If you are building a personal habit, translate your values into **sensory bookmarks**: a playlist, a post-workout drink, a checklist click, a streak counter. You are not being shallow—you are **giving the reward system something to anticipate**.

---

### **Febreze: when there is no cue, there is no loop**

P&G’s Febreze initially flopped because many people **could not smell their own odors**—there was **no reliable cue** that something was “wrong,” so no itch to reach for the product.

It succeeded when marketing reframed the product around a **reward after cleaning**: a **pleasant finishing scent** that made the house feel “done.” The loop became: **finish chores → spray → enjoy the signal of completion**. The **reward** carried the habit; the brand had to **engineer a visible, crave-able payoff** people could notice and want again.

---

### **The emotional truth**

Hope lives in this chapter: **you are allowed to use honest, immediate pleasures**—tingle, scent, endorphins, small wins—as the glue for better routines. Dignity does not require suffering; it requires **designing a craving you can live with**.

---

### **The chapter’s principle**

> **Cue + routine + reward** explains the shape of a habit; **craving**—the anticipation of the reward—is what makes the loop repeat until it feels automatic.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Create a new habit that actually lasts**

Then:

- **Pick a cue** you will encounter reliably.
- **Define the routine** in small, repeatable steps.
- **Attach a reward** your senses or emotions can register *today*.
- **Deliberately cultivate craving** for that reward (same time, same payoff, until anticipation kicks in).

If you want to:

- **Diagnose why a “good” habit won’t stick**

Then:

- Ask whether there is a **clear cue** (if not, your brain never gets pinged).
- Ask whether the **reward is real to you** (not just morally “correct”).
- Ask what **concrete sensation or state** you could learn to **anticipate**—that is where craving is built.

If you want to:

- **Apply this to products, teams, or personal systems**

Then:

- **Make the trigger obvious** and the **payoff immediate and sensory** when possible.
- Remember: **people repeat what they learn to want in advance**, not what they merely approve of in theory.

**Craving is the missing instruction between “I should” and “I do”—design for anticipation, not just intention.**
