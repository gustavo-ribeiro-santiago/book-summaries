### **Core idea:**

**Stories are remembered and retold in ways that facts alone cannot achieve. Data communication that is structured as a story — with a beginning, middle, and end — captures attention, creates emotional resonance, and drives action in a way that bullet-pointed facts cannot.**

Put simply: **the same data presented as bullet points is forgettable. The same data structured as a story — with tension, stakes, and a resolution — is retained and motivates action.**

---

### **The central principle**

McKee (renowned screenwriting lecturer) distinguishes two ways to persuade: conventional rhetoric (bulleted facts and statistics) and story. With conventional rhetoric, the audience argues back in their heads; even if persuaded, it's only intellectual. People are not inspired to act by reason alone. Story, by contrast, unites an idea with an emotion — it bypasses intellectual resistance and engages the audience at a deeper level.

The magic of *Red Riding Hood* as a framing device: after hearing the story dozens of times, most people can still recount its plot, twists, and resolution. That retention and reproducibility is precisely what we want our data communications to achieve.

---

### **Narrative structure: beginning, middle, end**

Aristotle's three-act structure, refined as setup/conflict/resolution, is the template:

**Beginning (plot)**: Set the context. Introduce the *setting*, the *main character* (framed in terms of your audience — *they* are the hero), the *imbalance* (why this situation requires attention; what has changed), the *desired state* (what success looks like), and the *solution path* (how you will get there). Answer the audience's implicit question: "Why should I pay attention? What's in this for me?"

**Middle (twists)**: Build the case for action. Develop the problem, introduce external context, provide examples and data that demonstrate the issue, articulate what happens if nothing is done, propose options, illustrate the benefits of the recommended solution. Everything here serves to convince the audience that the change you are recommending is necessary and achievable. Keep them in mind: what motivates *them* — making money, saving resources, beating competitors, learning a skill?

**End (call to action)**: Make it completely clear what you want your audience to *do* with the new understanding you've given them. Tie the ending back to the beginning — restate the problem, remind them of the urgency, send them off ready to act. The ending should produce a clear decision or action, not just a vague sense that "that was interesting."

---

### **Conflict and tension are not optional**

A story where everything is fine and expected to stay fine is not compelling. Conflict — the gap between *what is* and *what could be* (Nancy Duarte's framing) — is the engine of engagement. If you find yourself thinking "I don't have a problem," reconsider: you're withholding the most powerful tool you have for holding your audience's attention. Frame the tension in terms of your audience's problem — they must have a stake in the solution.

---

### **Narrative flow: the order of your story**

Two primary options:

- **Chronological**: Take the audience through the analytical process in the order you experienced it. Works well when you need to establish credibility or when the audience cares about process. Builds naturally to the recommendation.
- **Lead with the ending**: Start with the call to action, then back into the supporting evidence. Works when trust is already established or the audience wants the "so what" immediately. Has the added benefit of orienting the audience to the lens they should apply while listening to the rest.

Tell your audience which approach you're using — especially if leading with the ending deviates from expectations. "I'm going to start with what we're asking of you, then walk through the analysis that led us there."

---

### **The power of repetition**

*Red Riding Hood* stays in memory partly because it was heard many times. Important information moves from short-term memory to long-term memory through repeated exposure. The *Bing, Bang, Bongo* structure leverages this: tell them what you're going to tell them (intro), tell them (content), tell them what you told them (summary/conclusion). Your audience hears the core message three times — they are far more likely to retain it.

Related tactics:
- **Repeatable sound bites**: succinct phrases that are easy to recall and repeat ("Death to pie charts!"). If people can recall, repeat, and transfer your message, you succeeded.
- **Write the headlines first**: before building slides or documents, write the action title for each section on a Post-it. Rearrange until the sequence tells a coherent story. These become your slide titles or section headers.

---

### **Four tactics for ensuring clarity**

1. **Horizontal logic**: Reading only the slide titles (action titles, not descriptive titles) should tell the entire story in sequence. Test by doing exactly that — flip through your deck and read only the titles.

2. **Vertical logic**: Everything on a single slide is self-reinforcing. The visual supports the title. The words support the visual. Nothing is extraneous. No unrelated information. What you cut is as important as what you include.

3. **Reverse storyboarding**: Take the finished communication, flip through it, write down the main point from each page. Does the resulting list look like the story you intended to tell? If not, rearrange, add, or remove.

4. **Fresh perspective**: Give the final communication to someone without context. Ask them to narrate their thought process — where they focus, what they think is important, where they have questions. This tells you immediately whether the story you intended is the one they received.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> The main character in every story you tell should be your audience. Frame the problem as their problem, the solution as their opportunity, the call to action as their decision to make. When the story is about them — not about you — they are engaged, invested, and ready to act.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Make your data communication memorable and action-driving**

Then:

- **Structure it as a story: beginning (plot/context), middle (data + why it matters), end (clear call to action).** Every communication — even a short email — benefits from this structure.
- **Lead with or end with the Big Idea** (from Chapter 1) — but either way, make sure the main point appears at least twice.

If you want to:

- **Know if your story is working**

Then:

- **Apply horizontal logic** (read only the titles), **vertical logic** (check that each slide's content supports its title), and **reverse storyboarding** (write down the main point from each page and see if it forms a coherent narrative).

**"No longer will you just show data. Rather, you will tell a story with data."**
