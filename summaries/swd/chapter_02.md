### **Core idea:**

**There are only a handful of chart types you actually need — choosing the right one means matching the visual to the relationship you want to show, not to what looks impressive.**

Put simply: **the best graph is whatever will be easiest for your audience to read and understand. Start with that question, not with "what looks cool."**

---

### **The central principle**

When reviewing 150+ visuals created over a year of real work, only a dozen distinct chart types appeared. These are the visuals that cover the vast majority of everyday communication needs. Mastery of this limited toolkit — used deliberately — outperforms novelty every time.

---

### **The main chart types and when to use them**

**Simple text**: When you have only one or two numbers to communicate, use the numbers themselves prominently, with a few supporting words. Tables and bar charts add structure that obscures the impact of a strong number.

**Tables**: Best for mixed audiences who each need different rows of data, or when multiple units of measure must be shown together. Tables interact with the verbal system — people *read* them. Minimize borders (make them light grey or remove them); let the data stand out, not the structure.

**Heatmaps**: A special case of tables that adds color saturation as a visual cue, allowing faster identification of high and low values than reading raw numbers allows.

**Scatterplots**: Useful for showing the relationship between two variables simultaneously. Less common in business settings but valuable when the relationship itself is the point.

**Line graphs**: Best for continuous data, especially over time. The connected line implies a relationship between points, so use only when that continuity is meaningful. Keep time intervals consistent on the x-axis. Variants: standard line (single or multiple series); slopegraph (two time points or comparison states, showing relative increase/decrease across categories).

**Bar charts**: The workhorse for categorical data. Easy for our eyes because we compare end points along a common baseline. **Critical rule: bar charts must always have a zero baseline** — a non-zero baseline artificially inflates or deflates visual differences. Bar chart variants: vertical bar (single/multi-series); stacked vertical bar (compare totals while showing subcomponents — use with caution, harder to read beyond the bottom series); waterfall chart (show a starting point, increases, decreases, and ending point); horizontal bar (best single go-to for categorical data — category names read left-to-right, structure naturally leads the eye to labels before data); stacked horizontal bar (useful for survey Likert scale data, with consistent baselines on both ends).

**Area graphs**: Generally avoid them — humans are poor at ascribing value to 2D space. The one exception: square area graphs for comparing numbers of vastly different magnitudes, where a second dimension helps.

---

### **Chart types to avoid**

**Pie charts**: The human eye is poor at comparing angles and areas. Slices of similar size are nearly impossible to rank. 3D pie charts introduce perspective distortion that can make a smaller slice appear larger. Replace pies with horizontal bar charts, which give a common baseline for easy comparison.

**Donut charts**: Ask the audience to compare arc lengths — even harder than comparing pie angles. Avoid.

**3D charts**: Never use 3D unless you are literally plotting a third dimension of data. 3D skews values, adds unnecessary chart elements (side panels, floor panels), and causes graphing applications to plot values in non-intuitive ways. The only rule needed: *never use 3D.*

**Secondary y-axes**: When two datasets with different units are plotted together, the dual-axis format is confusing and can imply a relationship that doesn't exist. Alternatives: (1) label the secondary data series directly on the graph, or (2) split into two separate graphs with a shared x-axis.

---

### **Logical ordering of categorical data**

When categories have no natural order, organize by data value (descending or ascending) to provide a construct for the audience. For ordinal categories (age groups, satisfaction ratings), preserve the natural order and use other means (color, callouts) to direct attention.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> There is rarely a single "correct" visual for a given dataset. What matters is asking: what do I need my audience to know? Then choose whatever makes that clearest.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Always choose the right chart**

Then:

- **Show it to someone else** — ask where their eyes go, what they see, what they conclude. This tells you whether the chart is working.

If you want to:

- **Avoid the most common mistakes**

Then:

- **Zero baselines on all bar charts. No pie charts. No 3D. No secondary y-axes.** These four rules alone eliminate the most common errors in business data visualization.

**"There is an easy way to test whether your visual is the right choice: create it and show it to a friend. Have them articulate where they focus, what they see, what observations they make."**
