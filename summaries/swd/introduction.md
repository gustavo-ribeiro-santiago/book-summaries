### **Core idea:**

**Nobody sets out to make a bad graph, but bad graphs are everywhere — because we have powerful tools to create visuals but no training in how to tell stories with data, and our tools have no idea what our story is.**

Put simply: **the gap between "I can put data into Excel and get a chart" and "I can communicate effectively with data" is enormous — and this book exists to close it.**

---

### **The central principle**

Technology democratized data visualization: anyone can create a chart. But ease of creation is not the same as effectiveness. Tools apply defaults that often work against clear communication. Without a framework — without someone teaching how to pair the language of numbers with the language of story — most people default to showing all the data they have rather than telling the story that matters.

---

### **Why this gap exists**

School teaches language (how to put words into sentences and stories) and math (how to make sense of numbers) as separate disciplines. The combination — telling stories with numbers — is almost never taught. This creates adults who are comfortable with data or comfortable with narrative, but rarely both simultaneously.

---

### **What this book teaches: 6 lessons**

1. **Understand the context** — know your audience and what you need them to do before touching a chart
2. **Choose an appropriate visual display** — different questions call for different chart types
3. **Eliminate clutter** — every element on a page consumes cognitive load; remove what doesn't earn its place
4. **Focus your audience's attention** — use design to direct where the eye goes
5. **Think like a designer** — form follows function; aesthetics and accessibility matter
6. **Tell a story** — structure your communication with a beginning, middle, and end

---

### **Who this is for**

Anyone who communicates something to someone using data: analysts presenting findings, managers communicating strategy, nonprofit leaders proving impact, students visualizing thesis data. The skills are fundamental — not industry-specific, not role-specific — and demonstrably teachable.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> There is a story in your data. But your tools don't know what that story is. That's where it takes you — the analyst or communicator — to bring that story visually and contextually to life.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Stop showing data and start communicating with it**

Then:

- **Learn the six lessons in this book** — they are a practical, tool-agnostic framework that closes the gap between "I made a chart" and "I told a compelling story."
- **Recognize that the communication step is the only part of the analytical process your audience ever sees** — it deserves time, thought, and craft.

**"There is a story in your data. But your tools don't know what that story is."**
