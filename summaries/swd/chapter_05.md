### **Core idea:**

**Form follows function. Before designing a visualization, define what you want your audience to *do* with the data — then create a form that makes doing that as easy as possible. Good design fades into the background; bad design makes your audience feel the burden.**

Put simply: **you don't need to be a professional designer. But you do need to think about affordances, accessibility, and aesthetics — because more aesthetic designs are not just prettier, they are perceived as easier to use and are more readily accepted.**

---

### **The central principle**

Great product design features *affordances*: aspects of the design that make it obvious how the object should be used (a knob affords turning, a button affords pushing). OXO kitchen gadgets are the canonical example — designed so there's really only one correct way to pick them up. Applied to data visualization: design your communications so there is only one natural way to interact with them, and that way is the one you intended.

---

### **Affordances in data visualization**

Three specific strategies for making visuals that afford understanding:

**1. Highlight the important stuff**
Highlighting works only when it's sparse — recommended maximum 10% of the visual design. Highlighting tools in descending strength: bold (preferred — minimal noise, clear signal), italics (less noise, less standout), underlining (adds noise — use sparingly), uppercase text (good for titles and labels, scannable), color (effective when used sparingly and alongside other cues), size (signals importance through scale).

Preattentive attributes can be layered: if something is truly critical, make it large, colored, *and* bold simultaneously.

**2. Eliminate distractions**
Every elimination causes what remains to stand out more. Ask: "Would removing this change anything important?" No? Remove it. Candidate distractions: data that isn't critical to the story, detail that can be summarized, redundant elements, elements that are cute but don't serve the communication. Non-critical context should be pushed to the background (light grey) or moved to an appendix.

**3. Create a clear visual hierarchy**
Layer elements so the audience knows what to process first, second, third. This is done through the same preattentive tools: size (larger = more important), color (bolder = higher priority), position (top-left = seen first). Super-categories (grouping labels like "age," "race," "income") can organize complex data and reduce cognitive effort — the audience doesn't have to derive the grouping from raw data.

### **Accessibility**

When people struggle to understand something — a confusing graph, a cluttered slide — they blame themselves. This is wrong: difficulty of understanding is almost always a design failure, not a user failure. The onus is on the *designer* to make the visual accessible to people of varying technical backgrounds.

**Don't overcomplicate**: Research shows that harder-to-read fonts make tasks seem harder and less worth attempting. The same applies to cluttered, complex-looking visuals. If it looks complicated, your audience assumes it *is* complicated and disengages before trying.

Principles: use a consistent, legible font; choose simple language; favor fewer words over more; define jargon; spell out acronyms; remove unnecessary complexity in every form.

**Text is your friend**: Every chart needs a title; every axis needs a title (exceptions are extremely rare). Don't assume two people looking at the same visual will draw the same conclusion — if there's a specific conclusion you want your audience to reach, *state it in words*.

**Action titles on slides**: The slide title bar is the first thing your audience sees. Don't waste it on descriptive labels ("2015 Budget"). Use it for action titles: what you want the audience to know or do ("Estimated 2015 spending is above budget"). The audience won't miss your main point if it's the first thing they read.

**Annotation**: Add context directly to the visual at the relevant data point. Annotations explain nuances, highlight what to pay attention to, or describe external factors — reducing the cognitive work of connecting text to data elsewhere on the page.

---

### **Aesthetics**

People perceive more aesthetic designs as easier to use — whether or not they actually are. Studies show aesthetic designs are more readily accepted, promote creative thinking, and foster tolerance of problems (people will overlook issues in a design they find beautiful). Spending time on aesthetics is not vanity — it is a practical strategy for getting your message across.

Three components of aesthetic design in data visualization:

- **Smart use of color**: intentional, sparse, strategic
- **Alignment**: organize elements to create clean horizontal and vertical lines that signal unity and cohesion; the center-aligned title that floats disconnected from everything else is the most common alignment failure
- **White space**: preserve margins; never stretch graphics to fill space; never add content because space is available

---

### **Acceptance**

Even well-designed visuals face resistance when they look different from what an audience is used to. Strategies for gaining acceptance:

- **Articulate the benefits** of the new approach — give people transparency into *why* it's better
- **Show the side-by-side** before/after comparison — let the evidence persuade
- **Provide options and seek input** — rather than prescribing, present alternatives and involve the audience in the choice
- **Get a vocal advocate** — identify an influential audience member, win them over individually, let their endorsement carry momentum

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Design that works fades into the background. Your audience doesn't notice the alignment, the white space, or the color logic — they just find the visual easy to understand. That invisibility is the mark of success.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Make your visuals immediately clear**

Then:

- **Highlight only the most important ~10% of the visual** — the rest is either grey background or eliminated.
- **State your conclusion explicitly in text** — never assume the audience will draw the same conclusion you drew.
- **Use action titles** — the first thing they read should tell them what to think or do.

If you want to:

- **Overcome resistance to new approaches**

Then:

- **Show the before-and-after side by side** and find one vocal champion before going wide.
