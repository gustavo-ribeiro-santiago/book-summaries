### **Core idea:**

**Preattentive attributes — size, color, position, intensity, and others — are processed by the brain before conscious thought kicks in. Used strategically and sparingly, they allow you to direct your audience's attention to exactly where you want it, without them realizing it's happening.**

Put simply: **your audience has about 3–8 seconds to decide whether to engage with your visual. Preattentive attributes determine where their eyes go in those seconds — design them intentionally or lose control of the story.**

---

### **The central principle**

You don't see with your eyes — you see with your brain. Visual processing begins in iconic memory (ultra-fast, pre-conscious), passes through short-term memory (limited to ~4 chunks at a time), and eventually reaches long-term memory (where things stick). Preattentive attributes exploit iconic memory: they cause certain elements to register before the audience has consciously decided to look at them.

**The count-the-3s test** demonstrates this viscerally: finding 3s in a block of undifferentiated numbers is slow and effortful. Bold one digit in a contrasting color and the 3s leap out before you can blink. This is preattentive processing.

---

### **The preattentive attributes**

Attributes processed before conscious attention is engaged include: **orientation, shape, line length, line width, size, curvature, number, motion, spatial grouping, color (hue), intensity (brightness), position (2D)**.

Not all carry quantitative meaning. Bar charts work because line length intuitively implies magnitude — longer = more. Color (hue) does not: "which is greater, red or blue?" is a meaningless question. Use quantitative attributes (length, position, size, intensity) for data encoding; use categorical attributes (hue) for labeling and highlighting.

---

### **Two strategic uses**

1. **Draw attention to where you want your audience to look** — make the one important thing visually distinct from everything else.
2. **Create a visual hierarchy** — establish an implicit order that tells your audience what to process first, second, third, so they always encounter the most important thing first even if they spend only a few seconds on your visual.

---

### **Size**

Relative size communicates relative importance. If something is big, audiences assume it's important. This can work for you (make the most important data point the largest) or against you (information that takes up 60% of dashboard real estate will be treated as most important, regardless of whether it actually is). Always make size decisions intentionally — never let them be accidental artifacts of what data was available first.

---

### **Color**

The most powerful preattentive attribute — and the most frequently misused.

**Use it sparingly**: Color's preattentive power comes from contrast. When everything is colored, nothing stands out — the "rainbow table" problem. Design in shades of grey and use one bold color to direct attention. Grey is the preferred base (not black) because it provides more contrast for the standout color.

**Use it consistently**: A change in color signals a change. Audiences learn what your colors mean after one exposure and apply that learning throughout the communication. Never use the same color for different concepts in the same communication.

**Design for colorblindness**: ~8% of men are colorblind, most commonly unable to distinguish red from green. Avoid red-green combinations, or pair them with additional cues (bold, shape, plus/minus signs). Blue and orange is a safer pair with similar positive/negative connotation.

**Be mindful of tone**: Color evokes emotion. Clinical content calls for clinical colors (bold black, not friendly blues). A playful article can use hot pink and teal. Match the color to the intended emotional register.

**Brand colors**: Identify one or two brand colors to use as attention signals; keep everything else grey. If brand colors are too muted to create contrast, use bold black instead — clarity always wins over brand compliance.

---

### **Position on page**

Without other cues, audiences start at the top left and scan in zigzag "Z" patterns across the page. Top of the page is the most valuable real estate: put the most important thing there. If something is important, don't make the audience wade through other content to reach it — put it at the top.

Avoid organizing information in ways that require the audience to read in an unnatural direction (bottom-to-top, right-to-left) — this creates friction and discomfort that works against you.

---

### **The "where are your eyes drawn?" test**

After creating a visual, look away and then look back, noting where your eyes land first. Do they go to where you want the audience to focus? Better yet, ask a colleague to talk through their eye path. This test reveals whether your preattentive attributes are working as intended.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Without visual cues, your audience is left to process everything. Ease this by leveraging preattentive attributes to signal what's important, create hierarchy, and guide the eye through the information in the order you intend.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Control where your audience looks**

Then:

- **Design in grey first.** Push everything to the background, then selectively bring forward only the most important elements. This forces intentional decisions about what deserves attention.
- **Use one bold color sparingly.** One or two colored data series in a sea of grey will command attention. More colors dilute this effect.

If you want to:

- **Test whether it's working**

Then:

- **Apply the "where are your eyes drawn?" test** immediately after creating any visual.

**"If we use preattentive attributes strategically, they can help us enable our audience to see what we want them to see before they even know they're seeing it."**
