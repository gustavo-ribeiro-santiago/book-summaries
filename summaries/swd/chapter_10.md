### **Core idea:**

**Data visualization sits at the intersection of science and art. The science — best practices, rules, and principles — is learnable. The art — personal style, creativity, and judgment — develops through practice. Having finished the lessons, the question becomes: how do you build these skills into lasting habits?**

Put simply: **reading about storytelling with data is step one. Doing it — repeatedly, with feedback, with time — is the only way to actually get good at it. Here is a practical roadmap for what comes next.**

---

### **The central principle**

There is no single "right" answer in data visualization — there are multiple paths to effective communication, and different designers will make different valid choices when facing the same data. The goal is to forge your own path using the principles as guardrails, with the constant north star being: does this make the information easier for my audience to understand?

---

### **Five tips for continued growth**

**Tip 1: Learn your tools well**

The principles in this book apply to any tool — Excel, Tableau, R, D3, Python, Google Sheets, Adobe Illustrator, or paper. Don't let tool limitations become an excuse for poor communication. Pick one tool and develop deep mastery of it. When you can't figure out how to do something, don't give up — search, experiment, persist. The frustration of learning a tool is a one-time investment; the clarity it enables is permanent.

**Tip 2: Iterate and seek feedback**

The storytelling with data process is not linear — it requires iteration. When the best path is unclear, sketch on paper first. Paper reduces attachment to early ideas, making it easier to abandon them without loss. Then apply the "optometrist approach" in your tool: make version A, copy it to B, make one change, determine which is better — and repeat. At any point of uncertainty, ask a colleague to narrate their experience with the visual: where their eyes go, what they think is important, what questions they have. Fresh eyes consistently reveal problems invisible to the designer.

**Tip 3: Devote adequate time**

The communication step is the only part of the analytical process your audience sees — it deserves proportionate investment. In practice, after collecting, cleaning, and analyzing data, there is often pressure to "just put it in a chart and ship it." Resist. The defaults of your charting tool are almost never good enough. The story your data tells requires time to shape. Budget explicitly for it. Plan for this step to take longer than expected.

**Tip 4: Seek inspiration from good examples**

Imitation is not a weakness — it is how all skills are developed. Build a personal library of visualizations you admire. When you see something that works, analyze *why* it works: the color choice, the data ordering, the annotation, the emphasis decisions. Emulate those aspects in your own work. Blogs like *storytellingwithdata.com*, *FiveThirtyEight's Data Lab*, *Flowing Data*, and *Perceptual Edge* are rich ongoing sources of both good and bad examples. Learn from both equally — poor visualizations teach you what to avoid and why.

**Tip 5: Have fun and find your style**

Data can be made breathtakingly beautiful. Creativity has a role here. Personal style — consistent font choices, a characteristic color palette, a preference for minimalism or density — develops over time through experimentation. Don't be afraid to try new approaches. Some will fail, some will become signature choices. What matters is that your stylistic choices always serve the audience's ability to understand the information, not just your aesthetic preferences.

### **Building the capability in your team or organization**

Three strategies, often best combined:

**Upskill everyone**: Provide foundational training to all who visualize or present data. Tactics: book clubs (read a chapter, discuss where it applies to your work), do-it-yourself workshops (bring real examples from your team and improve them together), "Makeover Monday" challenges, peer feedback loops, monthly/quarterly contests for model examples.

**Invest in an internal expert**: Identify someone with interest and aptitude; give them deep investment (books, tools, workshops, time to practice); make them the team's internal data visualization consultant and teacher. This creates a multiplier — one person's skills spread through others over time.

**Outsource selectively**: For high-stakes, one-time communications (an important annual presentation, a critical board report), an external consultant can deliver quality you can't yet produce internally. But outsourcing without learning is a missed opportunity — watch the process, ask questions, use the output as a template to build from internally.

### **The six lessons: final recap**

1. **Understand the context**: Know your audience, what you need them to do, how you'll communicate, and what data supports the case. Use the 3-minute story, Big Idea, and storyboarding.

2. **Choose an appropriate visual display**: Simple text for one or two numbers; line charts for continuous data; bar charts for categorical data (always zero baseline); avoid pie/donut/3D/secondary y-axes.

3. **Eliminate clutter**: Use Gestalt principles to identify and remove elements that add cognitive load without adding information. Leverage alignment, white space, and strategic contrast.

4. **Focus attention where you want it**: Use preattentive attributes (color, size, position) sparingly and intentionally to signal importance and create visual hierarchy. Test with "where are your eyes drawn?"

5. **Think like a designer**: Offer affordances, ensure accessibility (don't overcomplicate, text is your friend, use action titles), pursue aesthetics, work to gain acceptance.

6. **Tell a story**: Clear beginning (plot), middle (twists), end (call to action). Use conflict and tension. Consider narrative order and manner. Leverage repetition. Check with horizontal logic, vertical logic, reverse storyboarding, and fresh perspectives.

### **The chapter's principle**

> The communication step is the only part of the entire analytical process your audience sees. This step deserves — and rewards — your full investment.

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Build these skills permanently**

Then:

- **Practice in your actual work** — look for opportunities to apply each lesson incrementally. One improved chart this week, one better presentation next week.
- **Iterate with feedback** — never finalize a visualization without showing it to at least one fresh set of eyes.
- **Devote time** — schedule the communication step explicitly, plan for it to take as long as the analysis itself.

**"There is a story in your data. Use the lessons we've covered to make that story clear to your audience. Never again will you simply show data — you will tell stories with data."**
