### **Core idea:**

**Success in data visualization does not start with data visualization — it starts with a clear understanding of who you are communicating to and what you need them to know or do. Everything else follows from that.**

Put simply: **before touching a chart, answer three questions: Who is my audience? What do I need them to know or do? What data supports that? In that order.**

---

### **The central principle**

There are two types of analysis: *exploratory* (hunting through the data to find what's interesting) and *explanatory* (communicating a specific finding to a specific audience). Most people do the exploratory work and then show it all to their audience — forcing them to reopen every oyster to find the two pearls you already found. The discipline of explanatory communication means showing only the pearls.

---

### **Who, What, How**

**Who**: Be as specific as possible about your audience. "Internal and external stakeholders" is not an audience — it's a reason to create multiple communications. Identify the decision maker. The more specific your audience, the better positioned you are to communicate effectively.

**What**: Determine what you need your audience to *do* or *know*. If you can't articulate this concisely, reconsider whether the communication is necessary. Data becomes the evidence supporting the story — not the story itself. Even when you can't recommend an action explicitly, suggest possible next steps to drive productive conversation.

**How**: Only after clarifying Who and What should you ask what data is available to make your point. The communication mechanism also matters: a live presentation gives you control (you can be sparse with slides because you're there to explain); a written document or email must stand alone (requires more detail). A "slideument" — a hybrid trying to serve both needs — typically serves neither well.

**Tone**: The mood of the communication (celebratory, urgent, serious, lighthearted) should be defined upfront, as it influences every design decision that follows.

---

### **The 3-minute story and the Big Idea**

- **3-minute story**: If you had only 3 minutes to tell your audience everything they need to know, what would you say? This exercise forces clarity on the most important parts and removes dependence on slides.
- **Big Idea**: Boil the so-what down to one complete sentence that (1) articulates your unique point of view, (2) conveys what's at stake, and (3) is a complete sentence. Everything in your communication should support this one sentence.

---

### **Storyboarding**

Plan your communication before opening presentation software. Use Post-it notes or a whiteboard — it's much easier to recycle an idea on paper than to delete something you've already built on a computer. A storyboard establishes the structure before you invest in content, reduces wasted effort, and enables stakeholder alignment on direction before execution begins.

**Key tip**: don't start with PowerPoint — it triggers slide-generation mode before you've established what the slides need to say.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> Before creating any content, be able to concisely articulate: who your audience is, what you need them to know or do, and what data supports your case. Without this foundation, even a beautiful visualization fails.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Stop creating presentations that don't land**

Then:

- **Answer the Who/What/How before opening any tool** — the communication you build will be sharper, require fewer iterations, and be more likely to drive the action you want.
- **Craft a Big Idea**: one sentence, your point of view, what's at stake, a complete sentence.

If you want to:

- **Plan more effectively**

Then:

- **Storyboard first, build second** — Post-it notes are your most powerful planning tool.

**"If you can't concisely articulate what you need your audience to know or do, you should revisit whether you need to communicate in the first place."**
