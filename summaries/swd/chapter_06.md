### **Core idea:**

**The best way to internalize what makes a visualization effective is to study excellent examples and articulate — specifically — what design choices were made and why. Every model visual embodies a set of intentional decisions; understanding those decisions builds the instincts you need for your own work.**

Put simply: **dissecting great visuals is as valuable as learning rules. When you can explain *why* something works, you can replicate and adapt it.**

---

### **The central principle**

When creating effective data visualizations, the same considerations arise again and again: how do I want my audience to process this information? What should they focus on? What should be de-emphasized? This chapter reinforces those recurring considerations through five annotated examples, each built from a specific real-world need.

---

### **Model visual #1: Line graph (giving campaign progress)**

**What it does well**: Everything is labeled — graph title, axes, data series labeled directly (no legend). Dark grey for the title ensures visibility without the sharp contrast of pure black. Multiple preattentive attributes are layered on the "Progress to date" line: color, thicker line, data marker on the final point, larger text for the label.

**Key design decisions**: Goal and prior year data are preserved for context but pushed to the background — thin lines, lighter colors, same grey as structural elements. Axis tick marks are aligned with data points. Not every day is labeled on the x-axis, only every 5th day, because the trend matters more than daily specifics.

---

### **Model visual #2: Annotated line graph with forecast**

**What it does well**: Actual and forecast data are visually distinguished — solid line vs. thinner dotted line (dotted implies less certainty). "ACTUAL" and "FORECAST" labels in all caps for easy scanning, with a light background shading behind the forecast region.

**Key design decisions**: Everything is pushed to grey *except* the title, annotated date labels (in blue), the data line, selected data markers, and forward-looking numeric labels. Data markers only appear at points referenced by annotation, making it easy to link text to data. The footnote is smaller, lower-priority, grey — present for reference but not competing.

---

### **Model visual #3: 100% stacked bars (consulting project goals)**

**What it does well**: Red (burnt red, not primary red — primary red is too loud) is used as the single attention-grabbing color for the "Miss" category. White numeric labels on red segments create stark contrast that further draws the eye. All other categories are different shades of grey — differentiated enough to be distinguished, not competing with the red.

**Key design decisions**: Categories are ordered deliberately — "Miss" at the bottom (anchored at the x-axis baseline, enabling easy comparison across time), "Exceed" at the top (also consistently anchored at the top of the graph). Words at the top right explicitly state what to pay attention to. Super-categories of years in the x-axis reduce redundant labeling.

---

### **Model visual #4: Positive and negative stacked bars (director pipeline)**

**What it does well**: Color tells the story — blue for current directors, lighter blue for attrition (same family, less saturated, falling below the axis to visually reinforce "leaving"), green for additions (positive connotation), outline-only for unmet need (visually empty = a gap).

**Key design decisions**: The order of elements within each stacked bar is deliberate. "Today's directors" is the base at the axis. Attrition falls below in negative direction. Promotions and acquisitions build upward above. "Unmet need (gap)" sits at the very top — where the eye hits first. The most important numbers (unmet need) are the only ones labeled directly, in larger, bolder text. Everything else is grey or smaller.

---

### **Model visual #5: Horizontal stacked bars (survey priorities, developing nation)**

**What it does well**: Categories are ordered in descending order of "Total %" giving the audience a clear sorting construct. The top three priorities are emphasized through consistent color across category name, total %, and stacked bars — creating visual cohesion between text and data.

**Key design decisions**: Numeric labels inside bars are deliberately de-emphasized — small text, left-oriented (creates a clean vertical line down the "Most important" column), written in a lighter color. The x-axis is eliminated entirely; specific values are labeled directly. Legend appears immediately above the first bar in the visual hierarchy.

---

### **Common threads across all five examples**

1. **Words are used well**: Every visual has a title, axis titles, and appropriate labeling. Action titles or narrative callouts tell the audience what to focus on.
2. **Emphasis and de-emphasis are deliberate**: One color draws attention; everything else is grey or a lighter variant.
3. **Ordering of categories and data series is intentional**: Categories are sorted to provide a construct (descending, natural order, or narrative-driven).
4. **Alignment is clean**: Elements are positioned to create clear vertical and horizontal lines; titles are upper-left justified.

---

### **The chapter's principle**

> There is rarely a single "right" answer in data visualization — there are flavors of good. What matters is that every design choice is intentional, understood by the designer, and in service of the audience.

---

### **Bottom line**

If you want to:

- **Improve your own design instincts**

Then:

- **Study effective visuals actively** — when you see a graph you admire, stop and articulate *specifically* what makes it work: color choices, ordering decisions, what was emphasized, what was de-emphasized, how text was used.
- **Build a visual library** of examples you want to emulate.

**"In data visualization, there is rarely a single 'right' answer. Rather, there are flavors of good. The examples we've looked at are the haute cuisine of charts."**
