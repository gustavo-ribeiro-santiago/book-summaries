# Chapter 1: Don't Bargain Over Positions — Getting to Yes

## Overview

This chapter introduces the central problem with the most common form of negotiation—positional bargaining—and explains why it consistently fails to produce wise, efficient, or amicable agreements. It sets up the case for an entirely different approach.

## Key Concepts

- **Positional Bargaining Defined**: Each side stakes out a position, argues for it, and makes incremental concessions until a compromise is reached (or talks break down). The classic example is the haggling between a customer and shopkeeper over a brass dish.
- **Three Criteria for Good Negotiation**: Any sound method should (1) produce a *wise agreement* when possible, (2) be *efficient*, and (3) *improve or at least not damage the relationship*.
- **Why Positional Bargaining Fails**:
  - **Unwise outcomes**: Locking into positions makes people defend them long after the underlying interests have shifted. Ego gets tied to position, making compromise feel like personal defeat. Example: the 1961 nuclear test-ban talks broke down over the number of annual inspections—a purely positional standoff that ignored what an "inspection" actually needed to accomplish.
  - **Inefficiency**: Extreme opening positions, small concessions, and delaying tactics all drag out the process. Every concession feels like a defeat, creating incentives to stonewall.
  - **Damaged relationships**: Positional bargaining turns negotiation into a contest of will. Resentment builds as one side feels it is bending to the other's rigid demands. Commercial relationships end; neighbors stop speaking.
  - **Worse with many parties**: In multilateral negotiations (e.g., UN conferences), positional bargaining becomes nearly impossible. Coalitions form around symbolic rather than substantive interests; any agreed position becomes harder to change as more people commit to it.
- **Soft vs. Hard Bargaining**: The table comparing the two styles shows that soft bargaining (concessions, trust, friendliness) is vulnerable to exploitation by hard bargainers. Neither approach reliably produces wise agreements.
- **The Meta-Game**: Every negotiation operates on two levels—the substance being negotiated and the *process* of how to negotiate it. Positional bargaining is itself a choice of process; you can choose to change it.
- **The Four-Point Alternative**: The chapter previews *principled negotiation*: separate people from the problem, focus on interests not positions, invent options for mutual gain, insist on objective criteria.

## Core Message

Positional bargaining is the default mode of most negotiations, yet it reliably produces worse outcomes than what's possible—wasting time, straining relationships, and locking both sides into suboptimal compromises. The solution is not to bargain harder or softer, but to change the game entirely.
