# Chapter 4: Invent Options for Mutual Gain — Getting to Yes

## Overview

The third principle of principled negotiation addresses the creative challenge at the heart of any negotiation: generating the full range of possible solutions before settling on one. Most negotiators arrive with too few options and leave value on the table as a result.

## Key Concepts

- **The Orange Parable**: Two children split an orange in half, one eating the fruit and discarding the peel, the other discarding the fruit and using the peel for baking. A simple conversation about *why* each wanted the orange would have given each child the whole of what they needed. Negotiators do this constantly—"leaving money on the table."
- **Four Obstacles to Creative Options**:
  1. **Premature judgment**: The critical mind shuts down imagination. Under negotiation pressure, any new idea feels risky.
  2. **Searching for the single answer**: The assumption that the job is to narrow the gap, not to broaden the space of possibilities.
  3. **The fixed-pie assumption**: Believing that what's good for you must be bad for them—that the pie cannot grow.
  4. **"Solving their problem is their problem"**: Emotional investment in your own side makes it hard to think creatively about meeting the other side's interests.
- **The Prescription — Four Steps**:
  1. **Separate inventing from deciding**: Brainstorm without judgment first; evaluate later. The ground rule: no criticism during the inventing phase. Wild ideas are welcome; they spark better ones.
  2. **Broaden options using the Circle Chart**: Move between specific and general in four steps—(1) the real-world problem, (2) descriptive analysis/diagnosis, (3) general theoretical approaches, (4) concrete action ideas. Use one good idea to generate others by asking what general principle it reflects and what other ideas that principle suggests.
  3. **Search for mutual gains**:
     - *Identify shared interests*: Latent in every negotiation. Make them explicit and frame them as shared goals.
     - *Dovetail differing interests*: Differences in interests, beliefs, valuation of time, forecasts, and risk aversion can all become the basis for creative trades. "Vive la différence!" One party may value form, the other substance; one the short term, the other the long term; one certainty, the other upside potential.
  4. **Make their decision easy**: Put yourself in the other side's shoes. Give them a specific, realistic proposal—a "yesable proposition." Reduce the number of approvals needed. Make the agreement easy to implement. Shape proposals to appear legitimate (consistent with precedent, principle, and their stated values).
- **Varying the Strength and Scope of Agreements**: If a comprehensive agreement is out of reach, try partial, provisional, or procedural agreements. If you can't agree on substance, agree on procedure. If you can't agree on first-order issues, at least agree on where you disagree.

## Core Message

Most negotiations are constrained not by the limits of what's possible but by the limits of what people bother to imagine. By deliberately separating the creative act from the evaluative one, systematically looking for shared and complementary interests, and designing proposals that make it easy for the other side to say yes, negotiators can routinely find better agreements than either side thought possible.
