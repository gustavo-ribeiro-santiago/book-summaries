# Chapter 3: Focus on Interests, Not Positions — Getting to Yes

## Overview

The second principle of principled negotiation shifts attention from stated positions (what people say they want) to underlying interests (why they want it). This distinction is the key to unlocking solutions that satisfy both sides and that positional bargaining can never find.

## Key Concepts

- **The Position vs. Interest Distinction**: A position is the explicit, stated demand; an interest is the underlying need, desire, concern, or fear that drives it. Two men argue about a library window—one wants it open, the other closed. The solution (opening a window in the next room) only becomes visible when the librarian asks *why* each wants what they want: fresh air vs. no draft.
- **Real-World Example — Camp David**: Israel and Egypt were deadlocked over the Sinai Peninsula, each committed to incompatible positions. Behind Israel's position lay an interest in *security*; behind Egypt's lay an interest in *sovereignty*. A demilitarized Sinai with the Egyptian flag flying over it satisfied both. No positional compromise could have found this.
- **Behind Opposed Positions Lie More Compatible Interests Than Conflicting Ones**: Shared interests (both want stability, a good relationship) and compatible-but-different interests (one wants no pets, the other wants prompt payment) usually outnumber truly opposed ones.
- **How to Identify Interests**:
  - Ask "Why?"—what need does their position serve?
  - Ask "Why not?"—what stops them from accepting what you're asking?
  - Recognize that each side has *multiple* interests, not just one, and that different people on the same side may have different interests.
  - Look for *basic human needs*: security, economic well-being, belonging, recognition, control over one's life. These are often overlooked but powerfully motivating.
- **How to Talk About Interests**:
  - **Make your interests vivid and concrete**: Vague interests don't move people; specific, credible descriptions do.
  - **Acknowledge their interests as part of the problem**: Demonstrating that you understand their interests makes them more likely to listen to yours.
  - **Put the problem before your answer**: State your interests and reasoning *before* your conclusion or proposal, so the other side is listening rather than preparing counterarguments.
  - **Look forward, not back**: Focus on what you both want to happen tomorrow, not on defending or attacking what happened yesterday.
  - **Be concrete but flexible**: Use "illustrative specificity"—specific options that show what meeting your interests would look like, while remaining open to other ways of meeting them.
  - **Be hard on the problem, soft on the people**: Commit to your interests, not your position; advocate them vigorously while supporting the person across the table.

## Core Message

Positions are the visible surface of a negotiation; interests are the hidden engine. When both sides argue over positions, they miss the vast space of possible agreements that could satisfy both sets of underlying interests. Shifting from "what we demand" to "what we actually need" opens the door to creative, durable solutions that neither side could find by bargaining alone.
