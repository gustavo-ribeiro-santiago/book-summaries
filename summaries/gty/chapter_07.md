# Chapter 7: What If They Won't Play? — Getting to Yes

## Overview

What happens when you want to negotiate on the merits but the other side insists on positional bargaining, attacks your ideas, or attacks you personally? This chapter presents *negotiation jujitsu*—a set of techniques to redirect their energy toward problem-solving rather than pushing back and escalating.

## Key Concepts

- **Three Approaches When They Won't Play**:
  1. **Keep playing the principled game yourself** — the approach is contagious; modelling it creates conditions for the other side to join.
  2. **Negotiation jujitsu** — redirect their positional moves toward the merits without pushing back.
  3. **Bring in a third party** — use mediation or the one-text procedure when direct methods fail.
- **Negotiation Jujitsu**: Rather than resisting the other side's force (which escalates), sidestep it and channel it toward the problem. Three specific counter-moves:
  - *Don't attack their position—look behind it*: Treat their position as one possible option. Ask what interests it reflects, probe how it addresses both sides' concerns, examine the principles underlying it. "What's the theory behind that figure?" A Nasser anecdote shows how asking "What would happen if Israel did that?" led Nasser himself to realize the proposal was unrealistic.
  - *Don't defend your ideas—invite criticism and advice*: "What concerns would this proposal fail to take into account?" Turns criticism from an obstacle into an ingredient. Ask them what they would do if they were in your position.
  - *Recast attacks on you as attacks on the problem*: "When you say a strike shows we don't care about children, I hear your concern for children's education—we share it. What can we both do to end this quickly?"
- **Two Key Tools**:
  - **Questions instead of statements**: Questions don't offer a target to attack; they educate and challenge. "Do you think it would be better to have teachers cooperating or actively resisting?"
  - **Silence**: If they make an unreasonable proposal or an unjustified attack, say nothing. Silence is uncomfortable and often prompts them to fill the void constructively.
- **The One-Text Procedure**: When direct negotiation stalls, a third party develops a single draft—not asking for positions but for *criticism*. Each round of critique drives improvements without requiring either side to make concessions. Drafts iterate until the mediator recommends a final text.
  - The most celebrated use: Camp David (1978), where the U.S. mediated between Egypt and Israel through 23 drafts over 13 days, producing the Camp David Accords.
  - Useful in multilateral negotiations (Law of the Sea) and in any situation where the parties are too locked in to negotiate directly.

## Core Message

When the other side won't engage on the merits, the worst response is to push back harder—that only deepens positional entrenchment. Instead, use the energy of their attack by redirecting it: look behind their positions, invite criticism of your own ideas, and reframe personal attacks as shared problems. If that fails, a skilled third party using the one-text procedure can often unlock what direct negotiation could not.
