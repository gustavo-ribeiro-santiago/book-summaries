# In Conclusion — Getting to Yes

## Overview

The conclusion is brief and pointed. It frames the entire book's purpose, acknowledges its limits, and redefines what "winning" in a negotiation really means.

## Key Concepts

- **You Already Knew This**: The principles in *Getting to Yes* codify common sense and common experience. Skilled lawyers and businesspeople, upon reading the book, often say: "Now I know what I've been doing, and why it sometimes works." The framework organizes intuitions most people already have but have never articulated.
- **Learning Requires Doing**: A book can point you in a direction; only practice makes you skillful. Reading about negotiation is like reading about swimming—you only get better by getting in the water.
- **Redefining "Winning"**: The deepest negotiation is not over substance but over process—about what kind of game to play, how to deal with each other and with shared and differing interests. Asking "Who's winning a negotiation?" is like asking who's winning a marriage: if that's the question, you've already lost the more important game.
- **Both, Not Either/Or**: The method of principled negotiation is designed so that you don't have to choose between getting what you deserve and being decent. Over the long run, it produces substantive outcomes as good as or better than any alternative strategy—while being more efficient and less damaging to relationships.

## Core Message

Principled negotiation is not a trick or a set of tactics. It is a coherent approach to dealing with differences that makes it possible to pursue your interests vigorously while remaining respectful, fair, and relationship-preserving. The goal is to win the right game—not the contest of who can be more stubborn, but the deeper challenge of finding agreements that are genuinely wise and durable.
