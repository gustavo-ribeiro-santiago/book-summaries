# Chapter 5: Insist on Using Objective Criteria — Getting to Yes

## Overview

The fourth and final principle of principled negotiation addresses the unavoidable reality of conflicting interests: when neither side can fully get what it wants, the result should be determined by fair standards—not by who is more stubborn or more powerful. This chapter explains how to find and use objective criteria to reach principled, durable agreements.

## Key Concepts

- **The Problem with Deciding by Will**: When conflicts are resolved by who holds out longest, the outcome depends on arbitrary willpower rather than on what is fair or wise. This is inefficient, adversarial, and produces agreements vulnerable to later repudiation.
- **Objective Criteria Defined**: Standards that are independent of either party's will—market value, precedent, scientific judgment, professional norms, legal standards, efficiency, equal treatment, moral standards, expert opinion, tradition, reciprocity. The key test: a criterion should be *legitimate* (accepted by a broad community) and *practical* (applicable to the actual problem).
- **Fair Procedures as an Alternative**: When fair standards for substance are hard to agree on, fair procedures can produce legitimate outcomes. Classic examples:
  - "One cuts, the other chooses" for dividing a shared resource.
  - Letting an expert arbitrate.
  - Drawing lots or taking turns.
  - "Last-best-offer arbitration" in professional baseball.
  - The Law of the Sea solution: companies propose two sites; the UN picks one, the company gets the other—harnessing the company's expertise for mutual benefit.
- **Three Principles for Negotiating with Objective Criteria**:
  1. **Frame each issue as a joint search for objective standards**: "What would a fair price be, given the following benchmarks?" shifts the conversation from battle to problem-solving.
  2. **Reason and be open to reason**: Principled negotiation is *not* insisting on your preferred standard. It means genuinely being open to better standards, behaving like a judge rather than an advocate.
  3. **Never yield to pressure, only to principle**: A bribe, a threat, an appeal to trust, or a refusal to budge are all forms of pressure. The principled response is always the same: ask for the reasoning, propose objective criteria, and refuse to move except on the basis of principle.
- **Illustrated with a Real Case**: Tom negotiates a car insurance settlement by consistently asking "What standard did you use?" and then building the case objective criteria by objective criteria (mileage, technology package, comparable listings). He walks out with $18,024 vs. the initial offer of $13,600—without ever making a positional demand.

## Core Message

When conflicting interests cannot be fully reconciled, the fairest and most durable agreements are those grounded in standards independent of either party's will. By insisting on objective criteria—and remaining genuinely open to which criteria are most appropriate—principled negotiators can hold their ground without becoming adversarial, achieve fair outcomes without being exploited, and reach agreements that both sides can defend to their own constituents.
