# Chapter 2: Separate the People from the Problem — Getting to Yes

## Overview

The first principle of principled negotiation addresses the human dimension of any negotiation. Negotiators are people first—with emotions, perceptions, and egos—and ignoring that reality is a sure path to failure. This chapter explains how to disentangle the relationship from the substance and handle the "people problems" directly.

## Key Concepts

- **Negotiators Are People First**: Beyond the substantive issue lies the human relationship. Every negotiator has two kinds of interests: in the substance *and* in the relationship. Failing to attend to the human side can derail an otherwise straightforward deal.
- **The Entanglement Problem**: The relationship and the substance routinely become tangled. Substantive proposals are heard as personal attacks; anger about a situation gets directed at the person associated with it; egos become identified with positions.
- **Three Categories of People Problems**:
  1. **Perception**: Conflict lives in people's heads, not in objective reality. To influence someone, you must understand how they see the situation—not just what the facts are. Key practices:
     - *Put yourself in their shoes*: Understand the emotional force of their viewpoint, not just its logical content.
     - *Don't deduce intentions from your fears*: We tend to assume the worst.
     - *Don't blame them for your problem*: Blame entangles people with the problem.
     - *Discuss each other's perceptions openly*.
     - *Give them a stake in the outcome* by involving them in the process—people support what they help create.
     - *Face-saving*: Help people reconcile any agreement with their principles and self-image.
  2. **Emotion**: Feelings can be more important than substance in a bitter dispute. Tactics include: acknowledge and legitimize emotions (yours and theirs), allow the other side to let off steam without reacting, use symbolic gestures (an apology, a gesture of respect), and pay attention to "core concerns" (autonomy, appreciation, affiliation, role, status).
  3. **Communication**: Three common failures—parties talking past each other (playing to the audience), not listening, and misunderstanding. Remedies: *active listening* (paraphrase their view back to them), *speak to be understood* (not to impress), *speak about yourself not about them* ("I feel let down" vs. "You broke your word"), and *speak for a purpose* (don't disclose what doesn't serve you).
- **Prevention Is Best**: Build a working relationship *before* the negotiation begins. The more quickly a stranger becomes someone you know, the smoother the negotiation. Frame the negotiation as a side-by-side joint search—literally sit on the same side of the table if possible.

## Core Message

Handling the human dimension of negotiation is not a soft add-on—it is a prerequisite for getting the substantive work done. By systematically attending to perception, emotion, and communication, negotiators can separate the person from the problem and deal with each on its own merits, making it possible to be firm on substance while remaining respectful of the person across the table.
