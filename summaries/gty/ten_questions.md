# Ten Questions People Ask About Getting to Yes

## Overview

This section, added in the second edition, directly addresses the most common objections, edge cases, and tactical questions readers raise after encountering principled negotiation. It deepens and refines the method across four broad topics: fairness, dealing with difficult people, tactics, and power.

## Key Concepts

### Questions About Fairness
- **Does positional bargaining ever make sense?** Yes, in limited situations: one-off transactions between strangers, simple single-issue negotiations where costs of exploring interests are high. But the outcome is almost always better with principled negotiation, especially when issues are complex, the relationship matters, or the negotiation will set a precedent.
- **What if the other side believes in a different standard of fairness?** Agreement on the "best" standard isn't necessary—standards are tools to find better agreements, not ultimate arbiters. When standards conflict, argue which is more directly applicable, more widely accepted, and more contextually relevant. If still unresolved, fair procedures (arbitration, splitting the difference) can resolve remaining gaps.
- **Should I be fair if I don't have to be?** The book makes a practical, not moral, argument: unfair agreements are often fragile (the other side may refuse to implement them), damage relationships, hurt your reputation, and may trouble your conscience. The windfall of "getting more than is fair" often costs more than it gains.

### Questions About Dealing with People
- **What if people ARE the problem?** Disentangle relationship issues from substantive ones and address each on its own merits. Build a working relationship independent of agreement. Don't appease—it doesn't work and signals that stubbornness pays. Don't coerce—it damages the relationship. When behavior is genuinely problematic, negotiate *about* the relationship the same way you negotiate substance.
- **Should I negotiate with terrorists or people like Hitler?** Unless you have a better BATNA, the question is not *whether* but *how*. Direct dialogue usually offers more influence than actions at a distance. Principled negotiation doesn't mean being naive—it means understanding that even with deeply unsavory parties, your interests are better served by clear-eyed engagement than by refusal that cedes influence.
- **How do I adjust for differences in personality, gender, or culture?** The four principles are broadly applicable across styles and cultures. What varies is *style*, not substance. Be alert to different norms around directness, relationship-building, and what constitutes "fair." Adapt your manner without abandoning your method—and always confirm that your counterpart's style reflects their actual interests, not just cultural convention.

### Questions About Tactics
- **Tactical questions (where to meet, who goes first, how high to start):** Prefer neutral territory or accept their turf (it can put them at ease). The first mover can anchor the negotiation—useful if you know the terrain well, risky if you don't. High opening positions can signal bad faith and damage relationships; start with a position you can justify on objective grounds.
- **How to move from inventing options to making commitments?** Don't let the brainstorming phase collapse prematurely into positional bargaining. Move gradually: conditional agreements ("If you agree to X, we would agree to Y"), then a package, then a commitment. Signal genuine interest without locking in prematurely.
- **How to try these ideas without taking too much risk?** Start with low-stakes negotiations. Disclose your interests provisionally ("Speaking personally, I care most about…"). Ask about their interests before revealing yours. Reframe aggressively if they push back into positional bargaining.

### Questions About Power
- **Can the way I negotiate make a difference if they're more powerful? How do I enhance my power?** Yes—significantly. Negotiating power is the ability to persuade someone that an agreement serves their interests. Sources of power include: your BATNA (the most important), legitimacy (having objective standards on your side), a good relationship (they trust and respect you), a well-framed solution, and commitment (your readiness to walk away). Principled negotiation maximizes all of these. Even against much more powerful parties, the method shifts the terrain from raw power to principle—where you are often better positioned.

## Core Message

Principled negotiation is not a rigid formula that breaks down at the edges. It is a robust method that adapts to different cultures, personalities, power levels, and even bad-faith opponents. The ten questions show that the method's logic—focus on interests, use objective criteria, develop your BATNA, separate people from problems—applies consistently whether you are negotiating with a terrorist, a tough corporate lawyer, or a spouse, and whether you are the more or less powerful party.
