# Chapter 6: What If They Are More Powerful? — Getting to Yes

## Overview

This chapter confronts the reality that power imbalances exist in negotiations and explains why the answer is not to match force with force—but to develop your BATNA (Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement), the single most important source of negotiating leverage.

## Key Concepts

- **No Method Guarantees Success Against Overwhelming Power**: If the other side holds all the cards, even the best negotiation technique has limits. The realistic goal is to (1) protect yourself from making an agreement you should reject, and (2) make the most of the assets you do have.
- **The Problem with Bottom Lines**: Many negotiators set a predetermined "bottom line" to protect themselves. But bottom lines are rigid by definition—they shut your ears to new information, inhibit creative solutions, tend to be set too high or too low, and are almost certain to be wrong. They are a blunt instrument.
- **BATNA — Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement**: The right standard against which to judge any proposed agreement is not a predetermined number but your best realistic alternative if the negotiation fails. BATNA is flexible, reality-tested, and naturally calibrated to your actual interests.
- **Knowing Your BATNA**:
  - Negotiate with your eyes open: if you haven't thought through your alternatives, you are vulnerable.
  - Develop your BATNA in three steps: (1) generate a list of actions you might take if no agreement is reached; (2) improve the most promising into realistic alternatives; (3) select the best one as your working BATNA.
  - The better your BATNA, the stronger your negotiating position—and the more confidently you can walk away from a bad deal.
- **A Trip Wire**: A specific agreement slightly better than your BATNA that triggers a pause and review before you go below it.
- **The Other Side's BATNA**: Understanding their alternatives is equally important. If their BATNA is very strong, they may have little incentive to negotiate seriously—consider what you can do to weaken it or improve yours relative to it.
- **Power Comes from Alternatives, Not Resources**: A wealthy tourist is weaker than a local vendor who knows the market. A small town with a clear legal alternative (annexing the factory) outmaneuvered a large corporation with no contingency plan. Power in negotiation is determined by attractiveness of no-deal alternatives, not size or wealth.

## Core Message

The antidote to facing a more powerful party is not matching their power but improving your own walk-away position. Developing a strong BATNA—knowing exactly what you will do if no agreement is reached—is the most effective step you can take. It tells you when to accept a deal and when to walk away, and it shifts the leverage in your favor without requiring you to bluff, threaten, or abandon the principled approach.
