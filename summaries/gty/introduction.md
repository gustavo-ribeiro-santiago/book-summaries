# Introduction — Getting to Yes

## Overview

The introduction establishes the universal reality of negotiation and diagnoses the fundamental problem with the two most common approaches—soft and hard positional bargaining—before proposing a third way: *principled negotiation*, or negotiation on the merits, developed at the Harvard Negotiation Project.

## Key Concepts

- **Negotiation Is Unavoidable**: Every day, everyone negotiates—about salaries, prices, household decisions, legal disputes, and international agreements. Negotiation is simply "back-and-forth communication designed to reach an agreement when you and the other side have some interests that are shared and others that are opposed."
- **The Soft Negotiator**: Prioritizes the relationship, makes concessions readily, seeks harmony—but often ends up exploited and resentful.
- **The Hard Negotiator**: Treats every situation as a contest of will, takes extreme positions, holds out longest—but often damages relationships and produces equally hard resistance.
- **Principled Negotiation — The Third Way**: Neither soft nor hard, but *both*: hard on the merits, soft on the people. Built on four core principles:
  1. **People**: Separate the people from the problem.
  2. **Interests**: Focus on interests, not positions.
  3. **Options**: Invent multiple options looking for mutual gain before deciding.
  4. **Criteria**: Insist that results be based on objective standards.
- **Three Stages**: The method applies throughout analysis (diagnosing the situation), planning (generating ideas and deciding what to do), and discussion (communicating back and forth toward agreement).
- **Universal Applicability**: The method works regardless of the number of parties, issues, power differences, or whether the other side also learns it—in fact, if both sides use it, it becomes *easier*, not harder.

## Core Message

Most negotiations fail not because of bad intentions but because of a flawed framework. Positional bargaining is inherently inefficient, adversarial, and likely to produce outcomes worse than what was possible. Principled negotiation offers a method that is simultaneously fair, effective, and relationship-preserving—a way to get what you are entitled to while remaining decent.
