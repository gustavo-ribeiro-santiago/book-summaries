# Chapter 8: What If They Use Dirty Tricks? — Getting to Yes

## Overview

Some negotiators resort to deception, psychological pressure, and positional pressure tactics to gain an advantage. This chapter explains how to recognize these tactics, neutralize them, and redirect the negotiation back to principled ground—without getting trapped into playing their game.

## Key Concepts

- **Two Common (Bad) Responses**: Most people either tolerate dirty tactics hoping the other side will be appeased (Chamberlain at Munich), or retaliate in kind—both approaches typically make things worse.
- **The Principled Counter**: Tricky tactics are, in effect, one-sided proposals about *negotiating procedure*. Counter them with principled negotiation about the rules of the game: (1) recognize the tactic, (2) raise it explicitly, (3) negotiate over it using the same four principles (separate people/problem, focus interests, invent options, use objective criteria).
- **Three Categories of Dirty Tricks**:
  1. **Deliberate Deception**:
     - *Phony facts*: Don't trust—verify. Make the negotiation proceed independent of trust.
     - *Ambiguous authority*: Before conceding anything, establish whether the other side has authority to make a binding decision. If they suddenly claim they need approval, invoke reciprocity: "Then we'll both treat this as a draft."
     - *Dubious intentions*: Build compliance mechanisms into the agreement itself (e.g., contingent terms that protect you if they don't follow through).
     - Distinction: *not disclosing* your full thinking is legitimate; *actively misrepresenting* facts or intentions is not.
  2. **Psychological Warfare**:
     - *Stressful physical environments*: Hot room, uncomfortable seat, facing the sun—recognize these as deliberate and raise them objectively: "I'm finding the sun in my eyes distracting. Can we move?"
     - *Personal attacks*: Comments on your appearance, making you wait, interrupting, refusing eye contact—recognize the tactic; naming it usually stops it.
     - *Good-guy/bad-guy routine*: One negotiator is tough, the other "reasonable." Treat both the same: ask the good guy the same principled questions you asked the bad guy.
     - *Threats*: Pressure tactics that often produce counter-pressure and escalation. Counter by asking for the reasoning behind the threat, or by issuing *warnings* (what will happen independently of your will) rather than counter-threats.
  3. **Positional Pressure Tactics**:
     - *Refusal to negotiate / extreme demands*: Don't accept their definition of the game; explore interests and standards independently.
     - *Escalating demands*: When they raise demands after seeming close to agreement, note the tactic explicitly and take a break.
     - *Lock-in tactics*: Public commitments designed to make retreat impossible. Ignore or reframe: "I understand you feel strongly; let's see what a fair outcome looks like."
     - *Calculated delay*: Recognize it; create your own objective deadline or develop your BATNA.
     - *Take-it-or-leave-it*: Never accept ultimatums as the only option; ask for the reasoning, propose objective standards, and let the other side know you're treating it as an opening position.
- **Don't Be a Victim**: The goal is not to get angry or to teach them a lesson, but to redirect the process. Attack the tactic, not the person.

## Core Message

Dirty tricks are fundamentally illegitimate because they are one-sided proposals about how negotiation should work. The response is to apply the same principled approach to the *process* that you apply to the *substance*: name the tactic, question its legitimacy, and insist on a fair game. Recognizing and calmly naming a tactic is often enough to neutralize it—and it keeps you from being dragged into a destructive contest of wills.
